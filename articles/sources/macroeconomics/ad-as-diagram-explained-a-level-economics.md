## This is a "how to draw it" guide

You already half-know what aggregate demand is. What loses marks in the exam is the *drawing* — the axes, the labels, the difference between a shift and a movement. This guide is the diagram technique: get it framed correctly once and every macro essay that needs AD-AS gets easier.

The AD-AS diagram is the workhorse of Theme 2. Demand-pull inflation, a fiscal stimulus, an export boom, a recession — they are all the same diagram with a different curve moving. Learn to draw the frame cleanly and you only have to decide *which* curve shifts and *which way*.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Frame the axes first" color="purple" icon="scale"

## Axes, curves, equilibrium — in that order

Start with the axes. The vertical axis is the **general price level** — write that, not "price", because this is the whole economy, not a single market. The horizontal axis is **real national output** (real GDP). Getting these two labels down is often worth the first mark on its own.

Now the curves. **AD slopes down**: a lower price level raises real wealth, lowers interest rates and makes exports more competitive, so real spending rises. **SRAS slopes up**: higher prices give firms an incentive to produce more in the short run. Where they cross is **macroeconomic equilibrium** — drop dashed lines to both axes and read off the equilibrium price level and equilibrium output.

That fully labelled frame — two named axes, two named curves, one equilibrium with co-ordinates — is the baseline the mark scheme checks before it gives you anything for the shift.

## What shifts AD, what shifts AS, and what only moves along

This is where most marks are won or lost. A **movement along** AD happens for one reason only: the general price level changes. Nothing else moves you along the curve.

A **shift of the whole AD curve** happens when a determinant of demand changes — and AD = C + I + G + (X−M). So a rise in consumer confidence, an investment boom, higher government spending, or a weaker pound (raising net exports) all shift AD *right*. Tighter monetary policy, a fiscal squeeze or falling confidence shift it *left*.

The **SRAS** curve shifts when costs of production change: an energy price spike or a wage surge shifts SRAS *left* (output down, prices up — cost-push). Falling input costs or a productivity gain shift it *right*.

The exam discipline: when a determinant changes, shift the whole curve, label the new one AD1 (or SRAS1), mark the new equilibrium, and draw an arrow. Never draw a determinant change as a slide up the old curve.

:::

## Convert the diagram into marks

Two moves lift the diagram from decoration to analysis. First, **refer to your own diagram in the prose**: "as the diagram shows, AD shifts right from AD to AD1, raising the price level from P to P1 and real output from Y to Y1." That sentence is what links the AO3 diagram marks to the written analysis.

Second, **say which region of SRAS you are in**. The same rightward AD shift mostly raises output when there is spare capacity (shallow SRAS) but mostly raises the price level near full capacity (steep SRAS). Naming the region is the difference between a generic answer and a top-band one — and it is the natural springboard into evaluation.

:::
