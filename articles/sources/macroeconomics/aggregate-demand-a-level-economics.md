## AD is total planned UK spending at each price level

Aggregate demand is the **total planned spending** on UK-produced goods and services that households, firms, the government and foreigners would make at each possible price level, over a given period. It's an *ex-ante* concept — what people *plan* to spend — and it's drawn against the price level, not against any individual price.

Four components add up to AD:

- **C — Consumption**: household spending on goods and services. ~60% of UK GDP.
- **I — Investment**: firms' spending on new capital (machinery, buildings, IT) plus housing. ~17%.
- **G — Government spending**: state purchases of goods and services. Note: transfer payments (pensions, benefits) are *not* G — they become C when households spend them. ~20%.
- **X − M — Net exports**: exports minus imports. The UK runs a current-account deficit so this is usually a small negative number, around −2% to −5%.

The shorthand: **AD = C + I + G + (X − M)**. Examiners love a student who quotes the identity, then explains which component the question is asking them to move.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The big picture · why AD slopes down" color="purple" icon="target"

## Three reasons, ranked by exam frequency

The AD curve slopes downward in price-level / real-GDP space for three reasons examiners want you to name in this order:

**1. The real-balances (Pigou) effect.** A lower price level raises the *real* value of cash savings, bank deposits and other fixed-nominal-value assets. Households feel wealthier and spend more. This effect is small in practice but appears first in every textbook.

**2. The interest-rate (Keynes) effect.** A lower price level reduces the nominal money demanded for transactions. With money supply held constant, the equilibrium interest rate falls. Lower rates stimulate consumption (cheaper credit) and investment (lower hurdle rate). This is the biggest contributor in modern textbooks.

**3. The international-trade effect.** A lower UK price level makes UK exports relatively cheaper for foreign buyers and makes imports relatively dearer for UK buyers. Net exports rise. This effect depends on exchange-rate flexibility; a fixed-exchange-rate regime would mute it.

All three operate together; the AD curve summarises their combined effect. None of them is a *shift* — they're movements *along* the curve, triggered by the price level changing.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The friction · movement vs shift" color="pink" icon="scale"

## The one distinction examiners punish you for missing

A **movement along AD** happens when the price level changes. That's it. If the question is "what happens to spending when CPI falls?", you slide down the AD curve.

A **shift of AD** happens when *anything else* changes. Specifically: any non-price-level cause that changes one of C, I, G, or (X − M). Common shift triggers, by component:

- **C shifters**: consumer confidence, household disposable income (tax cuts, wage growth), interest rates set by the MPC, household debt levels, wealth effects from asset prices.
- **I shifters**: business confidence, interest rates (cost of borrowing), profit expectations, technological change, corporation tax.
- **G shifters**: fiscal policy decisions — Budget changes to spending or investment programmes.
- **(X − M) shifters**: exchange rate, world growth, UK competitiveness, protectionist policy abroad, world trade conditions.

The diagnostic for any macro essay: "what changed *first*?" If the answer is "the price level", it's a movement. If the answer is anything else, it's a shift — and the shift causes the price level to adjust as a consequence.

Drawing the wrong move is the most common Level-2 paper-loss in AD/AS essays.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Real world example" color="blue" icon="globe"

## The 2020 UK furlough scheme as a textbook AD-shift

In March 2020 the UK Chancellor introduced the Coronavirus Job Retention Scheme: the state would pay 80% of wages for workers furloughed by Covid lockdowns. By peak in May 2020, 8.9 million jobs were supported at a total cost of around £70bn — roughly 3% of annual GDP.

Read this as an AD intervention. Without furlough, mass layoffs would have collapsed household disposable income and so consumption (C). Furlough preserved consumption directly. The government didn't pump money into the economy in pursuit of higher demand — it transferred income to households to *stop AD collapsing*. The Bank of England's August 2020 *Monetary Policy Report* estimated the policy held UK consumption roughly 5 percentage points higher than it would otherwise have been.

The AD/AS diagram for this episode shifts AD **right** (or, more accurately, prevents a leftward shift) while SRAS shifts **left** (lockdowns took capacity offline). Real GDP still fell — by 9.3% across 2020, the largest annual fall since 1709 — but the price level didn't collapse, because the AD support held demand up against the supply contraction.

The exam move on Covid: don't write "the government spent £70bn so AD rose". Write "the policy prevented an AD collapse that would otherwise have come from a sharp fall in consumption". The distinction is the difference between a Level 3 and a Level 4 paragraph.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## The three-mark moves that lift your essay

For **AO1** (knowledge), always state the identity: AD = C + I + G + (X − M). One mark.

For **AO3** (analysis), name the chain. "The MPC raises base rate → cost of borrowing rises → C and I fall → AD shifts left → real GDP falls, price level falls, unemployment rises." Each linked step is a mark; isolated facts are not.

For **AO4** (evaluation), make the response conditional. "The size of the AD fall depends on the **marginal propensity to consume** out of disposable income — high MPC means a small rate rise has a large AD effect; low MPC (households deleveraging) means the rate hike has little bite." That single conditional sentence is worth more than a paragraph of unhedged counter-points.

The discipline to practise: every time you draw an AD shift, write *one sentence* about what would make that shift smaller or larger. That sentence is your evaluation mark.

:::
