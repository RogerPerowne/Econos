## Two curves answering two questions

Aggregate supply has **two** curves because economists are interested in two different questions about output.

**Short-run aggregate supply (SRAS)** asks: at each price level, how much do firms supply *with input costs held constant*? Wages are sticky in the short run — workers have contracts, firms have wage agreements, prices reset slowly. So a rise in the price level with fixed nominal wages means firms' *real* labour cost falls, profit margins rise, and they expand output. The SRAS curve slopes upward in price-level / real-GDP space.

**Long-run aggregate supply (LRAS)** asks: what's the *sustainable* level of real output the economy can produce when all input markets clear and all resources are fully employed? That depends on the quantity and quality of factors of production: labour, capital, land, enterprise, productivity. LRAS is independent of the current price level — it's a capacity ceiling.

The two curves are about different timescales, and they shift for different reasons. Don't confuse a leftward shift of SRAS (e.g. an oil shock) with a leftward shift of LRAS (e.g. a fall in trend productivity) — they need different policy responses.

## Two LRAS shapes, two policy worlds

The most important disagreement in macroeconomics for A-level purposes is the shape of LRAS. Keynesians and Classicals look at the same data and draw it differently. The exam expects you to know both and use whichever the question calls for.

:::: two-col

::: card variant="mr" title="Keynesian LRAS — the kinked curve"

Three stages. Horizontal when the economy is in deep recession with masses of unemployed resources: AD shifts raise output without raising prices, because firms have spare capacity to absorb the new demand. Upward-sloping as the economy approaches capacity: AD shifts raise both output and prices. Vertical at full employment: AD shifts raise only prices, because there's no more capacity to expand into.

The policy implication: in a recession, demand-side stimulus (lower rates, higher G) raises output for free. Fiscal policy works. Austerity in a slump is self-defeating.

:::

::: econos-diagram svgKey="srasShiftInteractive" state="shift" label="Short-run aggregate supply" caption="Short-run aggregate supply: an SRAS shift (e.g. an input-cost or productivity change) moves the price level and output."
:::

::: card variant="ar" title="Classical LRAS — vertical at Y_FE"

A single vertical line at the full-employment level of real output (Y_FE). The price level can vary, but real GDP in the long run is fixed by supply-side factors — productivity, factor quantities, institutions. Wages and prices adjust quickly enough that the economy snaps back to Y_FE after any demand shock.

The policy implication: demand-side stimulus only raises prices in the long run, not output. Long-run growth comes only from supply-side policy — education, infrastructure, deregulation, R&D, immigration. Fiscal expansion is inflationary, not growth-creating.

:::

::::

The Bank of England's macro modelling actually uses something closer to Keynesian LRAS — they recognise a meaningful output gap that monetary policy can close. The Treasury's long-run productivity work is closer to Classical. The right answer is: it depends on the time horizon and the state of the economy.

## Reading the question to pick the diagram

The mistake students make is to default to whichever LRAS their teacher drilled hardest. The mark scheme expects you to *pick* — and to justify your pick.

**Use Keynesian LRAS when** the question mentions:

- "Spare capacity" / "unemployed resources" / "demand-deficient unemployment"
- A specific recession (2008-09, 2020 lockdown, 1981)
- Short-run policy choices about a slump
- Output gap that's clearly negative

**Use Classical LRAS when** the question mentions:

- "Long-run growth" / "productive potential" / "supply-side policy"
- Full employment as a given
- The inflationary cost of demand-side stimulus
- 10+ year time horizons

**Use both when** the question is about a *transition* — e.g. an economy recovering from recession and approaching full employment. Draw Keynesian for the trough years, Classical for the long-run equilibrium, and walk the AD adjustment from one to the other. That's a Level 4 move.

The wording cue to look for: "in the long run" almost always means draw a vertical LRAS. "In a recession" almost always means draw a Keynesian LRAS or just SRAS.

## The UK's productivity puzzle and LRAS

UK labour productivity (GDP per hour worked) grew at around 2% per year from 1970 to 2007. After the 2008 financial crisis it slowed sharply: barely 0.5% per year from 2008 through 2019, and roughly flat since. By 2024 UK productivity sat around 20% below the pre-2008 trend line.

This is an LRAS story, not an SRAS story. The economy's *capacity* to produce real output per worker grew slower than it used to. Causes are still debated — under-investment in capital, slowed knowledge diffusion, weaker management quality, Brexit-related trade frictions reducing competitive pressure, falling public investment. All of them shift LRAS *left* relative to the pre-crisis trend, or equivalently, slow its rightward drift.

The implication for policy: a demand-side stimulus large enough to push UK output back to the pre-2008 trend line would *not* do so in a Classical model — it would just generate inflation, because productive capacity is genuinely lower now. The Bank of England's persistent worry through the 2010s was that they couldn't tell whether the output gap was 5% or 0%. If it's 5%, run loose monetary policy; if it's 0%, you're priming the pump for inflation. The 2021-23 inflation spike vindicated the worry.

The exam move: when you cite UK productivity, frame it as an *LRAS* shift (or slower drift), not an SRAS one. Confusing those two is the same diagnostic error as confusing AD shifts with movements along AD.

## The structural-vs-cyclical instinct

For AO3 (analysis), every diagram should start by asking: is the change in output cyclical (short-run, SRAS / AD) or structural (long-run, LRAS)? The mark scheme rewards students who write that question explicitly before they draw.

For AO4 (evaluation), the conditional move: "An expansionary AD policy raises real output by ΔY *only if* the economy starts on the horizontal or upward-sloping part of LRAS. If LRAS is vertical at this point, ΔY = 0 and the policy is purely inflationary. Which case applies depends on the size of the output gap, which is contested for the UK in [year]." That single sentence covers both shapes of LRAS and explains why the choice matters.

The Schumpeter / Bank-of-England-style move for top-band: even when you've picked Keynesian, acknowledge that the *long-run* sustainable output is fixed by supply-side factors. Use Keynesian to analyse the short run, then say "in the long run, growth comes from rightward shifts of LRAS, which require supply-side investment in [productivity / skills / capital]."
