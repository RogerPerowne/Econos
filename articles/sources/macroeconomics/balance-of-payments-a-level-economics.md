## Four buckets, one current account

The current account = **trade in goods** + **trade in services** + **primary income** + **secondary income**.

- **Trade in goods.** Physical exports minus physical imports. UK runs a chunky deficit here — manufacturing decline, energy import bills, consumer goods preference for foreign brands.
- **Trade in services.** Financial, legal, consulting, education exports minus imports. UK runs a sizeable surplus — the City, English universities, UK consultancies abroad.
- **Primary income.** Income earned on UK-owned foreign assets (dividends, interest) minus income paid to foreign-owned UK assets. Roughly balanced for the UK; depends heavily on the year's FDI returns.
- **Secondary income.** Transfers — remittances, foreign aid, the old EU budget contribution (pre-Brexit). UK runs a small deficit here.

Total = persistent UK current-account deficit, roughly 2–4% of GDP year on year.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Why deficits matter (sometimes)" color="blue" icon="target"

## When a current-account deficit is fine — and when it isn't

A current-account deficit is mathematically a capital-account surplus: the country is importing more capital than it exports. Equivalently, the country is borrowing from the rest of the world (or selling foreign-owned assets).

Conditions where that's fine:
- Capital is flowing in because the country has strong investment opportunities (FDI into UK financial services, US tech).
- The deficit is being funded by long-term inflows (FDI, gilts to long-horizon central banks) rather than hot money.
- The currency is freely floating and can adjust.

Conditions where it isn't:
- The deficit reflects loss of competitiveness rather than investment opportunity.
- It's funded by short-term inflows that can reverse rapidly (the sterling crisis of 1992, the Asian crisis of 1997).
- The currency is pegged or the central bank's reserves are limited.

The UK has mostly been in the first camp — the deficit reflects capital INFLOWS more than capital flight.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The two policy responses" color="purple" icon="scale"

## Expenditure switching vs expenditure reducing

Two ways to attack a current-account deficit. They have opposite output implications:

- **Expenditure-switching policy** moves demand FROM imports TO domestic substitutes without lowering total demand. The classic tool is **currency devaluation** (or letting the pound fall) — exports get cheaper abroad, imports get dearer at home, the trade balance improves. Whether it works depends on the **Marshall-Lerner condition**: the sum of price elasticities of export demand and import demand must exceed 1. In the short run elasticities are low (contracts already signed, supply chains rigid) so the trade balance worsens BEFORE improving — that's the **J-curve**. After 6–18 months elasticities rise, trade balance improves.
- **Expenditure-reducing policy** lowers aggregate demand directly — tighter monetary or fiscal policy. Households can afford less, including fewer imports. The trade balance improves mechanically. Cost: output and employment fall too. A blunt instrument.

The strongest answers note that policymakers usually do both at once — the IMF's classic structural-adjustment programmes combined devaluation with fiscal tightening.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The Marshall-Lerner condition and the J-curve" color="pink" icon="globe"

## Why a weaker pound doesn't always help immediately

Marshall-Lerner: a devaluation improves the trade balance only if `PED(X) + PED(M) > 1`. If demand for exports and imports is too price-inelastic, the higher import bill (paying more pounds per import) outweighs the export volume gain (slightly more goods sold abroad).

In the SHORT run, elasticities are typically below 1: existing contracts hold quantities fixed, supply chains take time to rewire. So the trade balance worsens first. After 6–18 months, elasticities rise, the volume effect dominates, and the trade balance improves.

The graph: trade balance starts at zero, falls into a J-curve trough, then climbs back through zero and into surplus. That's the canonical exam diagram.

UK 2008–2009: sterling fell ~25% against the dollar after the financial crisis. The UK current account did improve — but with a 12-to-18-month lag and only partially. Marshall-Lerner held; the J-curve played out roughly as predicted.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Exam edge" color="green" icon="cap"

## Three moves that lift the mark

- **Break out the four components.** Don't write "UK has a trade deficit" — write "UK has a goods deficit of ~£200bn, a services surplus of ~£140bn, and a structural primary income roughly balanced, leaving a net current-account deficit of ~2–4% of GDP".
- **Distinguish expenditure-switching from expenditure-reducing.** A devaluation switches; a Bank Rate rise reduces. Both correct deficits; only one preserves output.
- **Name the Marshall-Lerner condition AND the J-curve.** The two go together — Marshall-Lerner is the test, the J-curve is what happens during the lag.

:::
