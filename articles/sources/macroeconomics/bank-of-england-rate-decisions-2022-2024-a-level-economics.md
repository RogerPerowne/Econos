## The fastest tightening in a generation

Between late 2021 and August 2023 the Bank of England's Monetary Policy Committee raised **Bank Rate from a record low of 0.1% to 5.25%** — one of the sharpest tightening cycles in its modern history. The reason was the worst inflation in roughly four decades: UK **CPI peaked at around 11.1% in October 2022**.

What makes the episode the perfect live case study is the timing. The MPC began raising rates in late 2021, yet inflation kept climbing for most of 2022. That is not a failure of the policy — it is the **transmission mechanism working with a lag** of roughly 6 to 18 months, combined with a chunk of inflation coming from an energy shock that interest rates cannot directly touch.

So this is monetary policy in real time: the right lever, pulled hard, working slowly, against a problem only partly within its reach.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The mechanism" color="blue" icon="target"

## The transmission mechanism, in motion

Bank Rate is the rate the Bank of England pays on commercial banks' reserves; banks price their lending relative to it. A rate **rise** works through a five-link chain:

1. **Bank Rate up.** The MPC raises the rate.
2. **Commercial rates follow.** Mortgage, business-loan and savings rates rise.
3. **Borrowing falls, saving rises.** Dearer credit makes households delay big purchases and firms postpone investment.
4. **C and I fall.** Lower consumption and investment drag aggregate demand left.
5. **Inflation eases.** Reduced demand relieves price pressure, pulling CPI back toward 2%.

The chain end-to-end takes roughly 6 to 18 months — which is exactly why CPI kept rising through 2022 even as the MPC tightened. The early hikes had not yet bitten.

There is a second job for the rate rises that the diagram alone misses: **anchoring expectations**. If households and firms expect high inflation to persist, they bid for higher wages and set higher prices, and the inflation becomes self-fulfilling. By raising rates aggressively, the MPC signalled it would not let that happen — defending the credibility of the 2% target even while the energy shock raged.

::: section eyebrow="Real world example" color="purple" icon="globe"

## 2022 to 2024, step by step

The starting point was a Bank Rate of 0.1%, the emergency level set during the pandemic. As inflation surged through 2022 — driven by the post-lockdown demand rebound and, decisively, the energy and food price shock following Russia's invasion of Ukraine — the MPC raised rates meeting after meeting, taking Bank Rate to 5.25% by August 2023 and then holding it there.

The lag is the lesson. Hiking began in late 2021, yet CPI did not peak until around 11.1% in October 2022, nearly a year later. A student writing "the Bank raised rates so inflation fell" cannot explain that delay; a student who names the 6-to-18-month transmission lag can.

The supply-shock mismatch is the second lesson. A large part of the 2022 inflation was **cost-push** — an external spike in gas and food prices. Bank Rate works on **aggregate demand**, so it could do nothing to reverse the supply shock itself. Why raise rates at all, then? To stop the temporary shock becoming permanent: to cool the demand-side component and, above all, to anchor expectations so that one-off price rises did not turn into an embedded wage-price spiral. As the energy shock faded and the tightening fed through, inflation fell back, allowing rates to be cut later in the cycle.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## What the policy's success depends on

**The lag cuts both ways.** Because the full effect arrives 6 to 18 months later, the MPC risks over-tightening — pushing rates higher than needed and only discovering it once the economy has already slowed too much. Forecasting under uncertainty is the core difficulty, and hindsight always looks clearer than the decision did at the time.

**Wrong tool for a supply shock.** Bank Rate addresses demand, not energy prices. Raising it to fight a cost-push shock risks weakening output and employment while doing little to the underlying cause — the classic case for saying monetary policy is a blunt instrument against supply-side inflation.

**Distributional bite.** Higher rates fall hardest on mortgage holders and borrowers, while savers gain. The policy that defends the inflation target also redistributes income — a fairness dimension a pure demand diagram hides.

The evaluation move is to weigh the cost of over-tightening against the cost of letting expectations unanchor. The MPC judged that the greater danger was inflation becoming entrenched — so it accepted the risk of slowing the economy to protect the credibility of the 2% target.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## Name the lag, name the mismatch

For **AO2 application**, anchor the numbers: Bank Rate from 0.1% to 5.25% by August 2023, CPI peaking near 11.1% in October 2022.

For **AO3 analysis**, run the five-link transmission chain and state the 6-to-18-month lag explicitly — it is what explains inflation rising after hikes began. For **AO4 evaluation**, make the supply-shock point conditional: Bank Rate is the right tool for the demand-side and expectations components of the inflation, but a blunt instrument against the energy shock, *because* it acts on aggregate demand and cannot reverse a contraction in supply. Naming both the lag and the mismatch is the top-band move.

:::
