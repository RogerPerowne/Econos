## Two different shocks, not one

"Brexit hurt UK trade" is true but too blunt for the exam. There were really two distinct shocks to the current account, and they work through different mechanisms.

The first was a **price shock**: sterling fell sharply after the June 2016 referendum result. A weaker pound makes UK exports cheaper abroad and imports dearer at home — a relative-price change whose effect on the current account plays out over time through the **J-curve**.

The second was a **structural cost shock**: at the end of the 2020 transition the UK left the single market and customs union, and trade with the EU from 2021 had to clear new **non-tariff barriers** — customs declarations, checks, rules-of-origin paperwork. These raise the *cost* of trading in both directions and dampen trade volumes persistently. That isn't a relative-price story the J-curve captures; it's a permanent increase in trade frictions.

Keep these apart and add the UK's goods-versus-services split, and you can explain why the current account moved the way it did rather than just asserting that it got worse.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The mechanism · the J-curve for the 2016 fall" color="purple" icon="target"

## Why a weaker pound worsens the balance before it helps

Take the 2016 depreciation first, because it's the clean textbook case. When sterling falls, UK exports become cheaper to foreign buyers and imports become more expensive to UK buyers. You'd expect that to improve the current account — eventually. But in the short run it does the opposite.

The reason is **elasticity over time**. Immediately after a depreciation, trade volumes are sticky: contracts are already signed, supply chains can't switch overnight, and habits are slow to change. So volumes barely move while *prices* have changed — and the dominant effect is that the same volume of imports now costs more in pounds. The import bill rises, and the current account worsens.

As time passes, volumes adjust. Foreign buyers respond to cheaper UK exports by buying more; UK buyers respond to dearer imports by buying fewer. Once those volume responses are large enough, the balance improves — tracing the **J-shape**: down first, then up. Whether it ultimately improves at all depends on the **Marshall-Lerner condition**: the combined price elasticities of demand for exports and imports must exceed one. If demand is too inelastic, the depreciation never delivers the improvement.

## The structural shift: friction, goods and the services exception

Now the second shock, which the J-curve doesn't cover. From 2021 UK-EU trade faced non-tariff barriers for the first time in decades. These don't change relative prices in the way a depreciation does; they raise the fixed and variable *costs* of trading — paperwork, delays at the border, compliance with rules-of-origin to qualify for tariff-free treatment. The predictable effect is to reduce trade *volumes* with the EU in both directions, and to do so persistently rather than as a one-off adjustment.

The crucial application point is the **goods-versus-services split**. Brexit friction bears most heavily on physical goods, which must clear customs and meet product rules at the border. The UK's comparative strength, though, is in **services** — financial, legal, professional, education exports — much of which is delivered without crossing a customs post. So the two halves of the trade balance were affected unevenly: the goods channel took more of the friction, while a large part of the services channel was less directly exposed.

That is why the *components* of the current account tell a richer story than the headline figure. The UK entered all this with a long-standing structural deficit — a sizeable goods deficit partly offset by a services surplus, financed by capital inflows under the balance-of-payments identity. Brexit didn't create that structure; it added friction on top of it, concentrated on the goods side.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## Isolating Brexit from everything else is the hard part

A strong answer is honest about attribution.

**Other shocks overlapped.** The pandemic disrupted global trade from 2020, and the 2022 energy price spike hit the UK's import bill hard. Disentangling the Brexit effect on the current account from Covid and the energy shock is genuinely difficult, so confident single-cause claims should be resisted. Say which direction each force pushes and acknowledge they ran together.

**Trade can redirect.** Friction with the EU creates an incentive to trade more with non-EU partners and to substitute domestically. How far that re-routing offsets the lost EU trade depends on whether equally good partners and substitutes exist — which for many integrated supply chains they don't, at least in the short run.

**The current account is only half the identity.** A deficit is matched by a financial-account surplus. Whether a wider or more friction-bound trade position is a problem depends on the UK's continued ability to attract capital inflows. A deficit financed by stable investment is more comfortable than one financed by volatile hot money.

The evaluative move: separate the price channel (2016 depreciation, J-curve) from the structural channel (post-2020 friction), concede the difficulty of isolating Brexit from Covid and energy, and judge sustainability through the financing side, not the trade balance alone.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## Two channels, the components, the identity

For **AO3 analysis**, separate the mechanisms cleanly: the 2016 depreciation works through relative prices and the J-curve (short-run worsening, improvement subject to Marshall-Lerner), while post-2020 non-tariff barriers work as a structural rise in trade costs that dampens volumes. Then apply the goods-versus-services split to explain why the components diverged.

For **AO4 evaluation**, flag the attribution problem (Covid and the energy shock overlapped), note the scope for trade re-routing, and judge sustainability through the financial account, not the headline deficit. The mark is in distinguishing the two channels and reading the components, not in asserting Brexit simply "worsened the current account".

:::
