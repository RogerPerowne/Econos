## A chain of analysis is the core AO3 skill

Open any economics mark scheme and you will find the phrase "a logical chain of reasoning" or "developed analysis". That is what AO3 — analysis — is testing, and it is the engine of every long-answer question. Students who can build a clean chain reach the upper levels almost regardless of topic; students who cannot stay capped however much content they know.

A chain of analysis links a *cause* to a final *outcome* by spelling out the steps in between. The skill is not knowing the endpoint — it is making the journey visible. This guide is how to build one without skipping steps.

::: section eyebrow="Why the links matter" color="purple" icon="bulb"

## Marks live in the links, not the destination

Here is the rule that fixes most lost analysis marks: the mark scheme credits the *developed links*, not the conclusion.

Take "a weaker pound boosts growth." It is perfectly true. It is also worth almost nothing for AO3, because it collapses five separate steps into a single assertion. There is nothing for the examiner to tick except the endpoint.

Now unpack it. A weaker pound → UK exports become cheaper abroad and imports become dearer at home → net exports (X-M) rise → aggregate demand shifts right → assuming spare capacity, real output rises and unemployment falls. The same point now contains four or five creditable links instead of one. Identical economics, very different mark.

The test for any sentence in your answer: could a reader ask "but why does that follow?" between this step and the next? If so, there is a missing link, and a missing link is a missed mark.

:::

::: section eyebrow="How to build one" color="blue" icon="spark"

## Plan in arrows, write in prose

Build the chain in two stages. First, plan it in arrow shorthand so you can see every step. Then translate it into connected prose for the answer, because examiners read sentences, not diagrams.

Start with the trigger from the question. Ask "and then what?" at every step until you arrive at a macro or micro outcome — a change in output, employment, the price level, welfare or the balance of payments.

Worked example, a cut in income tax: lower income tax → households keep more of each pound earned → disposable income rises → consumption rises (the largest component of AD) → aggregate demand shifts right → with spare capacity, real output and employment rise → as the economy nears full capacity, further rises feed into the price level instead.

Then write it as prose with logical connectives doing the linking work: "Because households keep more of each pound, disposable income rises, which raises consumption — the largest component of aggregate demand — so AD shifts right..." The connectives ("because", "which means", "so", "as a result") are how you signal the chain to the marker.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Common breaks" color="pink" icon="scale"

## Where chains snap

Three failures break a chain.

**The jump.** Cause straight to conclusion with the mechanism missing — "lower rates boost growth." Insert the steps.

**The dead end.** A chain that stops at a half-outcome — "consumption rises" — without carrying through to the macro objective the question is about. Always finish on output, employment, the price level or the balance of payments.

**The unsupported assumption mid-chain.** A link that only holds under a condition you never state — "AD shifts right *so* output rises" quietly assumes spare capacity. Either state the assumption ("with spare capacity, output rises") or flag it for your evaluation. Naming where the chain depends on a condition is also where your AO3 hands neatly to your AO4.

A diagram can carry part of the chain, but only if you refer to it: "as shown, AD shifts from AD to AD1 and output rises from Y to Y1." A diagram nobody points to earns nothing for analysis.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="green" icon="cap"

## The chain habits to drill

Make every step visible. After each sentence, ask "and then what?" and "but why does that follow?" — if either question has an unwritten answer, add the link.

Always land the chain on a macro or micro outcome the question cares about, not on a halfway point. Use connectives — because, so, which means, as a result — to signal the logic, and refer to any diagram inside the chain so it earns AO3 rather than decorating the page.

Plan in arrows, write in prose, and remember the marks are in the links. A short answer with four clean links beats a long answer with one big jump.

:::
