## Same number, two completely different stories

Inflation is a sustained rise in the general price level, measured in the UK by the Consumer Prices Index. But a single CPI figure — say 6% — tells you nothing about *why*. It could be an economy running hot, or it could be an economy being squeezed by rising costs. Those are opposite problems, and they need opposite handling.

A-level reduces the causes to two you must be able to tell apart on a diagram: **demand-pull** and **cost-push**. Get the diagnosis right and the analysis and the policy fall out naturally. Get it wrong and you prescribe the wrong medicine — which is exactly what mark schemes are watching for.

The whole skill is reading the AD-AS diagram. Here is the difference.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The big picture · two diagrams" color="purple" icon="target"

## Demand-pull pushes AD right; cost-push pushes SRAS left

**Demand-pull inflation** happens when aggregate demand outpaces aggregate supply — too much spending chasing too few goods. On the diagram, AD shifts right along the SRAS curve. Both the price level *and* real output rise. The symptom is an economy running hot: low unemployment, high capacity utilisation, confidence strong. The chain runs: rising consumption and investment → AD shifts right → firms near capacity raise prices → the price level rises with output.

**Cost-push inflation** happens when aggregate supply contracts — the cost of producing each unit goes up regardless of demand. On the diagram, SRAS shifts left. The price level rises but real output *falls*. That combination — rising prices *and* falling output — has a name: **stagflation**, the nastiest case for policymakers. The chain runs: an input cost rises (energy, wages, imported materials) → unit costs climb → SRAS shifts left → output falls and prices rise together.

The diagnostic that ties it together is the **direction of output**. Rising output with rising prices means demand-pull. Falling output with rising prices means cost-push. That single observation tells you which diagram to draw and which policy to recommend — and it is the move examiners reward.

::: section eyebrow="Real world example" color="blue" icon="globe"

## The 2021 rebound and the 2022 energy shock

The UK gives a clean back-to-back illustration. The **2021 post-lockdown rebound** was textbook demand-pull. Households had built up savings and pent-up demand during lockdowns, and as the economy reopened that spending hit supply chains that had not yet healed. Demand ran ahead of supply, output recovered fast and prices started climbing — AD shifting right.

Then in **2022 came the cost-push shock**. Russia's invasion of Ukraine sent wholesale gas prices soaring and disrupted global food and fertiliser markets. UK firms faced sharply higher input costs across the board. That is a leftward shift of SRAS: prices rose hard while growth weakened — the stagflation signature. UK CPI peaked at 11.1% in October 2022, the highest in four decades.

The Bank of England's response — raising the base rate from a record low of 0.1% to 5.25% over the following two years — is revealing. A rate rise works on *demand*. It could lean against the demand-pull component, but it could do almost nothing about the supply shock itself: higher rates do not make more gas appear. The Bank tightened anyway, mainly to stop high inflation becoming entrenched in expectations and wage-setting — a defensible but blunt move against a cause that monetary policy cannot directly fix.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## The cause determines the cure — and the cost

The standard answer recommends higher interest rates for inflation, full stop. The top band makes the response *cause-specific*.

**For demand-pull, monetary tightening fits.** The problem is excess demand; raising the base rate raises borrowing costs, slows consumption and investment, and shifts AD back. The instrument addresses the right side of the diagram, and the main cost is a slowdown the policy is deliberately engineering.

**For cost-push, monetary tightening is a blunt instrument.** The problem is a leftward SRAS shift the central bank cannot reverse. Raising rates cools demand, but the supply shock remains, so the bank risks pushing output further below trend — a deeper downturn — while inflation stays high because its source is untouched. You can end up with weaker growth *and* persistent inflation.

The genuine "however" is that the two causes rarely arrive cleanly separated, and expectations blur the choice. Even a pure cost-push shock can force a central bank to tighten — not to fix the supply side, which it cannot, but to stop temporary high inflation feeding into wage demands and becoming self-sustaining. So the evaluation is not "demand-pull needs rates and cost-push does not"; it is that against cost-push, tightening trades real output for the *credibility* of the inflation target. Whether that trade is worth it depends on how anchored expectations already are.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## The two-mark moves that lift your essay

For **AO3 analysis**, name the chain and tie it to the right shift. "Demand-pull → AD shifts right → output and prices both rise" versus "cost-push → SRAS shifts left → output falls while prices rise." A bare claim that "inflation rose" earns little; the linked steps anchored to a labelled diagram earn the analysis marks.

For **AO4 evaluation**, make the policy point conditional on the cause. "Raising the base rate is the right response to demand-pull inflation but a blunt instrument against cost-push, *because* it works on aggregate demand and cannot reverse a supply shock — so it risks deepening the output fall without bringing prices back to target." That single sentence is worth more than a list of generic pros and cons.

The habit to drill: diagnose the cause from the evidence *before* you recommend a policy. The diagnosis is the evaluation.

:::
