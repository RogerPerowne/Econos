## "Inflation fell" does not mean prices fell

Here is the sentence that costs marks in data-response: *"UK inflation fell from 11.1% in October 2022 to around 4% in late 2023."* A surprising number of students read that and write that prices fell, that the cost of living went down. It didn't. Prices kept rising the whole time — just more slowly.

The three terms describe three different things, and the trick is that two of them are about the **price level** and one is about the **rate of change** of the price level:

- **Inflation** — the price level is rising.
- **Deflation** — the price level is falling.
- **Disinflation** — the price level is still rising, but the *rate* is slowing.

Only deflation means prices are actually falling. Inflation slowing is disinflation, and confusing the two reverses what the data is telling you.

## Level versus rate of change

Think of the price level (the CPI) as a position, and the inflation rate as a speed.

**Inflation** is a sustained rise in the general price level — the CPI is climbing, so the inflation rate is **positive**. CPI +4% means the average basket of goods costs 4% more than a year ago.

**Deflation** is a sustained fall in the general price level — the CPI is dropping, so the inflation rate is **negative**. CPI −1% means the basket is 1% *cheaper* than a year ago. This is rare in modern Britain; Japan is the textbook case, with bouts of mild deflation across the 1990s and 2000s.

**Disinflation** is a fall in the *rate* of inflation while it remains positive. The CPI is still rising, but more slowly: 11% one year, 4% the next. Prices have not fallen — their *speed of increase* has. The UK's path through 2023 was disinflation, the direct result of the Bank of England raising base rate from 0.1% to 5.25% to slow demand.

The clean test: look at the sign of the inflation *rate*. Positive and rising → inflation accelerating. Positive but falling → disinflation. Negative → deflation.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Why the distinction carries weight" color="blue" icon="scale"

## Disinflation is the goal; deflation is the danger

Central banks actively *want* disinflation when inflation is above target — it is what success looks like when they are bringing 11% back towards 2%. They emphatically do **not** want deflation.

That asymmetry surprises students, because cheaper goods sound like a win for consumers. But sustained deflation can be corrosive:

- **Deferred spending** — if households expect prices to keep falling, they delay purchases, weakening aggregate demand and pushing prices down further. A self-reinforcing **deflationary spiral**.
- **Rising real debt** — falling prices raise the *real* value of fixed nominal debts. Borrowers (households, firms, the government) find their debts harder to service even as incomes stagnate. Irving Fisher called this **debt deflation**.
- **Sticky wages** — firms struggle to cut nominal wages, so deflation squeezes margins and can force layoffs rather than pay cuts.
- **Policy paralysis** — with nominal interest rates already near zero (the zero lower bound), the central bank has little room to cut further to fight it.

That is why the Bank of England's symmetric 2% target sits comfortably *above* zero: a small inflation buffer keeps the economy clear of the deflation trap, while leaving real wage adjustment and monetary policy room to work.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## Read the rate, then say what it means

In data-response, the marks start with reading the figure correctly. State whether the inflation *rate* is positive and rising (accelerating inflation), positive and falling (disinflation, prices still rising), or negative (deflation, prices falling). Getting that sign right is AO2 application and it gates everything after it.

The evaluation move that lifts the answer: explain *why* the distinction matters for policy. "The fall in CPI from 11% to 4% is disinflation, not deflation — prices are still rising, so the cost-of-living squeeze continues even as the headline rate improves." Naming that disinflation is the Bank's intended outcome, while deflation would be a policy failure to avoid, shows you understand the target is a rate of change, not a price.

:::
