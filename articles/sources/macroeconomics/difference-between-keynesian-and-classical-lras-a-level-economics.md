::: section eyebrow="The confusion" color="green" icon="bulb"

## Two shapes, two arguments

Both views agree on one thing: in the long run, output is constrained by the economy's productive capacity. They disagree about how *fast* the economy gets back to that capacity if something knocks it off — and they draw that disagreement as two different curves.

The **Classical LRAS** is a single **vertical line** at the full-employment level of output (often labelled Yfe or Yf). The **Keynesian LRAS** is a **reverse-L** — flat at low output, curving upward as the economy approaches capacity, then vertical at full employment.

The shape is not a stylistic choice. It encodes whether the economy can sit below capacity for a meaningful length of time. And that single question decides whether a rise in aggregate demand raises real output (good news, jobs) or merely raises the price level (inflation, no real gain). Choosing which curve to draw is choosing a side in the central macro policy debate.

## A vertical line — markets clear, output returns to capacity

The Classical position assumes **flexible wages and prices**. If demand falls and unemployment rises, wages fall, costs fall, and firms rehire until the economy returns to full employment. The labour market clears; involuntary unemployment is at most temporary. So in the long run output is pinned at Yfe regardless of the price level — hence a **vertical** LRAS.

The policy implication is stark. Because output is fixed at capacity in the long run, a rightward shift in AD (from a fiscal or monetary stimulus) cannot raise real output — it only bids up the price level. Demand management is therefore inflationary and, beyond smoothing very short-run wobbles, pointless. The only way to raise output sustainably is to shift LRAS right with **supply-side policy**: investment, skills, competition, incentives. This is the intellectual home of monetarism and the supply-side reforms of the 1980s.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The Keynesian view" color="blue" icon="globe"

## A reverse-L — an economy can get stuck below capacity

Keynes's objection was empirical. In a deep slump, wages do not fall fast enough to restore full employment — they are **sticky downward** (workers resist nominal pay cuts, contracts are fixed, firms fear damaging morale). So an economy can settle at an **underemployment equilibrium** and stay there: idle factories, unemployed workers, output well below capacity, with no automatic force pulling it back.

That gives the curve three segments:

- **Horizontal (deep recession)** — huge spare capacity. Firms can hire idle workers and run idle plant without bidding up costs, so higher AD raises real output with negligible inflation.
- **Upward-sloping (approaching capacity)** — spare resources are being used up; bottlenecks emerge, so higher AD raises both output and prices.
- **Vertical (full employment)** — capacity reached; from here, higher AD raises only the price level, exactly as in the Classical model.

The policy implication: in a slump, on the flat segment, demand-side stimulus is powerful and cheap in inflation terms — the case for the fiscal expansion Keynes urged in the 1930s and that governments reached for again in 2008–09 and 2020.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## Let the position on the curve drive the conclusion

The marks are in connecting the *shape* to the *effect of a policy*. State the assumption that distinguishes the schools — flexible wages and self-correcting markets (Classical) versus sticky wages and a possible underemployment equilibrium (Keynesian) — then read the consequence off the diagram.

The highest-band move is conditional, not tribal: "If the economy has significant spare capacity (the flat Keynesian segment), a demand stimulus raises output and employment with little inflation; if it is at or near full capacity (the vertical segment, where the Classical view always sits), the same stimulus raises only the price level." That sentence shows you can evaluate the policy by reference to where the economy actually is on the curve — which is exactly what AO4 rewards, rather than asserting one school is simply right.

:::
