## Two curves, not one curve at two speeds

The most common aggregate-supply mistake is to treat SRAS and LRAS as the same idea looked at over a longer or shorter window. They are answering two different questions.

**SRAS** answers: *at the current capacity, how expensive is it to produce output right now?* It is about **costs**.

**LRAS** answers: *how much can this economy produce in total when its factors of production are fully and efficiently used?* It is about **capacity**.

Because they answer different questions, different things shift them. A spike in gas prices makes today's output dearer to produce — that moves **SRAS**. A new generation of skilled workers, a motorway, a wave of business investment makes the economy capable of producing *more* in total — that moves **LRAS**. Put a cost shock on the LRAS curve, or a supply-side reform on the SRAS curve, and the rest of the analysis collapses.

## Short-run aggregate supply slopes up because costs are sticky

SRAS slopes upward: in the short run, a higher general price level lets firms expand output profitably because some input costs — wages locked into contracts, rents, materials bought at agreed prices — are slow to adjust. With output prices rising faster than these sticky costs, margins widen and firms produce more. That upward slope is the short-run relationship between the price level and output.

What **shifts** the whole SRAS curve is a change in the cost of production that has nothing to do with the price level:

- **Energy and raw materials** — the 2022 surge in wholesale gas prices shifted UK SRAS left.
- **Wages** — a national pay round or a large minimum-wage rise raises labour costs across the economy.
- **The exchange rate** — a weaker pound raises the cost of imported inputs, shifting SRAS left.
- **Business taxes and subsidies** — higher employer National Insurance shifts SRAS left; a production subsidy shifts it right.

Higher costs shift SRAS left (the cost-push picture: prices up, output down). Lower costs shift it right.

:::

::: section eyebrow="LRAS — the capacity curve" color="blue" icon="globe"

## Long-run aggregate supply is about productive potential

LRAS represents the economy's **productive potential** — the maximum sustainable output when factors of production are fully employed. It does not depend on the price level, which is why it is drawn vertical on the Classical view (and why a rightward LRAS shift is the only way to raise real output without eventually just raising prices).

What shifts LRAS is anything that changes the **quantity or quality of the factors of production**:

- **Investment** in new capital deepens the capital stock.
- **Education, training and skills** raise the productivity of labour.
- **Technology and innovation** raise output per unit of input.
- **Net migration and a rising participation rate** add to the labour supply.
- **Infrastructure** (HS2, port capacity, broadband) and **competition or deregulation reforms** raise efficiency.

These are exactly the targets of **supply-side policy**. The point of cutting income-tax rates to sharpen work incentives, or funding apprenticeships, is to shift LRAS right — to make the economy capable of producing more, not merely to make today's output cheaper.

The clean diagnostic: if the change alters how *expensive* current output is, it is SRAS. If it alters how *much* the economy could produce at full employment, it is LRAS.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## Put the shock on the right curve, then say what's permanent

For AO2 application, the marks turn on the diagnosis: name the shock and assign it to the correct curve. "Higher oil prices raise production costs, shifting SRAS left" is right; "higher oil prices shift LRAS left" is the error that voids the analysis.

For AO3/AO4, draw out the difference in permanence. A leftward SRAS shift raises the price level and can cut output in the short run, but leaves productive potential intact — so the effect can fade as costs adjust. A leftward LRAS shift (capital scrapped in a deep recession, skills eroded by long-term unemployment — hysteresis) lowers the output achievable even at full employment, a permanent loss. Saying *which* curve moved is therefore also saying whether the damage is temporary or lasting — and that is the evaluation.

:::
