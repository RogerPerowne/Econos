## Growth is a real-output story, not a money story

Economic growth is a sustained increase in **real GDP** — the total volume of goods and services an economy produces over a year, with the effect of rising prices stripped out. The "real" matters: if prices rise 5% and money output rises 5%, nothing has actually been produced. Nominal figures flatter; real figures tell the truth.

The Office for National Statistics measures it as the year-on-year change in real GDP, and the long-run UK average has been roughly 2% a year — interrupted hard by the 2008 financial crisis and again by the 2020 COVID contraction, the deepest annual fall in modern UK records.

But "growth" hides a distinction examiners insist on, and it is the spine of the whole topic.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The big picture · two kinds of growth" color="purple" icon="target"

## Actual growth and potential growth are different things

**Actual growth** is the real increase in output the economy genuinely achieves in a year. It depends on aggregate demand and on how much **spare capacity** exists. If factories are idle and workers unemployed, a rise in demand can lift output with no new investment at all. On the production possibility frontier, this is a *move towards* the curve — using resources you already had.

**Potential growth** is the increase in the economy's maximum possible output — its productive *capacity*. This comes from more or better factors of production: a larger or more skilled workforce, more capital, new technology, better institutions. On the PPF, this is an *outward shift* of the curve itself.

The exam tell is the diagram. A move towards the frontier means you were producing below capacity and have now used some of the slack — that is actual growth and it can run out. A shift of the frontier means the ceiling itself has risen — that is potential growth and it is what sustains living standards over decades.

The drivers of potential growth are worth naming as a chain: investment in capital → higher labour productivity → more output per worker → the PPF shifts outward → sustainable rises in real income.

## The 2020 contraction and the recovery that followed

The clearest recent illustration of the actual-versus-potential split is the COVID shock. In 2020 UK real GDP fell sharply — the largest single-year contraction in modern records — as lockdowns shut down whole sectors. That was not a fall in the economy's *capacity*: the factories, workers and skills still existed. It was a collapse in *actual* output as demand and the ability to trade vanished.

That distinction matters for diagnosis. Because capacity was intact, much of the rebound in 2021 was actual growth — reopening the economy and putting idle resources back to work, a move back towards an unchanged frontier rather than a shift of it. Recovery from a demand-driven slump can be fast precisely because no new capacity has to be built.

Contrast that with the slower story of the 2008 financial crisis, where a decade of weak business investment and sluggish productivity growth meant the *potential* path itself appeared to flatten. When the frontier stops shifting outward, no amount of demand management restores the lost trend — you have to rebuild capacity through investment, skills and innovation, which takes years, not quarters.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## GDP is a scorecard with blind spots

The standard answer treats more GDP as straightforwardly better. The top band qualifies that, because GDP measures the *size* of output and almost nothing about its *quality* or *distribution*.

**It ignores distribution.** GDP is a national total. It can rise while the typical household sees nothing, if the gains concentrate at the top. Real GDP *per capita* corrects for population but still says nothing about who within the population benefits.

**It ignores environmental cost.** Output produced by burning through finite resources or polluting the air counts as a plus in GDP, while the depletion and damage are invisible. This is the core of the sustainable-growth critique: growth that erodes the natural capital it depends on is not growth that can continue.

**It ignores unpaid and informal work.** Caring, housework and volunteering produce real value but never enter GDP, so an economy can "grow" simply by shifting activity from the home into the market.

The evaluation move is not to say GDP is useless — it tracks material capacity well and correlates with health, education and life expectancy across countries. It is to say what its silence costs: a country can post solid GDP growth while inequality widens, the environment degrades and wellbeing stalls. That is why economists pair real GDP per capita with distribution data and broader wellbeing measures rather than reading the headline number alone.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## The two-mark moves that lift your essay

For **AO3 analysis**, anchor the type of growth to the diagram. "Higher business investment → rising capital stock and productivity → the PPF shifts outward → potential output rises → sustainable growth in real incomes." Naming the linked steps and tying them to a frontier *shift* (not a move along it) is what separates capacity-building growth from a short-run demand bump.

For **AO4 evaluation**, make the welfare point specific. "A rise in real GDP need not raise living standards *because* the gains may be unequally distributed, may come at an environmental cost not captured in the figure, or may simply reflect a larger population — which is why real GDP per capita and distribution data are needed alongside it." That conditional sentence outscores a paragraph of vague "it depends".

The habit to drill: state which type of growth you mean before you analyse it, and check whether the question is about output or about welfare. They are not the same thing, and the marks live in the gap.

:::
