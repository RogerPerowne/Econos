## Fiscal policy is two levers and a multiplier

Fiscal policy is the government's use of **spending (G)** and **taxation (T)** to influence aggregate demand. Two flavours, two directions:

- **Expansionary** (G up, T down) shifts AD right. Used in recessions and weak growth.
- **Contractionary** (G down, T up) shifts AD left. Used when the economy overheats or the deficit needs trimming.

And two delivery mechanisms:

- **Discretionary** — deliberate, legislated changes. Furlough scheme. Infrastructure programmes. Tax-bracket reforms.
- **Automatic stabilisers** — built into the system. Tax revenue falls in a recession because incomes fall; benefit spending rises because unemployment rises. No legislation required.

The mark scheme rewards naming both flavours AND both delivery mechanisms.

## £1 of spending, £X of GDP — and why X matters

The fiscal multiplier is the ratio of the change in GDP to the change in fiscal stimulus. A £20bn programme with a multiplier of 1.5 raises GDP by £30bn over the full cycle.

Multipliers depend on **leakage**. Income earned from government spending gets:
- Spent (raises future demand — feeds the multiplier)
- Saved (leaks out)
- Taxed (leaks out)
- Spent on imports (leaks out)

The formula: `k = 1 / (1 − MPC)` if you stick with the consumption framing, or `k = 1 / MPW` (where MPW = MPS + MPT + MPM) for the full open-economy version.

A small open economy with high marginal propensity to import has a low multiplier — that's the UK case. The IMF estimates UK fiscal multipliers between 0.5 (transfer payments, where most of the cash is saved or repays debt) and 1.5 (well-targeted capital projects with strong local supply chains).

The mark scheme rewards candidates who name the multiplier AND specify why it might be low or high in the case being analysed.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Automatic stabilisers — the silent lever" color="purple" icon="scale"

## What they are, why they matter

The cycle's volatility is dampened by features baked into the tax and benefit system. When recession hits:

- **Tax revenue falls** automatically. Income tax + VAT + corporation tax all fall with weaker incomes / consumption / profits. No legislation; the existing rates produce less revenue at lower nominal activity.
- **Benefit spending rises** automatically. Universal Credit + Jobseeker's Allowance roll out for the newly unemployed.
- Together these widen the deficit, supporting AD — without anyone in Parliament voting.

In booms the reverse: tax revenue rises, benefits fall, deficit narrows. Automatic restraint.

UK automatic stabilisers cushion ~30–40% of a typical demand shock. That's why most macro stabilisation in normal cycles is automatic, not Budget-driven.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The 2020 furlough scheme — the textbook case" color="pink" icon="globe"

## The cleanest discretionary stimulus of the last decade

When the UK locked down in March 2020, the Coronavirus Job Retention Scheme paid 80% of furloughed workers' wages directly to employers. At its peak, 8.9 million jobs were supported. Total cost: about £70bn.

What makes it textbook:
- **Massive, deliberate, time-limited.** Classic discretionary fiscal expansion.
- **Targeted at preserving employment** — kept the labour-market machinery alive so the recovery wasn't a structural-unemployment crisis on top of a demand crisis.
- **Funded by gilts** — UK debt-to-GDP rose from ~85% pre-pandemic to ~100% by end-2020.
- **Crowding out muted** by simultaneous QE — the Bank of England held gilts down so the deficit didn't push rates up.

A-level mark schemes specifically reward this case study because it covers every fiscal-policy concept in one example: discretionary, expansionary, multiplier, crowding out (or lack of), debt sustainability.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The limits — when fiscal policy doesn't work" color="green" icon="check"

## Five things that go wrong

The AO4 marks in a fiscal-policy essay come from naming the constraints.

- **Crowding out.** Government borrowing pushes up interest rates, dampening private investment. Empirically strongest at full employment, weakest in recessions with spare capacity.
- **Time lags.** Legislation, implementation, multiplier effects — the full cycle takes 12–36 months. Fiscal policy designed for last quarter's data can land in next year's economy.
- **Crowding in (the opposite).** Good public-investment programmes can ATTRACT private investment by improving infrastructure, skills, or confidence. Crossrail. Apprenticeships funding.
- **Ricardian equivalence.** The theoretical worry that households save the tax-cut windfall to pay for the future tax rise that funds the deficit. Empirically partial — saves blunt the multiplier without eliminating it.
- **Debt sustainability.** If debt rises faster than GDP, interest costs eat the budget. UK debt-service costs hit ~10% of total government spending in 2023.

The strongest essay names crowding out AND time lags AND debt sustainability — then evaluates which constraint dominates in the specific case being analysed.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Exam edge" color="green" icon="cap"

## What the mark scheme actually rewards

Three moves that lift a fiscal-policy answer:

- **Name the two distinctions.** Expansionary vs contractionary, discretionary vs automatic. Pick one of each and apply it.
- **Specify the multiplier.** Don't say "the multiplier means more impact". Say "a fiscal multiplier of about 1 for transfer payments, closer to 1.5 for targeted capital spending — the UK marginal propensity to import keeps it low".
- **Use 2020 furlough as the case study.** It's the cleanest recent example and covers every concept the mark scheme tests.

:::
