## GDP is one number with four hidden distinctions

GDP is the **total monetary value of final goods and services produced inside a country** in a set period. Sounds simple. The reason students lose marks is they treat it as one number when the mark scheme expects four distinctions to be live in every sentence:

1. **Real vs nominal** — nominal is at current prices, real strips inflation out using a deflator. For growth, always real.
2. **Total vs per-capita** — total GDP shows economic size; per-capita divides by population and is a closer welfare proxy. China's total GDP dwarfs Switzerland's; Switzerland's per-capita figure dwarfs China's.
3. **GDP vs GNI** — GDP measures output inside the country; GNI = GDP + net income from abroad. Matters most for economies with large multinational footprints (Ireland's notorious example).
4. **Value vs volume** — value is in money terms; volume tracks the physical quantity. Real GDP is essentially a volume measure.

That four-way distinction is what separates a Level 2 GDP paragraph from a Level 4.

## Output, income, expenditure — same total, three angles

National income accounting says GDP can be measured three ways and the three must agree:

- **Output approach.** Sum the value added at every stage of production (final output minus intermediate inputs). Avoids double-counting.
- **Income approach.** Sum wages + profits + rents + interest. Logic: every pound of output becomes someone's income.
- **Expenditure approach.** C + I + G + (X − M). The Y13 favourite. Every pound of output is spent by households, firms, government, or foreigners.

In practice the three diverge slightly due to measurement timing and the statistical discrepancy; the ONS publishes all three and reconciles them.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Real vs nominal in practice" color="purple" icon="scale"

## Why the headline GDP figure can mislead

Suppose UK nominal GDP rises 5% over a year. Has the economy grown 5%? Only if prices were flat. With inflation at 3%, real growth is roughly 2%.

The ONS chained-volume measure pins the value of output at the prices of a reference year and updates the weights annually. That's what "real GDP" means in published series — and it's what every A-level Paper 2 calculation expects.

Quick conversion: real GDP = nominal GDP × (base-year price index ÷ current-year price index).

:::

::: section eyebrow="Total vs per-capita — the welfare angle" color="pink" icon="globe"

## Per capita is the welfare workhorse

Total GDP shows economic size; per-capita divides by population. The two can move in opposite directions: a country with strong total GDP growth but faster population growth has falling per-capita GDP, which is what matters for the average household's living standards.

Cross-country comparison adds a second layer: a US dollar of GDP buys very different baskets in different countries. **Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)** adjustment fixes that. PPP-GDP per capita is the mark-scheme-approved metric for comparing living standards internationally.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What GDP misses" color="green" icon="check"

## The five limitations every A* essay closes on

The AO4 marks in any GDP question come from naming the things GDP cannot see:

1. **The informal economy.** Household production, childcare, voluntary work, the black economy — none of it shows up.
2. **Income distribution.** Average per-capita GDP can rise while the median household stagnates. Gini coefficient is the companion stat.
3. **Environmental cost.** GDP counts the output of resource extraction as positive; it counts none of the depletion or pollution as negative.
4. **Negative-good production.** Repairing flood damage, treating illness from pollution, cleaning oil spills — all GDP-positive, all welfare-negative.
5. **Subjective wellbeing.** The ONS wellbeing dashboard (life satisfaction, happiness, anxiety, sense of worthwhileness) tracks separately because GDP can't proxy it.

The Human Development Index combines per-capita GNI (PPP), education and life expectancy and is the cleanest single welfare metric A-level expects you to name.

:::

The way we measure the economy shapes the way we think about what economic policy should accomplish. GDP became the dominant metric not because it is the best measure of welfare, but because it was the measure that existed.

::: section eyebrow="Exam edge" color="green" icon="cap"

## What the mark scheme actually rewards

Three moves Year 13s consistently miss:

- **Specify real when it matters.** Whenever you write "GDP rose by X%", say "real GDP". The examiner reads carelessness into the omission.
- **Cite the ONS series, not just "the data".** "ONS quarterly real GDP estimate for Q2 2024" reads like an economist; "the data" reads like a beginner.
- **Close with limitations.** Even a 9-mark question rewards a sentence on what GDP misses — household production, distribution, environment — and a named alternative (HDI, ONS wellbeing).

:::
