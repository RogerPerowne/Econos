## A 25-marker is a structure, not a topic

The 25-mark question is where the highest marks in the paper live, and it is the question students most often get wrong for reasons that have nothing to do with economics. They know the content. What they lose is the *shape* of the answer.

Edexcel A marks the 25-marker against four assessment objectives. **AO1** is knowledge — defining terms. **AO2** is application — using the context in the question. **AO3** is analysis — building a logical chain. **AO4** is evaluation — weighing the argument. The mark scheme bundles AO1-AO3 into the **KAA** strand and keeps **EV** (AO4) as the other strand. The marks split between the two, so an answer that nails KAA and ignores EV cannot reach the top level, and vice versa.

Get the structure right once and every 25-marker becomes the same shape with different content poured in.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The plan" color="purple" icon="bulb"

## Five minutes of planning saves the whole answer

Before you write a word, plan. A 25-marker is worth a quick spider or a two-column list: KAA chains on the left, the EV counter for each on the right.

Pick **two or three arguments** you can develop deeply — not six you can only assert. For each one, jot the chain in arrow shorthand: cause → mechanism → effect. Then jot the evaluation that sits against it: is the effect large or small? Fast or lagged? What does it assume?

Decide your judgement *before* you start writing the body. Knowing where you are heading stops the essay drifting and makes the conclusion feel earned rather than tacked on. The plan also guarantees you allocate time to EV — the strand students run out of time for is almost always the second one.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The KAA paragraph" color="blue" icon="spark"

## Build the chain, don't just assert the conclusion

A KAA paragraph earns its marks by *connecting* steps, not by stating the endpoint. Open with the point, define the key term (AO1), anchor it in the question's context (AO2), then walk the chain link by link (AO3).

Take a contractionary monetary response to inflation. The weak version asserts: "higher interest rates reduce inflation." The Level 4 version chains it: a higher base rate → dearer mortgages and loans → households' disposable income falls and saving becomes more attractive → consumption and investment fall → aggregate demand shifts left → the inflationary gap narrows and the price level rises more slowly.

Every arrow is a step the mark scheme can credit. Notice that the chain *applies* to the case in the question — if the stimulus mentions a named economy or figure, use it. A diagram earns analysis marks only if you refer to it in the prose: "as shown, AD shifts from AD to AD1 and the price level falls from P to P1."

:::

::: section eyebrow="The EV paragraph" color="pink" icon="scale"

## Evaluation says what the argument depends ON

This is where the top band is won or lost. Evaluation is not a second list of points — it is weighing the chain you just built. The reliable levers are **magnitude** (how big is the effect?), **time lag** (how soon?), **assumptions** (what has to hold?), and the **counter-position** (what works against it?).

Run the levers on the rate-rise chain. *Magnitude:* the effect depends on how interest-sensitive consumption and investment are — if confidence is low, firms may not invest whatever the rate. *Time lag:* monetary policy works with a long and variable lag, so the price level may keep rising for months. *Assumption:* this only fixes inflation if it is demand-pull; if it is cost-push, tightening AD pushes output below trend without addressing the supply shock. *Counter:* higher rates also raise firms' costs, which can feed back into prices.

The move examiners reward is the conditional sentence: "raising the base rate is the right response *to demand-pull inflation* but a blunt instrument against cost-push, *because* it cannot reverse a leftward SRAS shift." That single qualified sentence outscores a paragraph of unqualified counter-points.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The finish" color="purple" icon="check"

## End on a supported judgement, not a fence-sit

The conclusion is an AO4 paragraph, not a summary. The mark scheme wants a *supported judgement*: a clear position, the strongest reason for it, and the condition under which it would flip.

The fence-sit — "there are arguments on both sides, so it depends" — earns almost nothing, because it names no condition. Compare: "On balance the rate rise is justified *if* the inflation is demand-pull and expectations are at risk of de-anchoring, because the cost to borrowers is outweighed by the value of credibility — but if the spike is cost-push, the better tool is supply-side, since tightening only deepens the output loss."

That sentence takes a side, gives a reason, prioritises one effect over another, and states what would reverse it. That is what "supported judgement" means, and it is the difference between Level 3 and Level 4.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="green" icon="cap"

## The moves that lift the level

For **KAA**, develop fewer chains more deeply. Two linked AO3 chains with application beat six asserted points. Define your terms once, apply to the context, and make every arrow a step the marker can tick.

For **EV**, weigh rather than list. Use magnitude, time lag, assumptions and the counter-position, and always say what the argument depends ON. The conditional sentence — "X is right *if* Y, because Z" — is the single highest-value habit in the whole paper.

Then plan first, judge last, and keep enough time for the second EV paragraph. Most lost marks on the 25-marker are not knowledge gaps — they are structure gaps.

:::
