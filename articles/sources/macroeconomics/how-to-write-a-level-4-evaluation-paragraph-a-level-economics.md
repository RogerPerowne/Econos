::: section eyebrow="The skill, not the topic" color="green" icon="target"

## Evaluation is weighing, not listing

Evaluation is the assessment objective — **AO4** — that decides whether a long-answer script reaches the top band. It is also the one most students misunderstand. They are taught "add a however and explain the downsides", so they produce a second pile of cause-and-effect chains. That is still analysis (AO3). It is not evaluation.

Evaluation means *weighing* an argument you have already built: how big is the effect, how soon does it arrive, what has to be true for it to hold, and what pushes the other way. Get that distinction and the rest is a small set of repeatable moves. This guide is those moves.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The diagnostic" color="purple" icon="bulb"

## The tell of a weak evaluation

Here is the quickest self-test. Read your evaluation sentence and ask: *could I paste this onto any other essay in the paper?* If yes, it is too generic to earn AO4.

"However, this might not work." "It depends on the situation." "There are other factors to consider." "In the long run things could be different." Every one of those would fit a question on monopoly, exchange rates or minimum wage equally well — which is exactly why they earn almost nothing. They show no engagement with *this* argument.

A Level 4 sentence cannot be moved. It is welded to the specific chain in front of it because it names the specific factor, condition or number that the argument turns on.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The four levers" color="blue" icon="scale"

## Magnitude, time lag, assumptions, counter-position

There are four reliable ways to weigh an argument, and a strong paragraph picks the one that bites hardest on the case.

**Magnitude.** How large is the effect, and on what does its size depend? A subsidy lowers price — but if demand is inelastic the quantity response is small, so the policy achieves little of its aim. Elasticities, multipliers and the size of the output gap are all magnitude levers.

**Time lag.** When does the effect arrive? Monetary policy works with a long and variable lag; supply-side reforms can take years; a fiscal stimulus hits faster. An argument that is correct in the long run can be the wrong call for a problem that is urgent now.

**Assumptions.** What has to be true for the chain to hold? "Higher rates cut inflation" assumes the inflation is demand-pull and that confidence does not collapse. Naming the assumption — and what happens if it fails — is pure AO4.

**Counter-position.** What works against the effect? Crowding out against a fiscal stimulus; firms passing higher costs into prices against a rate rise. State it, then judge whether it dominates.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Worked transformation" color="pink" icon="spark"

## From Level 2 to Level 4, one sentence at a time

Start with a typical Level 2 evaluation of a national minimum wage rise:

*"However, a higher minimum wage might cause unemployment, so it could be bad for workers."*

That asserts a downside but weighs nothing. Now apply the levers.

*Magnitude:* "The scale of any job losses depends on the elasticity of demand for low-paid labour — in sectors where labour is hard to substitute, such as social care, employment may barely fall."

*Counter and assumption:* "Higher wages can also raise demand, because low-paid workers have a high marginal propensity to consume, partly offsetting any fall in employment; this assumes firms cannot absorb the cost through lower profit margins, which larger firms often can."

*Conditional judgement:* "So a minimum-wage rise is more likely to reduce employment in low-margin, labour-intensive sectors than across the economy as a whole — meaning the policy's net effect on workers depends on which sectors dominate local employment."

Each sentence is anchored, each weighs rather than asserts, and the paragraph now reaches a reasoned position. That is Level 4.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="green" icon="cap"

## The Level 4 habits to drill

Switch from listing to weighing. If your sentence adds a new cause-and-effect chain, it is AO3; you only start earning AO4 when you judge the chain you already have.

Reach for the conditional form: "X holds *if* Y, *because* Z." It forces you to state the assumption (Y) and the mechanism (Z) in one move, and it is the single most reliable Level 4 sentence shape in the whole subject.

Finally, never let an evaluation sentence be one you could paste onto another essay. Anchor it to the specific factor, magnitude or context the question gives you — that anchoring is what separates a genuine Level 4 paragraph from a Level 2 hedge.

:::
