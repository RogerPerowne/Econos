::: section eyebrow="Learn it in 90 seconds" color="green" icon="bulb"

## Inflation is a price-level story, not a price story

A single price rising isn't inflation. Inflation is a *sustained* rise in the *general* price level — so the Office for National Statistics builds a basket of goods that an average household actually buys, prices the basket every month, and reports the year-on-year change.

That index — the **Consumer Prices Index (CPI)** — is the number everyone means by "the inflation rate" in the UK. The Bank of England's Monetary Policy Committee targets 2% CPI per year, symmetric (so falling below 2% is as much of a miss as overshooting). Anything that pushes the CPI above 2% pulls a policy response.

Three things can push it up, and they need very different responses.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The big picture · the three causes" color="purple" icon="target"

## Demand-pull, cost-push, and monetary

Each cause leaves a different signature on the AD/AS diagram, and that signature is the diagnostic tell every Edexcel mark scheme rewards.

**Demand-pull** — aggregate demand outpaces aggregate supply. AD shifts right; output and prices both rise. Symptom: an economy running hot, low unemployment, capacity utilisation high. The 2021 post-lockdown rebound was textbook demand-pull.

**Cost-push** — aggregate supply contracts. SRAS shifts left; output *falls* while prices rise (stagflation). Symptom: an external shock to input prices — energy, food, wages, imports. The 2022 energy price spike following Russia's invasion of Ukraine was textbook cost-push.

**Monetary** — too much money chasing too few goods. The money supply grows faster than real output for a sustained period; prices adjust upward to clear the gap. Symptom: usually a lagged consequence of central-bank balance-sheet expansion. The 2008-2021 QE programmes set this up; the 2022-23 spike collected on the bill.

The exam move is to use the **direction of output** as the diagnostic. Rising output + rising prices = demand-pull. Falling output + rising prices = cost-push. Rising output + accelerating prices with a long lag = monetary.

:::

::: econ-insight portraitKey="economistFriedman" tone="amber" label="Economist insight: Friedman and the monetarist view" attribution="Milton Friedman, <em>The Counter-Revolution in Monetary Theory</em> (1970)"
Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon in the sense that it is and can be produced only by a more rapid increase in the quantity of money than in output.
:::

::: section eyebrow="Real world example" color="blue" icon="globe"

## The 2022-23 UK inflation spike was all three at once

CPI hit 11.1% in October 2022 — the highest since 1981 — and that's why the Bank of England had to raise base rate from 0.1% to 5.25% over the following two years. But the textbook causes overlapped in a way they almost never do.

The **demand-pull** layer was pent-up household savings from lockdowns plus the £350bn furlough cushion meeting a partially-reopened economy in early 2022. Spending caught up with supply before supply chains had healed.

The **cost-push** layer was Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022. Wholesale gas prices peaked at over 600p/therm in August 2022 against a pre-pandemic norm of ~50p, and food prices followed because of fertiliser and grain market disruption. Ofgem's price cap rose from £1,277 (October 2021) to £4,279 (January 2023).

The **monetary** layer was a decade of QE. The Bank's balance sheet grew from £93bn (2008) to £895bn (2021). Most of that money sat as bank reserves through the 2010s — but as 2022 demand recovered, the velocity picked up and the latent inflation showed up.

The MPC's policy response — base rate to 5.25% — only addresses two of the three. Demand-pull responds directly (raise the cost of borrowing, slow spending). Cost-push barely responds (you can't make Putin sell more gas). Monetary responds slowly (it takes 18-24 months for rate hikes to feed through). That's why inflation peaked late and fell slowly.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## Inflation isn't always bad. Disinflation isn't always good.

The standard A-level answer treats inflation as obviously harmful — purchasing power, fixed-income earners, menu costs, shoe-leather costs. The top-band evaluation pushes back, three ways.

**Mild inflation greases the wheels.** A 2% target isn't arbitrary. With prices and wages rising slowly, firms can adjust *real* wages downward by holding nominal wages flat — much easier politically than cutting pay. A 0% target would mean real adjustments require nominal cuts; that's why deflation is the more dangerous mistake.

**Redistribution can be progressive.** Inflation transfers wealth from creditors to debtors. The UK government — by far the largest borrower in the economy — is the biggest beneficiary of unexpected inflation; mortgage holders are the second. Pensioners on fixed nominal incomes are the biggest losers. The distributional question is which group the policy framework is built to protect.

**The Phillips curve trade-off may still exist short-term.** A central bank deliberately running an economy slightly hot — accepting 3% inflation to keep unemployment at 3.5% rather than 5% — can be a defensible choice, *if* you don't believe expectations will adjust to undo the gain. The 2022-23 episode made that "if" much harder to defend.

The evaluation move is to say what the defence depends on, not to claim inflation is sometimes fine. A central bank that runs hot in a credible institutional setting (Bank of England, Fed) is different from one that does so in a low-credibility one (Argentina, Turkey).

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## The two-mark moves that lift your essay

For **AO3 analysis**, name the chain. "Demand-pull → AD shifts right → output and prices both rise → MPC raises base rate → cost of borrowing up → consumption + investment slow → AD shifts back." Mark schemes reward the linked steps. A bare statement that "interest rates fight inflation" gets one mark; the chain gets four.

For **AO4 evaluation**, make the cause-specific point. "Raising base rate is the right response to demand-pull inflation but a blunt instrument against cost-push, *because* it can't address the underlying supply shock and risks pushing output below trend without bringing prices back to target." That single conditional sentence is worth more than a paragraph of unqualified counter-points.

The exam habit to drill: diagnose the cause *before* recommending a policy. The diagnosis IS the evaluation.

:::
