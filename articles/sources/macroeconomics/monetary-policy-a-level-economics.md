## Monetary policy is one lever and a long arm

The Bank of England's job is to keep CPI inflation at 2%. To do it, the **Monetary Policy Committee** (nine members, eight meetings a year) sets one number: **Bank Rate**. That single rate works through a five-link chain — banks adjust their lending rates, households and firms adjust their spending and investing decisions, aggregate demand changes, and inflation responds.

The chain takes 6 to 18 months end-to-end. Monetary policy is always forecasting forward: the MPC is targeting inflation in 2026 by what it does today.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The transmission mechanism" color="blue" icon="target"

## The five-link chain examiners want named

The textbook transmission mechanism for a Bank Rate **rise**:

1. **Bank Rate up.** The MPC raises the rate the BoE pays on commercial bank reserves.
2. **Commercial bank rates follow.** Mortgage rates, business loan rates and savings rates all tend to rise.
3. **Borrowing falls; saving rises.** Higher rates make borrowing more expensive, saving more attractive. Households delay big purchases; firms postpone investment.
4. **C and I fall.** Lower consumption (C) and investment (I) drag aggregate demand left.
5. **Inflation eases.** Lower AD relieves price pressure; inflation moves back toward target.

Rate **cuts** work the chain in reverse: cheaper borrowing, more spending, AD shifts right, inflation pressure rebuilds.

The mark scheme rewards naming all five links AND naming the lag.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Beyond Bank Rate — the rest of the toolkit" color="purple" icon="scale"

## QE, forward guidance, credit easing

When Bank Rate is already near zero (the zero lower bound), the MPC reaches for less conventional tools.

- **Quantitative easing.** The Bank creates new reserves and uses them to buy financial assets — mainly UK government bonds (gilts). That pushes bond prices up, yields down, and long-term interest rates with them. Used aggressively from 2009 and again in 2020. The UK's QE programme peaked at £895bn.
- **Forward guidance.** The MPC commits, in advance, to a path for Bank Rate or to specific conditions for changing it. Lowers uncertainty and anchors expectations. Mark Carney's "out of the woods" framing in 2013 is the most-cited example.
- **Term Funding Scheme / Funding for Lending.** Cheap funding offered to commercial banks on condition they lend it to households and SMEs. A way of fixing the transmission mechanism when banks are deleveraging.
- **Quantitative tightening (QT).** The reverse of QE — allowing gilts to mature without rolling them over, or active selling. The UK started QT in 2022.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What can go wrong" color="pink" icon="globe"

## Why monetary policy doesn't always deliver

The transmission chain has weak links, and a high-mark essay names them.

- **Time lags.** 6 to 18 months between the rate move and the inflation effect. If the MPC is reacting to last year's data, it can be tightening into a recession or loosening into an overshoot.
- **Liquidity trap.** When Bank Rate is at zero, cuts can't go further. Conventional policy is out of room. QE was invented for this.
- **Confidence and expectations.** If households expect bad times, even low rates won't make them borrow. The MPC can lead the horse to water.
- **Bank willingness to lend.** Post-2008, commercial banks held more capital and lent less; rate cuts didn't translate to cheaper mortgages as fast. The Term Funding Scheme tried to fix this.
- **External shocks.** A 2022-style energy price spike is a supply shock. Tightening hits demand but doesn't touch oil and gas prices. The MPC ends up trading higher unemployment for a slower (not zero) inflation fall.
- **Exchange rate effects.** A rate rise tends to push sterling up, dampening exports. That helps cool inflation (imports cheaper) but also drags growth.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The 2022–24 case study" color="green" icon="check"

## What the MPC did when inflation hit 11%

In late 2021 the Bank of England started raising Bank Rate from 0.1%, the lowest in its history. By August 2023 it had reached 5.25%. CPI inflation, having peaked at 11.1% in October 2022, fell back to the 2% target by May 2024.

The textbook critique applies: the MPC was reacting to past data while the energy shock unwound. Some economists argued the tightening went too far given that much of the inflation was supply-driven; others argued it didn't go far enough given how anchored inflation expectations had to be kept. The lag means the question doesn't fully resolve until years later.

For exam purposes, the case study illustrates: monetary policy works against demand-pull inflation cleanly, against cost-push inflation only at the cost of output and employment.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Exam edge" color="green" icon="cap"

## What the mark scheme actually rewards

Three moves that lift a monetary-policy answer:

- **Name the chain.** Not "rates up → inflation down". Use the five links: Bank Rate → commercial bank rates → borrowing/saving decisions → C + I → AD → inflation.
- **Specify the lag.** "6 to 18 months" is the textbook figure. Mentioning it unlocks the AO4 evaluation about timing risk.
- **Distinguish the cause of inflation.** If the question is about cost-push or supply-shock inflation (energy, 2022), name the trade-off explicitly: monetary tightening reduces demand but doesn't shift the supply curve back. Output and employment pay the cost.

:::
