## Paper 2 is a macro paper that punishes generic answers

Paper 2 — "The UK economy: performance and policies" — is the macroeconomics paper on the Edexcel A specification. It draws on Theme 2 (the UK economy) and Theme 4 (a global perspective), and every part of it is built around a context: extracts of text, a table, sometimes a chart.

That structure is the whole point. The paper is not asking "do you know what a multiplier is?" It is asking "can you apply macro tools to *this* economy, in *these* conditions?" The students who lose marks are rarely the ones who don't know the content. They are the ones who know it and then write as if the extract weren't on the desk in front of them.

So before the mistakes, hold one idea: on Paper 2, the data is the application. Use it or leave marks behind.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Mistake 1" color="purple" icon="target"

## Writing past the extract

The single most common Paper 2 leak is the generic essay. A question gives you an extract on, say, UK business investment, and the answer that comes back is a textbook account of the accelerator and animal spirits with no figure, no firm, no line from the data quoted anywhere.

That answer can still earn AO1 and AO3 — knowledge and analysis — but it forfeits AO2 application, and on the longer questions AO2 is a meaningful slice of the marks. Worse, an ungrounded chain is usually a weaker chain: "investment rises" is a claim, but "the extract's fall in business confidence to a three-year low suggests planned investment will be deferred" is the same point doing work.

The fix is mechanical. Before writing, underline two or three usable details in the extract — a number, a direction of change, a named policy. Then make sure each appears in your answer, attached to a piece of analysis rather than dropped in as decoration.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Mistake 2" color="blue" icon="globe"

## Diagrams with no labels and no purpose

Macro diagrams — AD/AS, the Phillips curve, a currency market — earn analysis marks, but only when they are used. The recurring error is a diagram that is unlabelled, drawn in the wrong place, or never referred to again in the prose.

A diagram is a tool for showing a *change*. If you draw AD/AS, the question is what shifts and why: a fall in interest rates shifts AD right, so the price level and real output both rise. Axes must be labelled (price level and real output, not "P" and "Q" floating free), the shift must be shown with a clear arrow and a new equilibrium, and the prose must walk the reader through it.

A correctly labelled diagram that you then explain in words is worth several marks. A decorative one you never mention is worth almost nothing and eats time you needed for evaluation.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Mistake 3" color="green" icon="scale"

## Chains that break, and evaluation that never lands

Two failures travel together at the top of the paper.

The first is the broken chain. AO3 rewards connected logic — each link causing the next. "A weaker pound makes exports cheaper, so export volumes rise, so AD shifts right, so real output and employment rise" is a chain. "A weaker pound is good for exports and growth" is the same idea with the mechanism deleted, and it sits a level lower.

The second is evaluation that lists rather than judges. Examiner reports keep flagging answers that present two arguments and two counters and then simply stop. AO4 is not "show both sides"; it is "reach a supported judgement". Weigh the points using magnitude (how large is the effect?), time lag (when does it bite — and does it matter by then?), and context (which assumption actually holds in this extract?). Then commit: which side is stronger, and why. A conclusion that prioritises beats one that hedges, every time.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## The Paper 2 routine that protects your marks

Diagnose from the data, build one tight chain, evaluate on a stated criterion, conclude.

For **AO2**, plant two or three details from the extract into your analysis — a figure, a direction, a named policy — so the application is doing work, not sitting in the margin. For **AO3**, write fewer chains but finish each one: every link caused by the last, the diagram labelled and explained. For **AO4**, pick your ground — magnitude, time lag, or context — say which argument it favours, and state the judgement explicitly. The students who do this consistently are not writing more; they are writing answers that land.

:::
