## The Phillips curve is a trade-off, plotted

In 1958 the economist A.W. Phillips looked at nearly a century of UK data and noticed something tidy: years with low unemployment tended to have fast-rising wages, and years with high unemployment had slow-rising wages. Swap wage inflation for price inflation and you get the relationship every A-level student now draws — a downward-sloping curve with inflation on the vertical axis and unemployment on the horizontal.

The intuition is a labour-market one. When unemployment is low, firms compete for scarce workers, wages get bid up, costs rise, and prices follow. When unemployment is high, there is slack in the labour market, wage pressure is weak, and inflation stays low.

That gives policymakers an apparent menu: accept a bit more inflation to buy lower unemployment, or accept higher unemployment to squeeze inflation out. The rest of this topic is about why that menu turned out to be a short-run illusion.

## The short-run trade-off, and why it disappears

The short-run Phillips curve (SRPC) is the downward-sloping one. Along it, a government can genuinely trade a little inflation for a little less unemployment — for a while.

The catch is expectations. Suppose policymakers push aggregate demand up to drive unemployment below its natural rate. Inflation rises, as the curve predicts. But workers are not fooled forever: once they expect, say, 5% inflation, they bargain for 5% higher nominal wages just to stand still. Those higher wage costs feed straight back into prices. The short-run curve shifts *outward* — the same low unemployment now comes with even higher inflation.

Repeat the trick and inflation accelerates without any lasting fall in unemployment. The only rate of unemployment consistent with stable inflation is the one where expectations are met — the **non-accelerating-inflation rate of unemployment (NAIRU)**, also called the natural rate. Join up all the points where expectations are satisfied and you get the **long-run Phillips curve (LRPC)**: a vertical line at the NAIRU.

The exam move is to keep the two curves separate. The SRPC offers a trade-off; the LRPC says that in the long run you can have any inflation rate you like, but only one unemployment rate.

:::

::: econ-insight portraitKey="economistFriedman" tone="amber" label="Economist insight: Friedman on the natural rate" attribution="Milton Friedman, presidential address to the American Economic Association (1968)"
There is always a temporary trade-off between inflation and unemployment; there is no permanent trade-off. The temporary trade-off comes not from inflation per se, but from unanticipated inflation, which generally means, from a rising rate of inflation.
:::

::: section eyebrow="Real world example" color="blue" icon="globe"

## The 1970s: when the curve broke

For most of the 1950s and 1960s the trade-off looked reliable, and governments used it. Then the 1970s arrived and the relationship fell apart in plain sight.

The trigger was the supply side. The 1973 OPEC oil embargo quadrupled crude prices, and a second shock followed the 1979 Iranian revolution. Higher energy costs pushed up prices across the economy *while* squeezing output and raising unemployment. The result was **stagflation** — high inflation and high unemployment at the same time — which simply cannot sit on a single downward-sloping curve.

Two ideas explained it. First, the cost-push supply shocks shifted the whole short-run curve outward, worsening both variables together. Second, by the 1970s workers had lived through years of inflation and built it into their expectations and wage demands, so each burst of demand stimulus bought less and less unemployment relief and more and more inflation — exactly the expectations-augmented story Friedman and Phelps had set out before the decade began.

The policy lesson stuck. The UK's response under the early 1980s monetary squeeze was to accept a sharp, painful rise in unemployment to break inflation expectations — treating the trade-off as short-run and the NAIRU as the only sustainable target. It is no accident that central banks today, including the Bank of England, anchor on credible inflation targets rather than trying to ride down the short-run curve.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## How much of the trade-off survives?

The vertical long-run curve is the orthodox answer, but the top-band evaluation is not "the trade-off is fake." It is more careful than that.

**The short-run trade-off is real and useful.** Central banks exploit it every time they tighten or loosen policy. A credible central bank that surprises the economy can move unemployment in the short run — the debate is about how long "short run" lasts and how quickly expectations adjust.

**The NAIRU is not a fixed number.** It moves with the structure of the labour market — union power, benefit generosity, skills mismatch, hysteresis after long recessions. Supply-side policies that lower the NAIRU shift the *long-run* curve left, which is the only way to get lower unemployment and stable inflation together. That is the genuinely good news for policy.

**Expectations can be anchored.** The whole modern framework — independent central banks, explicit inflation targets — exists to keep expectations pinned near target so the short-run curve stays put. The flatter and more stable the curve, the cheaper it is to keep unemployment low. So the question is not whether a trade-off exists, but how credible the institution managing expectations is.

The evaluation move: say which curve you mean, then say what the result depends on — the speed of expectation adjustment, the source of the shock (demand or supply), and the credibility of the policymaker.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## The moves that lift your essay

For **AO3 analysis**, run the expectations chain explicitly. "Government boosts AD → unemployment falls below NAIRU → inflation rises → workers revise expectations up → nominal wages rise → SRPC shifts outward → unemployment returns to NAIRU at higher inflation." A diagram showing the SRPC shifting up the vertical LRPC earns the analysis marks a single curve never will.

For **AO4 evaluation**, anchor on the distinction between short run and long run and on the source of the shock. "There is a short-run trade-off, but no long-run one *because* expectations adjust; and a cost-push supply shock shifts the curve outward rather than moving along it." Naming the NAIRU and pointing out that supply-side policy is the only lever that improves both variables together is worth more than any list of generic costs.

:::
