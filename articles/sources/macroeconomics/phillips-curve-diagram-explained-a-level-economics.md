## This is a "how to draw it" guide

The Phillips curve is one of the few macro diagrams where students lose marks before they have written a word — because they draw the wrong axes. This guide is purely the diagram technique: the right axes, the two curves, and how to show a shift so the examiner can read your story at a glance.

Get the frame right and the Phillips curve becomes the cleanest way to argue the inflation-unemployment conflict in Theme 2 evaluation.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Frame the axes first" color="purple" icon="scale"

## Inflation up the side, unemployment along the bottom

The single thing to drill: **inflation rate on the vertical axis, unemployment rate on the horizontal axis**. This is not the AD-AS diagram. Write the labels out in full before you draw anything.

Now the curves. The **short-run Phillips curve (SRPC)** slopes downward — it captures Phillips' original finding that lower unemployment came with higher wage and price inflation. The **long-run Phillips curve (LRPC)** is a **vertical line** at the natural rate of unemployment (the NAIRU). Mark the point where the short-run curve crosses the long-run curve: that is where the economy sits when inflation is correctly anticipated.

A fully labelled frame — both axes named, the downward SRPC, the vertical LRPC, and the natural-rate point — is the baseline the mark scheme checks first.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Shift vs movement" color="blue" icon="bulb"

## What moves you along, what shifts the curve

A **movement along** the short-run curve is the trade-off in action: looser policy boosts demand, unemployment falls, inflation rises — you slide up the SRPC. That is the short-term gain governments are tempted by.

A **shift of the whole short-run curve** happens when **inflation expectations** change (or a supply shock hits). If people expect higher inflation, the SRPC shifts outward: at every unemployment rate, inflation is now higher. This is the expectations-augmented Phillips curve, and it is how the diagram represents the 1970s stagflation that broke the original single relationship.

The **long-run curve** does not shift with demand — it only moves if the natural rate itself changes (better labour-market flexibility, retraining, lower frictional unemployment shift it left). Keep that distinction sharp: demand moves you along and shifts the SRPC; structural change moves the LRPC.

:::

::: econ-insight portraitKey="economistFriedman" tone="amber" label="Economist insight: Friedman on the natural rate" attribution="Milton Friedman, presidential address to the American Economic Association (1968)"
There is always a temporary trade-off between inflation and unemployment; there is no permanent trade-off.
:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="pink" icon="cap"

## Convert the diagram into marks

Two moves earn the top band. First, **draw both curves and use them together**: show the economy sliding up the short-run curve in the short run, then the curve shifting out so unemployment returns to the natural rate at higher inflation. That sequence — short-run gain, long-run no-gain — is the entire evaluation argument in one diagram.

Second, **refer to the diagram in the prose**: "as shown, the economy moves from A to B along the SRPC, but rising expectations shift the curve to SRPC1, returning unemployment to the natural rate at point C with permanently higher inflation." Linking the labelled points to the written analysis is what turns AO3 diagram marks into AO4 evaluation marks.

:::
