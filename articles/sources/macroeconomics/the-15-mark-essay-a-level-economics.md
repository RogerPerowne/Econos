## Same skeleton, smaller frame

The 15-mark essay is the 25-marker's little sibling. It is assessed against the same four objectives — knowledge (AO1), application (AO2), analysis (AO3) and evaluation (AO4) — and bundles them the same way: a **KAA** strand and an **EV** strand, with the marks split between them.

So the structure does not change. What changes is *scope*. You have fewer marks, less time and a narrower question, which means fewer chains and tighter evaluation — but never *no* evaluation. The mistake to avoid is treating a 15-marker as a 25-marker with the volume turned down on everything equally. The right adjustment is to cut the *number* of arguments while protecting the *quality* of each and the time for EV.

## Cut the breadth, keep the depth

On a 25-marker you might build three KAA chains and evaluate each. On a 15-marker, build **one or two** — and develop them just as fully.

This is counter-intuitive under pressure: the instinct is to make every paragraph shorter so you can fit more of them in. But a half-built chain earns half-marks at best, and four half-built chains add up to less than two complete ones. Depth is what AO3 rewards. So choose your strongest one or two arguments, develop each link fully, and let the others go.

The selection itself is a skill. Pick the argument you can chain furthest and apply most directly to the context in the question — not the one that sounds most impressive. A chain you can complete beats a chain you merely start.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Protect the evaluation" color="blue" icon="scale"

## The clock is the enemy of AO4

The single most common way to lose marks on a 15-marker is to run out of time. Two enthusiastic analysis chains eat the whole window, the pen reaches the evaluation with three minutes left, and the script lands a hollow "however, it might not work" — or nothing at all. Because EV marks are baked into the total, that caps the answer below the top level no matter how good the analysis was.

The fix is to ration before you write. Reserve the final third of your time for evaluation and the judgement, and treat that boundary as fixed. If you are still mid-chain when it arrives, stop the chain and switch to EV. A complete answer with sharp evaluation beats a beautiful analysis that never gets weighed.

With the time you have, you do not need three evaluation points. Pick the **one lever that bites hardest** — usually magnitude, time lag or a key assumption — develop it on the chain you built, and move to the judgement.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The finish" color="pink" icon="check"

## A short supported judgement still has to be supported

The 15-mark conclusion is shorter than a 25-marker's, but it does the same job: a *supported judgement*, not a summary.

State a position, give the main reason, and name the condition it depends on — in two or three sentences. "On balance the policy is justified *if* the inflation is demand-pull, because the cost to borrowers is outweighed by the value of anchoring expectations; the judgement reverses if the spike is cost-push." That is enough. Brevity is fine; fence-sitting is not. "There are arguments on both sides" still earns nothing here, because it names no condition and takes no side.

The shorter format actually rewards the conditional sentence even more, because it lets you pack a position, a reason and a caveat into a single line — exactly the AO4 the marker is looking for, with no words wasted.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="green" icon="cap"

## The 15-mark habits to drill

Keep the structure of a 25-marker and shrink the scope, not the quality. One or two fully developed KAA chains beat four half-finished ones, so select your strongest arguments and complete them.

Ring-fence the last third of your time for evaluation and the judgement. Pick the single evaluation lever that bites hardest, develop it, and finish on a brief supported judgement that takes a side and names its condition.

The 15-marker is not a different exam skill — it is the same skill under a tighter clock. The candidates who do well are the ones who plan the timing as deliberately as the economics.

:::
