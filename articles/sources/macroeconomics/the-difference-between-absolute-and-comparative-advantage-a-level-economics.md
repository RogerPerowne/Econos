## Productivity versus what you give up

The two ideas answer different questions.

**Absolute advantage** asks: *who is more productive?* A country has an absolute advantage in a good if it can produce more of it from the same resources — or the same amount from fewer resources. It is a straight comparison of output per input.

**Comparative advantage** asks: *who gives up less?* A country has a comparative advantage in a good if its **opportunity cost** of producing that good — the quantity of the *other* good it must sacrifice — is lower than the rival's.

The reason this matters is that the two can disagree. A country can be more productive at making *everything* (absolute advantage in both goods) yet still only have a comparative advantage in *some* of them — because it is impossible to have the lowest opportunity cost in both goods at once. And it is comparative advantage, not absolute, that decides who should specialise in what.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The big picture · the two-country example" color="purple" icon="target"

## A worked example: cloth and wine

Take the classic two-country, two-good case. Suppose each country has the same amount of labour to allocate, and in one year of work each can produce the following maximum amounts:

- **Country A** can make either **20 units of cloth** or **20 units of wine**.
- **Country B** can make either **10 units of cloth** or **5 units of wine**.

**Absolute advantage first.** Country A out-produces Country B in *both* goods (20 vs 10 cloth, 20 vs 5 wine). On the absolute test, A is simply better at everything, and a naive answer would have A make both goods and B make nothing — which would mean no trade at all.

**Now opportunity cost.** Work out what each gives up to make one unit:

- In **Country A**, making 1 unit of wine means giving up 1 unit of cloth (20 cloth ÷ 20 wine). Its opportunity cost of wine is **1 cloth**.
- In **Country B**, making 1 unit of wine means giving up 2 units of cloth (10 cloth ÷ 5 wine). Its opportunity cost of wine is **2 cloth**.

So Country A gives up less cloth to make wine — A has the **comparative advantage in wine**. Flip it: A's opportunity cost of 1 cloth is 1 wine, while B's is only ½ wine (5 wine ÷ 10 cloth). Country B gives up less wine to make cloth — B has the **comparative advantage in cloth**.

Each country has a comparative advantage in one good, even though A has an absolute advantage in both. That mismatch is the whole story.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The payoff · why specialising raises output" color="blue" icon="scale"

## Specialise, then trade

If each country specialises where its opportunity cost is lower, total output rises.

Country A pours its labour into **wine** (its lower-opportunity-cost good); Country B pours its labour into **cloth**. Compared with each trying to make a bit of both, the world now produces more wine *and* uses B's labour where it is least wasteful. Combined output of the two goods is higher than if A had made everything.

The gains are then split through trade. The key is the **terms of trade** — the rate at which the goods exchange. As long as the exchange rate lies *between* the two opportunity-cost ratios (between 1 cloth-per-wine in A and 2 cloth-per-wine in B), *both* countries end up able to consume more than they could alone. A gets cloth more cheaply than making it itself; B gets wine more cheaply than making it itself.

That is the result absolute advantage cannot reach: trade benefits both countries, including the less productive one, because the gains come from differences in *opportunity cost*, not differences in raw productivity.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## The model leans on tidy assumptions

The comparative-advantage case for trade is powerful, but it rests on assumptions worth questioning — and saying so is what lifts an evaluation.

The simple model assumes **constant opportunity costs** (the trade-off does not change as a country specialises), **no transport costs**, **factor mobility within a country**, and that the **terms of trade actually settle between the two ratios**. In reality, opportunity costs often rise as a country specialises (diminishing returns), transport and trade costs can swallow the gains on bulky goods, and complete specialisation leaves a country exposed if demand for its single export collapses.

There are distributional limits too: comparative advantage shows the *total* gains are positive, but it says nothing about how they are shared *within* a country. Specialisation can wipe out the industries that lose out, concentrating the costs on particular workers and regions even as the nation gains overall.

So the evaluation is not "free trade is always best" but "the gains depend on stable terms of trade, modest transport costs, and a policy to compensate the losers." That conditional is the difference between quoting the model and judging it.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## Compute the opportunity cost, then specialise

For **AO2 application**, when a question hands you a numerical table, do not eyeball who is "better". Calculate each country's **opportunity cost** of each good, identify the lower one, and assign the comparative advantage from that. Showing the division — "A gives up 1 cloth per wine, B gives up 2, so A specialises in wine" — is the analysis examiners mark.

For **AO4 evaluation**, name the assumption you are relaxing: "the gains assume constant opportunity costs and low transport costs; with rising costs as A specialises, the benefit shrinks, and the gains may not reach the workers in B's wine industry that close down." Anchoring on a specific assumption — and on who bears the cost — is the judgement the top band rewards, rather than a blanket claim that trade is good.

:::
