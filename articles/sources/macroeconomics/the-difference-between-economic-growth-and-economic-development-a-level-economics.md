::: section eyebrow="Learn it in 90 seconds" color="green" icon="bulb"

## One is a number, the other is a verdict

**Economic growth** has a precise definition: an increase in **real GDP** — the value of everything an economy produces, adjusted for inflation — over time. It is one number, going up or down. That is its strength as a measure and its limitation.

**Economic development** is broader and more qualitative. It asks whether people's *lives* are getting better: are they healthier, better educated, freer, less poor? Are the gains shared, or captured by a few? Is the improvement sustainable, or borrowed from the future?

Growth feeds development — more output can fund schools, hospitals and infrastructure — but the link is not automatic. The whole topic lives in the gap between "the economy got bigger" and "people are better off".

:::

::: section eyebrow="The big picture · measuring development" color="purple" icon="globe"

## Why one number isn't enough — and the HDI

If growth is real GDP, the obvious question is how you measure something as broad as development. The standard answer is the United Nations' **Human Development Index (HDI)**.

The HDI deliberately combines three dimensions rather than relying on output alone:

- **Health**, proxied by life expectancy at birth.
- **Education**, proxied by expected and mean years of schooling.
- **Income**, proxied by gross national income per capita.

By blending health and education with income, the HDI captures cases where money is rising but lives are not — or where a country with modest income still delivers long, well-schooled lives. It deliberately *down-weights* pure output to make that point.

Even the HDI is incomplete: it says nothing directly about inequality, the environment, political freedom or gender. That is why economists treat development as a *family* of indicators — HDI alongside poverty rates, inequality measures and sustainability — rather than a single score. The lesson for the exam is that no single number captures development the way real GDP captures growth.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The payoff · when growth and development diverge" color="blue" icon="globe"

## Growth without development

The reason the distinction earns marks is that the two can come apart.

Growth can rise without much development when the gains are **narrowly distributed** — output climbs but only a small group captures it, so the median household sees little change. It can happen when growth is **extractive** — driven by selling natural resources without reinvesting the proceeds in health, education or infrastructure, so the productive base of the economy does not broaden. And it can happen when growth carries heavy **environmental or social costs** — pollution, congestion, displacement — that offset the material gains.

The reverse is rarer but possible: a country can improve health and education outcomes faster than its income alone would predict, by spending effectively on public services. The takeaway is that growth is a *means*, not the end. Whether it produces development depends on how the extra output is distributed, what it is reinvested in, and what it costs.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## Growth is usually necessary, rarely sufficient

The balanced position is not "growth is bad" — for most low-income countries, sustained growth in real GDP is the single most powerful driver of rising living standards, because it generates the resources to fund everything else. Without it, there is little to redistribute or reinvest.

The "however" is that growth is **necessary but not sufficient**. Whether it translates into development depends on choices: the tax-and-spending decisions that turn output into schools and hospitals, the institutions that determine whether the gains are shared, and the environmental constraints that determine whether today's growth undermines tomorrow's. So the evaluation says what development *depends on* — distribution, reinvestment, and sustainability — rather than treating GDP as either the whole story or an irrelevance.

That is also why the choice of *measure* is itself an evaluation point: judging a country only by GDP per capita can flatter an extractive or unequal economy, while the HDI and inequality measures reveal whether the growth is being felt.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## Use the right word, then test the link

For **AO1 knowledge**, define the two terms precisely and separately: growth is a rise in real GDP; development is a broad, multi-dimensional rise in living standards. Naming the HDI and its three dimensions — health, education, income — is the fast way to show you know development is more than output.

For **AO4 evaluation**, do not assert that growth equals development or that it is worthless. Test the link: "growth provides the resources for development, *but* whether living standards rise depends on how the gains are distributed and reinvested, which is why GDP per capita can rise while the HDI barely moves." That conditional, anchored on distribution and reinvestment, is the judgement the top band rewards.

:::
