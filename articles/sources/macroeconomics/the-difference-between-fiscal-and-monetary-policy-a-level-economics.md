::: section eyebrow="Learn it in 90 seconds" color="green" icon="bulb"

## Same target, different hands on the levers

Both fiscal and monetary policy are **demand-side** policies: their main job is to shift aggregate demand to manage growth, inflation and unemployment. That shared purpose is exactly why students confuse them.

The clean way to separate them is to ask three questions: *Who controls it? What tool do they use? How does the tool reach aggregate demand?*

**Fiscal policy** is controlled by the government — in the UK, HM Treasury, announced at the Budget. Its tools are **taxation and public spending**. It reaches AD directly: government spending is itself a component of AD, and tax changes alter households' and firms' disposable income.

**Monetary policy** is controlled by the **central bank** — the Bank of England's Monetary Policy Committee, independent of government since 1997. Its main tool is the **bank rate** (plus the money supply and asset purchases such as QE). It reaches AD indirectly, by changing the cost and availability of borrowing.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The big picture · the transmission routes" color="purple" icon="target"

## How each one reaches aggregate demand

The mechanisms are different even though the destination — a shift in AD — is the same.

**Fiscal, expansionary:** government raises spending or cuts taxes → government spending (G) rises directly and/or households keep more income → consumption (C) and investment (I) rise → AD shifts right. The injection is immediate and can be targeted (a specific tax cut, a named infrastructure project).

**Monetary, expansionary:** the MPC cuts the bank rate → commercial lending rates fall → borrowing is cheaper and saving less rewarding → consumption and investment rise, and a weaker exchange rate can lift net exports → AD shifts right. The effect is broad and economy-wide, but it works through private decisions the central bank cannot force.

The contractionary versions simply reverse each chain: higher taxes or lower spending, or a higher bank rate, pulling AD back to cool inflation.

The decisive differences are *control* and *side effects*. Fiscal policy changes the budget balance directly — expansion usually means a bigger deficit. Monetary policy changes borrowing costs for everyone but leaves the budget alone, and because it is set by an independent committee it is insulated from the electoral cycle.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The payoff · choosing between them" color="blue" icon="scale"

## Time lags and the lower bound

Two practical differences usually decide which policy an answer should recommend.

**Time lags.** Monetary policy can be changed quickly — the MPC meets regularly and can move the bank rate at a single meeting — but its effect on the economy works with a long lag, often quoted as 18 to 24 months, as borrowing decisions feed through. Fiscal policy is slower to *enact*, because tax and spending changes need Budget decisions and time to legislate, but a spending injection can hit demand faster once it lands.

**The lower bound.** When interest rates are already very low, the central bank has little room to cut further, so monetary policy loses traction. This is why, in deep recessions, the argument tilts towards fiscal policy: government spending can still add to demand directly even when rate cuts are exhausted. That is the case for using fiscal stimulus when monetary policy is "out of road".

:::

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## Each tool has a cost the other does not

Recommending one over the other is an evaluation, not a definition, so say what the choice depends on.

Fiscal expansion adds to the **budget deficit and national debt**, which can raise the cost of government borrowing and, in the Classical view, *crowd out* private investment if it pushes up interest rates. Its benefit is precision: spending can be aimed at the regions or sectors that need it.

Monetary policy avoids the deficit and is politically independent, which makes it more *credible* for controlling inflation. But it is a blunt instrument — a single bank rate hits the whole economy, it cannot target a struggling region, and it leans on private borrowing decisions the bank cannot compel.

So the answer depends on the situation: the size of any output gap, where interest rates already sit, the state of the public finances, and whether inflation or unemployment is the binding problem. Near full capacity with inflation rising, monetary tightening is the orthodox tool. In a deep slump with rates near zero, fiscal policy carries more of the load.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## Name the body, the tool, and the route

For **AO1 knowledge**, get the attribution right every time: monetary policy is the Bank of England's MPC and the bank rate; fiscal policy is the Treasury and tax-and-spend. Swapping them is the quickest way to lose marks at the start of an answer.

For **AO3 analysis**, draw the transmission chain rather than asserting an outcome — "rate cut → cheaper borrowing → C and I rise → AD shifts right" — and for **AO4 evaluation**, justify the choice with a condition the examiner can mark: "with the bank rate near its lower bound, monetary policy has limited room, so fiscal expansion is the more effective tool to close the output gap." That conditional is the move that separates a list of facts from a judgement.

:::
