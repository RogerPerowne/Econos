## Unemployment is one word for four problems

A worker without a job is unemployed. But **why** they're without a job determines what policy will help. The four causes:

- **Frictional** — between jobs. Short-term, voluntary. Always non-zero.
- **Structural** — skills or industry mismatch. Long-term, geographically concentrated. UK coalfield closures, manufacturing decline in the North-East.
- **Cyclical (demand-deficient)** — recession. AD shifts left, firms shed labour. 2008–09; 2020 COVID.
- **Seasonal** — predictable annual swing. Agriculture, tourism, retail at Christmas.

Diagnose the type first; the policy follows.

:::

## Claimant count vs Labour Force Survey

The UK publishes two unemployment figures every month and they usually disagree.

- **The claimant count** is administrative — it counts people receiving Jobseeker's Allowance or the unemployment component of Universal Credit. It's timely, cheap, and complete (every claimant counted) but misses anyone who's not eligible to claim (means-tested out, sanctioned, partnered with an earner over the threshold).
- **The Labour Force Survey** (LFS) is a sample-based ONS survey applying the ILO definition: willing, able, actively seeking work, available within two weeks. It captures people who don't claim. It's the internationally comparable measure and what every cross-country chart uses.

The LFS usually reports more unemployment than the claimant count because it sweeps in non-claimants. A 0.3-percentage-point gap is normal; in episodes of benefits reform (e.g. UC rollout) the gap widens.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Cyclical vs structural — the diagnostic" color="purple" icon="scale"

## The 2008 vs the UK Northern coalfields

The single most useful diagnostic test: **does unemployment fall when aggregate demand recovers?**

- **2008–09 cyclical unemployment.** AD shifted left after the financial crisis, firms shed workers, unemployment spiked to 8%. When demand recovered (with a long lag and a lot of QE), unemployment fell back to pre-crisis levels by ~2015. That's the cyclical signature.
- **UK coalfields structural unemployment.** Pit closures in the 1980s left whole communities with the wrong skills for the new economy. Aggregate demand recovered repeatedly through the 1990s and 2000s. Local unemployment did not. The skills and capital were misaligned.

If demand-side policy fixes it, it was cyclical. If demand-side policy doesn't touch it, it was structural — and you need supply-side intervention (retraining, infrastructure, regional regeneration).

:::

::: section eyebrow="Policy match — each type, each cure" color="pink" icon="globe"

## Diagnose first, then treat

| Type | Cause | Right policy |
|---|---|---|
| Frictional | Information / matching delay | Job-search platforms, employment services, easier housing mobility |
| Structural | Skills / industry mismatch | Training, apprenticeships, regional investment, education reform |
| Cyclical | Weak aggregate demand | Demand-side: monetary or fiscal stimulus to shift AD right |
| Seasonal | Annual demand pattern | Income smoothing, off-season employment programmes, tourism diversification |
| Real-wage | Wages held above market-clearing | Reform minimum wage / collective bargaining (politically charged) |

The classic A-level error is using fiscal stimulus to attack structural unemployment. It doesn't work — extra spending raises demand for the wrong skills.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The hidden category" color="green" icon="check"

## Inactivity — the labour force people leave

Headline unemployment can fall for two opposite reasons: (1) people find jobs, or (2) people give up looking. The second is **economic inactivity** and it's tracked separately.

Post-COVID the UK rate of long-term sickness rose sharply, pushing up inactivity. Unemployment looked stable; the underlying labour market was weakening. The Bank of England's MPC watches inactivity as closely as the unemployment rate.

For a complete labour-market picture you need three statistics together: employment rate (working as % of working-age population), unemployment rate (% of active labour force), and inactivity rate (% of working-age population not in the labour force).

:::

::: section eyebrow="Natural rate and NAIRU" color="purple" icon="scale"

## Why unemployment never reaches zero

Even in a boom, some unemployment persists. Frictional workers between jobs, structural workers in transition. The **natural rate of unemployment** is that floor — the level at which cyclical unemployment is zero and only frictional + structural remain.

The **NAIRU** (non-accelerating inflation rate of unemployment) is the related concept: the unemployment rate consistent with stable inflation. Push unemployment below the NAIRU and wage growth accelerates, dragging inflation up. The Phillips curve sits on top of this idea.

AQA examines the natural rate / NAIRU directly (4.2.3.2 (f)); Edexcel A treats it more loosely under the Phillips curve content in Theme 2.6.4.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Exam edge" color="green" icon="cap"

## Three moves that lift the mark

- **Name the type before you analyse.** "Demand-deficient unemployment caused by the 2008 contraction in AD" beats "unemployment".
- **Note the measure.** Quote the ONS LFS rate for international comparison; mention claimant count for timeliness.
- **Match policy to type.** A-level mark schemes specifically punish prescribing fiscal stimulus for structural problems, or training programmes for cyclical ones. The diagnosis IS the answer.

:::
