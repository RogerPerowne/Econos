## A vertical line means output is fixed

The long-run aggregate supply (LRAS) curve in the Classical model is drawn as a vertical line. A vertical curve says something specific: whatever the price level, the economy supplies the *same* quantity of real output in the long run.

That fixed quantity is the economy's **full-capacity** or **potential** output — what it can produce when every factor of production is being used at its sustainable rate and all markets have cleared. It depends on how many workers, machines, and resources there are and how productive they are, not on how much anyone wants to spend.

So the vertical LRAS is really a claim about cause and effect: in the long run, *supply* sets output and *demand* sets the price level.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The big picture · the Classical argument" color="purple" icon="target"

## Why the long run forces the curve upright

The argument runs through factor markets, especially the labour market.

Suppose aggregate demand rises. In the **short run**, nominal wages are sticky — set in contracts, slow to renegotiate. Firms face higher selling prices but the same wage bill, so profit margins widen and they expand output. That is why SRAS slopes upward, and why output can sit *above* potential temporarily.

But that boom drives up demand for labour and other inputs. Over time, wages and input prices rise to catch up with the higher price level. As costs climb, the temporary profit incentive disappears, and firms cut output back. The adjustment stops only when real output has returned to potential — the level set by the supply of factors.

Trace it as a chain: AD rises → prices rise → output rises above potential short-run → wages and costs adjust upward → SRAS shifts left → output returns to potential, at a higher price level. The end point is always the same quantity of output. Plot every possible end point and you get a vertical line.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The payoff · what the assumption decides" color="blue" icon="scale"

## The vertical curve is the policy verdict

Because the LRAS is vertical, two big conclusions follow automatically.

First, **demand-side policy cannot raise long-run output.** A government boosting AD through spending or tax cuts, or a central bank cutting rates, shifts AD right. Along a vertical LRAS that lifts the price level and nothing else once adjustment is complete. The Classical reading is that demand management buys a short-run output gain at the cost of long-run inflation.

Second, **growth must come from the supply side.** The only way to raise output permanently is to move the vertical line rightward — more workers, more capital, better technology, higher productivity. Education, infrastructure, deregulation, and incentives to invest are the levers, because they expand productive potential rather than just spending.

This is why a candidate who draws a vertical LRAS *and then* claims a demand stimulus raises trend growth has contradicted their own diagram. The shape of the curve is the argument.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## How fast is the long run?

The vertical LRAS is right only if the economy actually returns to potential reasonably quickly. The Keynesian challenge is that it might not.

If wages are *downwardly* rigid — workers resist nominal pay cuts — then a fall in AD does not trigger the smooth downward adjustment the Classical story assumes. Output can stay below potential for years, with unemployment that does not self-correct. On a Keynesian diagram the LRAS has a long flat or sloped stretch below full capacity where demand *does* change real output, becoming vertical only at the capacity ceiling.

So the evaluation is not "the curve is wrong" but "it depends on the time horizon and on how flexible markets really are." In a deep recession with spare capacity, demand-side policy can raise real output, just as the Keynesian region predicts. Near full capacity, the Classical vertical section bites and extra demand mostly fuels inflation. The skilled move is to say which regime the economy is in before recommending a policy.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## Make your diagram and your words agree

For **AO3 analysis**, state the mechanism that makes the curve vertical, do not just assert it: "in the long run nominal wages adjust to the higher price level, eroding the temporary profit incentive, so SRAS shifts back and output returns to potential." Naming the adjustment process is what separates a described diagram from an analysed one.

For **AO4 evaluation**, anchor on the time horizon. "The vertical LRAS holds once markets clear, but if wages are downwardly rigid the economy can sit below potential, so in a recession demand-side policy can still raise real output." That conditional shows you understand *why* the Classical and Keynesian models reach different policy verdicts — which is the entire reason the topic exists.

:::
