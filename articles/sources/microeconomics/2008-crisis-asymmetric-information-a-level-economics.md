## A crisis is easier to understand as an information failure

The everyday story of 2008 is "greedy banks and a housing bubble". The economics story is tighter and more useful: a cascade of **asymmetric information** failures, where one side of a transaction knew more than the other and markets stopped allocating risk properly.

Two failures do most of the work. **Adverse selection** is hidden *quality* before a deal — a lender can't see how risky a borrower really is, so it ends up with a worse pool than it bargained for. **Moral hazard** is hidden *action* after a deal — once someone is insulated from a risk, they behave less carefully.

Stack these through the mortgage chain — borrower, lender, securitiser, investor — and you can see how individual decisions that each made local sense added up to a system that mispriced risk on an enormous scale. The trick for the exam is to keep adverse selection and moral hazard distinct, because they happen at different points and need different fixes.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The mechanism · the chain of information gaps" color="purple" icon="target"

## From the borrower's kitchen table to a frozen market

Walk it in order.

**Origination — adverse selection.** Sub-prime borrowers knew more about their own ability to repay than the lenders did. When credit was loose, the borrowers most eager to take large loans on easy terms were disproportionately the riskiest — the lender's pool got worse precisely because it couldn't see quality.

**Securitisation — moral hazard.** Lenders increasingly didn't *hold* the loans. They bundled them into mortgage-backed securities and sold them on. The instant you no longer bear the default risk, your incentive to check the borrower's quality weakens. The party taking the action (lending) was insulated from its consequences (default).

**The market freeze — the lemons problem.** The securities were so complex that buyers couldn't tell which were sound and which were toxic. Once doubt set in, buyers did what Akerlof's theory predicts: they discounted *everything* or refused to trade at all, because they couldn't separate good from bad. Even sound assets became unsellable. The market for these securities seized up.

**Transmission — moral hazard again, at the top.** Some institutions had grown "too big to fail", expecting rescue. That expectation is itself a moral hazard: it weakens the incentive to manage risk, because the downside is someone else's problem. The chain ran: hidden borrower quality → broken originator incentives → unpriceable securities → frozen funding markets → bank failures.

:::

::: econ-insight tone="amber" label="Economist insight: Akerlof on the market for lemons" attribution="George Akerlof, <em>The Market for Lemons: Quality Uncertainty and the Market Mechanism</em> (1970)"
There are many markets in which buyers use some market statistic to judge the quality of prospective purchases. There is incentive for sellers to market poor quality merchandise, since the returns for good quality accrue mainly to the entire group rather than to the individual seller.
:::

::: section eyebrow="Real world example" color="blue" icon="globe"

## Northern Rock: when the information panic reached Britain

The clearest UK marker is Northern Rock. Unlike a traditional bank funded mainly by retail deposits, Northern Rock funded a large share of its mortgage lending through short-term **wholesale** borrowing — money it had to keep rolling over in the markets.

That model worked while those markets were liquid. But the 2007 panic over mortgage-backed securities — the lemons problem in action — made lenders unwilling to lend to anyone whose assets they couldn't assess. Wholesale funding dried up. Northern Rock could no longer refinance, turned to the Bank of England for emergency support, and the news triggered the first run on a British bank in over a century: queues outside branches in September 2007. It was nationalised in 2008.

Trace the information chain through to this point. Opacity about US sub-prime securities → buyers can't price risk → wholesale credit markets freeze → a bank dependent on that funding can't roll it over → depositors, themselves unable to verify the bank's health, rationally rush to withdraw (a self-fulfilling run driven by *their* information gap). A global adverse-selection-and-moral-hazard story became a very visible British queue.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## Information failure is necessary but not the whole story

A top-band answer treats asymmetric information as a powerful lens, not the only one.

**Other forces amplified it.** Loose monetary policy, a genuine housing bubble, ratings agencies stamping complex securities as safe, and thin capital buffers all mattered. Asymmetric information explains *why risk was mispriced*; it doesn't by itself explain the *scale*, which depended on leverage and the interconnection of institutions.

**The policy fixes have side-effects.** Bailing out banks stopped the immediate collapse but reinforced the very moral hazard that helped cause it — rescue confirms the "too big to fail" expectation. Post-crisis regulation (higher capital requirements, ring-fencing, tougher lending standards) tries to attack the information failures directly, but tighter screening also rations credit to some borrowers who would have repaid.

**Signals can be gamed.** Ratings were meant to solve the lemons problem by signalling quality; instead they gave false comfort. That is a general lesson: a remedy for asymmetric information (a signal or a guarantee) can itself be captured or misread, shifting the failure rather than removing it.

The evaluative move: say what the information story explains (mispricing of risk) and what it doesn't (the magnitude, which came from leverage and interconnection), and note that the fixes trade one problem for another.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## Keep the two failures apart, then chain them

For **AO3 analysis**, label each link by its failure type: sub-prime lending = adverse selection (hidden quality); originate-and-sell = moral hazard (hidden action); the frozen securities market = Akerlof's lemons problem; too-big-to-fail = moral hazard at the system level. The marks come from naming the mechanism, not from narrating events.

For **AO4 evaluation**, concede what information failure can't explain on its own — leverage, the bubble, interconnection — and note that the standard fixes (bailouts, regulation) each create a counter-effect (renewed moral hazard, credit rationing). Reference Akerlof by name; examiners reward a candidate who knows the lemons model is the theory behind the market freeze.

:::
