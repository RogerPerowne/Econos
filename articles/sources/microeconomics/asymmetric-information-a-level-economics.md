## Asymmetric information is unequal knowledge

A free market is supposed to allocate resources efficiently because buyers and sellers both know what they are trading. **Asymmetric information** breaks that assumption: one side of a transaction knows more than the other.

The used-car seller knows whether the gearbox is about to fail; the buyer does not. The job applicant knows how hard they will actually work; the employer does not. The person buying health insurance knows their own lifestyle; the insurer does not. In each case the information gap gives the better-informed party an advantage — and, crucially, it can stop good trades from happening at all.

That is why asymmetric information sits in the market-failure section of the syllabus. It is not merely unfair. It is **inefficient**: deals that would make both parties better off do not take place, because the less-informed side cannot trust what it is being offered. The two ways this plays out have names — **adverse selection** (a problem of hidden quality, before the deal) and **moral hazard** (a problem of hidden behaviour, after the deal).

## The market for lemons

The defining model is George Akerlof's 1970 paper *The Market for "Lemons"*, which won him a share of the Nobel Prize. A "lemon" is American slang for a bad used car, and the model shows how hidden quality can wreck a whole market.

Imagine used cars come in two kinds: good ones and lemons. Sellers know which is which; buyers cannot tell them apart. A buyer who knows half the cars are lemons will only offer a price somewhere in the middle — an average of the good-car and lemon values. But at that average price, the owner of a genuinely good car is being short-changed, so they refuse to sell and keep their car. The good cars withdraw from the market.

Now the cars left for sale are disproportionately lemons. Buyers work this out, lower the price they will offer again, and *more* good cars drop out. The process feeds on itself — the market "unravels". In the extreme, only lemons are left, and the market for good used cars can collapse entirely.

This is **adverse selection**: the information gap distorts *who participates*, selecting in the bad and selecting out the good, before any deal is struck. The same logic explains why the people most likely to buy health insurance are those who know they are high-risk, which pushes premiums up and drives the low-risk out — exactly the lemons mechanism in a different market.

:::

## Moral hazard, insurance, and what fixes the gap

**Moral hazard** is the second failure, and it strikes *after* the contract is signed. It is a change in behaviour because one party no longer bears the full consequences of its actions.

The textbook case is insurance. A driver with fully comprehensive cover may park less carefully or drive a little faster, because the insurer, not the driver, picks up the bill for a prang. The insurer cannot observe this behaviour — that is the hidden information — so it cannot price it perfectly. The 2008 financial crisis is the macro-scale version: banks took enormous risks partly because they expected to be bailed out if the bets went wrong, so they did not bear the full downside.

The good news is that markets and governments develop responses to close the gap. Sellers **signal** quality — a used-car dealer offers a warranty, a graduate displays a degree, a firm builds a brand — because only a genuinely good product can afford to. Buyers **screen** — insurers charge excesses and no-claims discounts so that careful drivers self-identify; lenders demand collateral. Governments mandate disclosure: car-history checks, food labelling, the Financial Conduct Authority's rules on selling financial products, and compulsory MOT certificates all exist to shrink the information asymmetry directly.

Each of these is a real answer to a real failure, and naming the mechanism — signalling, screening, regulation — is what turns a description into analysis.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## Does the gap always need fixing?

The standard answer is that asymmetric information justifies government intervention. The top-band evaluation is more measured.

**Markets often self-correct.** Warranties, brands, reviews, ratings and intermediaries (used-car certification, TripAdvisor, credit scores) emerge precisely because the information gap is profitable to close. Where private solutions work, heavy regulation may add cost without adding much information.

**Intervention has limits and side effects.** Mandatory disclosure only helps if consumers can understand and act on the information — a dense insurance document or a wall of food labels can leave the gap effectively open. And government itself faces an information problem: regulators may know less than the firms they police, raising the risk of regulatory failure.

**Some asymmetry is unavoidable and even useful.** A doctor knowing more than a patient is the whole point of expertise; the answer is to regulate the trust relationship (professional standards, liability), not to pretend the gap can be erased. The policy question is whether the cost of closing the gap is worth the efficiency it recovers.

The evaluation move is to ask whether a private remedy already exists, how well consumers can use mandated information, and whether the regulator is better-informed than the market it is correcting.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## The moves that lift your essay

For **AO3 analysis**, run the lemons unravelling as a chain. "Buyers can't observe quality → offer an average price → good-car owners withdraw → average quality falls → buyers lower offers again → market for good cars unravels → mutually beneficial trades lost → allocative inefficiency." Tying the failure to lost efficient trades, not just to unfairness, is what reaches the higher levels.

For **AO4 evaluation**, distinguish the two failures cleanly and weigh the remedy. "Adverse selection is a pre-contract problem of hidden quality; moral hazard is a post-contract problem of hidden behaviour. Mandatory disclosure can shrink the gap, *but* only if consumers can act on the information, and markets often supply their own signals and screens." Naming signalling, screening and regulation as the responses — and questioning whether each works — is exactly the move the mark scheme rewards.

:::
