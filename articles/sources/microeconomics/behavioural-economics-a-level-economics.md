## People don't behave like the textbook agent

Traditional microeconomics is built on a tidy assumption: people are rational, fully informed, and choose whatever maximises their own utility. It is a powerful starting point — and it is often wrong about how real humans behave.

Behavioural economics is the study of how people *actually* decide. Its founding observation is **bounded rationality**: we intend to choose well, but we are limited by time, information and mental processing power, so we lean on mental shortcuts. Those shortcuts produce *systematic* errors — biases that show up reliably enough to predict and, crucially, to design policy around.

That last point is what turned behavioural economics from a critique into a toolkit. If errors are predictable, you can gently steer choices without forcing anyone — a **nudge**. Here is how the pieces fit.

## From biases to choice architecture

Start with the biases, because the nudges only make sense once you can name what they work on.

**Loss aversion** — people feel a loss roughly more sharply than an equivalent gain, so they cling to what they have. **Anchoring** — the first number we see frames everything after it, which is why a crossed-out "original price" makes the sale price feel cheap. **Herding** — we copy what others appear to be doing, taking the crowd as information. **Present bias** — we over-weight rewards now and under-weight the future, which is why saving for a pension or quitting smoking feels easy to start *tomorrow*. And the quiet giant: **inertia and default options** — most people stick with whatever they are signed up to by default, simply because switching takes effort.

A **nudge** is a change to **choice architecture** — the way options are presented — that steers behaviour while leaving every option open and incentives unchanged. The mechanism is to work *with* a bias rather than against it. The cleanest example: change a default. If joining a pension is opt-out rather than opt-in, inertia and present bias now push people *towards* saving instead of away from it. No one is forced; the architecture just stops working against them.

That is the chain examiners want: identify the bias → identify the choice architecture → show how the nudge redirects the bias → predict the behaviour change. A nudge with no named bias behind it is just a slogan.

We think much less than we think we think. Most of our decisions are made by a fast, automatic system that runs on intuition and bias, and only sometimes do we hand control to slow, deliberate reasoning.

## Thaler, the Nudge Unit, and policy that actually shipped

Behavioural economics is not just theory — it became government practice. Richard Thaler, who with Cass Sunstein popularised nudge theory, won the Nobel Prize in 2017 for showing how psychological factors shape economic decisions. The UK took the idea seriously enough to build an institution around it: the **Behavioural Insights Team**, set up in 2010 and universally known as the **Nudge Unit**.

The flagship example is **pension auto-enrolment**, rolled out in the UK from 2012. Before it, joining a workplace pension was opt-in, and present bias plus inertia meant millions never got round to it. Auto-enrolment flipped the default: you are signed up unless you actively opt out. The same biases that once kept people *out* now keep them *in*, and participation rose dramatically — without removing anyone's right to leave.

A second example is **organ donation**. England moved to an opt-out ("deemed consent") system in 2020: adults are presumed willing to donate unless they register otherwise. Again, the architecture, not the incentive, does the work — most people never change a default either way, so the default itself decides the outcome for the majority.

Both cases show the same lesson: a tiny, cheap change to how a choice is framed can shift behaviour at national scale, precisely *because* people are predictably reluctant to act.

:::

## Nudges are powerful, cheap — and limited

The standard answer presents nudges as a clever, costless win. The top band qualifies it in three ways.

**The effect is bounded because the choice stays open.** A nudge's great virtue — it preserves freedom — is also its limit. People can opt out, ignore the default or override the framing, so a nudge moves the average but rarely shifts the determined. For behaviours where you need *everyone* to change, a tax or a ban may be the only tool strong enough.

**It can be manipulative.** Choice architecture is never neutral — *some* default always exists — so the question is who sets it and to what end. The same techniques that auto-enrol you into a pension can auto-enrol you into a subscription you forget to cancel. Critics call this the paternalism problem: a nudge assumes the architect knows better than the chooser.

**It does not fix the underlying cause.** A nudge steers a decision without changing prices, information or income. Where the real barrier is poverty rather than psychology, nudging someone towards a healthier basket they cannot afford achieves little.

So the genuine evaluation is comparative, not dismissive. A nudge is the right tool when the barrier is a bias, the cost of acting is mainly effort, and you want to preserve choice. It is the wrong tool when the barrier is price or information, when partial compliance is not enough, or when the deciding default is set by someone whose interests differ from the chooser's. Naming *which* of those conditions holds in the question is the evaluation.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## The two-mark moves that lift your essay

For **AO3 analysis**, always pair a named bias with the nudge that exploits it. "Present bias and inertia mean people under-save → switching pensions to an opt-out default → the same inertia now keeps them enrolled → saving rises with no loss of choice." A list of biases with no mechanism is AO1 recall; the linked chain is the analysis mark.

For **AO4 evaluation**, make the comparison explicit. "A nudge is preferable to a ban *because* it preserves freedom of choice and is low-cost, but its effect is smaller and easier to ignore — so where full compliance is essential, a tax or regulation may be more effective." Saying what the choice depends *on* beats a generic "it depends".

The habit to drill: never write "this is a nudge" without naming the bias it works on and the option it leaves open. That sentence carries the application and the evaluation at once.
