## The extract is evidence, not decoration

A data response question gives you a context — a short extract of text, a table of figures, sometimes a chart — and then asks you to analyse and evaluate using it. The extract is not background reading you skim before the "real" question. It *is* the question. The marks attached to it are AO2 application marks, and AO2 is the one objective a generic, memorised essay can never reach.

Students go wrong in two opposite directions. Some ignore the extract entirely and write the textbook answer they revised, which forfeits every application mark on offer. Others over-correct and copy the extract back out, summarising what it says without doing anything with it. Neither earns the marks, because both miss what the extract is for: it is evidence to be selected, interpreted, and welded onto your analysis.

## Select, interpret, attach

Using the extract well is a three-step habit, and it is the same every time.

**Select.** Read the extract with the question in mind and underline the two or three details that actually bear on it — a figure, a direction of change, a named policy or firm. You are not trying to use everything; you are choosing the evidence that will carry your argument.

**Interpret.** A raw number is not application. "Sales fell 12%" is a fact lifted from the page. "The 12% fall in sales suggests demand was more sensitive to the price rise than the firm expected" is interpretation — you have told the examiner what the figure *means* for the economics.

**Attach.** Bolt the interpreted figure onto a chain of reasoning so it does analytical work. The 12% fall becomes the evidence for an elasticity judgement, which feeds a revenue prediction, which feeds the firm's pricing decision. Now the data is driving the analysis rather than sitting beside it.

Select, interpret, attach. Do that with two or three details and your application marks look after themselves.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Watch the numbers" color="blue" icon="scale"

## Read the figures the right way round

A specific data response trap is the misread calculation. Extracts often invite a quick computation — a percentage change, a price elasticity, a real-terms figure — and getting it backwards quietly undermines the whole answer.

The classic is elasticity. If the extract shows quantity demanded falling 8% after a 20% price rise, price elasticity of demand is the percentage change in quantity divided by the percentage change in price: 8 ÷ 20 = 0.4. That is below one, so demand is price *inelastic*. A student who eyeballs "demand fell, so it must be elastic" reaches the opposite, wrong conclusion and builds the rest of the answer on sand.

The discipline is to do the small calculation explicitly, even when the question doesn't demand a number, and to sanity-check the direction: a proportionally smaller quantity response than price response is always inelastic. Showing the working also protects you — an examiner can credit a correct method even if you slip on the arithmetic.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Quoting well" color="green" icon="globe"

## Quote little, use it hard

There is a difference between quoting and copying, and the mark scheme cares about it.

Copying is reproducing a sentence from the extract to fill space: it shows you read the passage and nothing more. Quoting is lifting a short, specific detail — a figure, a phrase, a date — *because you are about to do something with it*. The test is simple: if you could delete the quoted detail and the sentence around it would say exactly the same thing, the quote was decoration. If deleting it removes the evidence your point rests on, it was working.

So quote little and use it hard. One precise figure that anchors a chain of reasoning is worth more than three sentences transcribed from the extract, and it costs a fraction of the time.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## The data response habit

Mine the extract for evidence, interpret each figure, and let the data steer the analysis.

For **AO2**, the rule is select-interpret-attach: choose the details that matter, say what each implies, and bolt it onto a chain. For accuracy, do the small calculations explicitly and check the direction — inelastic when quantity moves proportionally less than price, and the reverse. For quoting, lift little and use it hard: a single figure that carries an argument beats a paragraph copied out. An answer where every piece of data is doing a job is the one that collects the application marks a generic essay leaves behind.

:::
