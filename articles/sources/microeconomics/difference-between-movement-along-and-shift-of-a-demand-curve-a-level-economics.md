::: section eyebrow="The confusion" color="green" icon="bulb"

## The distinction the whole of Theme 1 hangs on

If you take one rule from microeconomics into the exam, take this one. A change in the good's **own price** causes a **movement along** the demand curve. A change in **anything else** causes a **shift of** the whole curve.

The two even have different names, and examiners use them precisely:

- **Movement along** = a **change in quantity demanded**.
- **Shift of the curve** = a **change in demand**.

Get this backwards and the damage compounds. You draw the wrong line on the diagram, attach the wrong cause to it, and every step of analysis that follows is built on a misdrawn first move. It is the most common error in Theme 1 precisely because the language is so close — "quantity demanded" versus "demand" — that students treat them as synonyms. They are not.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The mechanism" color="purple" icon="target"

## Why own-price moves you along, and everything else shifts the curve

The demand curve is drawn under **ceteris paribus** — "other things held equal". That phrase is the whole secret. The curve already shows what happens to quantity demanded as the good's *own* price changes: that relationship is *baked into the line*. So when only the own price changes, you are reading off a point that was always on the curve — you **slide along** it. Price up, quantity demanded down; price down, quantity demanded up. The curve does not move because nothing it was holding equal has changed.

Now suppose one of those held-equal things *does* change — income, tastes, the price of a substitute. The original curve was drawn for the old value of that condition, so it is no longer valid. At *every* price, the quantity people want is different. You have to draw a **new curve**: that is a shift. More wanted at every price shifts it **right**; less wanted at every price shifts it **left**.

The non-price conditions are worth memorising as **PIRATES**: **P**opulation, **I**ncome, **R**elated goods (substitutes and complements), **A**dvertising, **T**astes, **E**xpectations, **S**easons. Any one of them moving shifts the whole curve. The good's own price is *not* on that list — because it is the axis, and a change in it is a movement, not a shift.

The diagnostic question is a single sentence: *did the good's own price change, and nothing else?* If yes, movement along. If something other than its own price changed, shift.

::: section eyebrow="A worked example" color="blue" icon="globe"

## One event can be a movement in one market and a shift in another

Take a fall in the price of petrol. In the **petrol market**, its own price has changed, so consumers slide *down* the petrol demand curve — a movement along, a change in quantity demanded. Straightforward.

But petrol is a **complement** to large, thirsty cars. In the market for large cars, the relevant change is the price of a *related good* — a non-price condition for that market. So the demand curve for large cars **shifts right**: at every car price, cheaper running costs make people want more of them.

The same real-world event — a petrol price fall — is a *movement* in one market and a *shift* in another, depending on whose own price actually moved. Spotting that is exactly the AO3 analysis examiners reward: naming the own-price effect (movement) and the cross-market effect (shift) as two distinct mechanisms rather than blurring them into one.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## Use the exact phrase, and check whose own price moved

The marks are in the precision of the language. Use "change in **quantity demanded**" for a movement along and "change in **demand**" for a shift — examiners check for exactly these phrases, because they signal you understand the difference rather than just describing a diagram.

The reliable AO2 move before you draw anything: ask whether the trigger is the good's *own* price. If it is, and nothing else changed, it is a movement along the curve. If the trigger is income, tastes, the price of a substitute or complement, advertising, population, expectations or seasons, it is a shift. Stating that test explicitly — "because the change is in [non-price condition], the demand curve shifts rather than a movement along it" — earns the application mark and stops the rest of the answer going wrong.

:::
