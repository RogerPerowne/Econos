## A fast, labelled diagram beats a slow, beautiful one

In the exam, a diagram is a tool, and like any tool it is only worth the job it does. Two failures cost marks at opposite ends. Some students draw a careful, shaded masterpiece and then discover they have no time left to explain it. Others, panicking about the clock, throw down an unlabelled scribble that the examiner cannot read and so cannot credit.

The skill is not artistry. It is a repeatable stroke order that produces a complete, labelled diagram quickly — fast enough that the diagram is the cheap part of the answer and the explanation is where you spend your time. The monopoly diagram is the one most worth drilling, because it has four curves and goes wrong the moment you draw them in the wrong order. Get the order fixed and it takes about 90 seconds and practically labels itself.

This article describes that order in words. There is no picture here on purpose — the point is to internalise the sequence so your hand does it automatically under pressure.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The sequence" color="purple" icon="target"

## The 90-second order of strokes

Always draw the curves in the same order. The order is what makes it fast.

**1. Axes (about 5 seconds).** Vertical axis labelled "Costs and revenue (£)", horizontal axis labelled "Output (Q)". Big enough to fit four curves and the profit box without crowding.

**2. The AR / demand curve (about 10 seconds).** A single downward-sloping straight line from high on the vertical axis to the horizontal axis. Label it "AR = D". This is the demand the monopolist faces — the whole market.

**3. The MR curve (about 10 seconds).** From the same point on the vertical axis, twice as steep, hitting the horizontal axis exactly halfway along where AR hits it. Label it "MR". Drawing MR straight after AR, from the same start point, is what keeps it in the right place.

**4. The MC and AC curves (about 20 seconds).** A U-shaped AC, then an MC that cuts AC at its lowest point from below. Label both. Drawing AC first and threading MC through its minimum stops the two from drifting apart.

**5. Find the output (about 10 seconds).** Mark the point where MC crosses MR. Drop a vertical line down to the axis: that is profit-maximising output, Qm.

**6. Read off price and cost (about 15 seconds).** From Qm, go *up* to the AR curve and across to the axis — that is the price, Pm. From Qm, go to the AC curve and across — that is average cost at that output. The gap between Pm and AC, across Qm, is the supernormal profit box. Shade it lightly.

That is the whole diagram, complete and labelled, in roughly 90 seconds.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The one that trips people up" color="blue" icon="scale"

## Why MC = MR fixes output, but AR fixes price

The most common error in this diagram is reading the price off the wrong curve. Output and price come from two different lines, and they have to be read in the right order.

**Output** is set where marginal cost equals marginal revenue. A monopolist maximises profit by producing every unit whose marginal revenue covers its marginal cost and no more — so the quantity is fixed at the MC = MR crossing. That is the only thing that point tells you.

**Price** is then whatever consumers will pay for that quantity — and that is read up to the AR (demand) curve, never off the MR line. MR sits below AR because the monopolist must cut price on *all* units to sell one more, so marginal revenue is always less than the price charged. Students who read price off the MC = MR point land it far too low and mangle the profit box.

The rule to memorise: MC = MR gives you the quantity; go straight up to AR for the price. Quantity first, then price, every time.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Make it earn marks" color="green" icon="globe"

## The diagram is the setup, the prose is the score

A finished diagram is worth almost nothing on its own. AO3 analysis marks come from the explanation the diagram supports, so the diagram is the setup and the prose is where you score.

So once it is drawn, walk the reader through it: "The monopolist maximises profit at Qm where MC = MR, charging Pm read up to the demand curve. Because Pm exceeds average cost, the firm earns supernormal profit, shown by the shaded box. Note that Pm sits above MC at this output — price exceeds marginal cost — so the monopoly is allocatively inefficient, producing less and charging more than a competitive market would."

That paragraph is what the 90-second diagram bought you the time to write. Drill the stroke order so the picture is automatic, and spend your minutes on the explanation that actually carries the marks.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## The diagram routine under pressure

Fixed stroke order, full labels, then explain it.

Draw the four curves in the same sequence every time — AR, then MR, then AC, then MC — so each lands in the right place without thinking. Label every curve and axis; an unlabelled diagram the examiner can't read can't be credited. Read quantity off MC = MR and price up to AR, in that order. Then spend your real time on the prose that walks through the mechanism — profit maximisation, the supernormal profit box, the allocative inefficiency. The diagram is cheap; the explanation it enables is what earns the AO3 marks.

:::
