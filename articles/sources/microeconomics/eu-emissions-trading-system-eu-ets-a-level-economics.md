## Cap the pollution, then trade the right to emit

The EU Emissions Trading System, launched in 2005, was the world's first large carbon market. The idea is simple to state and powerful in practice: the EU sets a **cap** on total emissions from the industries it covers, issues **permits** up to that cap, and requires every covered firm to hold one permit for each tonne it emits.

Then it lets firms trade. A firm that can cut emissions cheaply has spare permits to sell; a firm that would find cutting expensive buys permits instead. Out of that trading comes a single **price of carbon** — a number that, in recent years, has traded in the region of roughly €70 to €90 per tonne, far above the near-worthless levels of the scheme's early years.

The contrast to hold in your head: a carbon tax fixes the *price* and lets quantity adjust; the ETS fixes the *quantity* (the cap) and lets the price adjust.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The mechanism" color="blue" icon="target"

## Why trading makes the cap cheap

Pollution is a classic **negative externality**: emitting imposes costs on third parties that the emitter does not pay, so the social marginal cost of production sits above the private cost and the market over-produces. Permits attack this by putting a price on the right to emit.

The least-cost logic is the part examiners reward. Picture two firms. Firm A can cut a tonne of emissions for £20; Firm B can only cut a tonne for £90. If the regulator simply ordered both to cut equally, B's expensive cuts would be wasted effort. Under trading, with a permit price of, say, £70: A cuts a lot (each cut below £70 is worth making) and sells its spare permits; B buys permits rather than making cuts that cost more than £70. Both end up abating until their **marginal abatement cost equals the permit price** — so the cost of cutting is equalised across the system, and the fixed total cap is hit at the lowest possible total cost.

The chain: cap set → permit supply fixed → firms demand permits to cover emissions → market price emerges → firms abate up to that price → cheapest cuts happen first → cap met efficiently. Tighten the cap over time and the price rises, sharpening the incentive to invest in clean technology.

## The EU ETS in practice

For its first decade the EU ETS was criticised because too many permits were issued, the cap was loose, and the carbon price collapsed to levels too low to change behaviour. The lesson — and the exam point — is that a cap-and-trade scheme is only as effective as its cap is tight.

Reforms tightened the supply of permits, and the carbon price climbed substantially, trading in recent years in the region of roughly €70 to €90 per tonne. At that level the price genuinely shapes decisions: it raises the running cost of high-carbon generation relative to cleaner alternatives and strengthens the business case for investing in abatement, because every tonne avoided is a permit a firm no longer has to buy.

Citing the EU ETS lets you make a sharper argument than "tax pollution." You can show that the policy fixes the environmental outcome (the cap guarantees the emissions ceiling) while letting the market discover the cheapest way to hit it, and that the carbon price is an endogenous result of supply and demand in the permit market — not a number a minister picked.

:::

Where private and social costs diverge, making the producer face the full social cost of an activity can realign self-interest with the common good — whether through a tax or, as here, a tradable price on the right to pollute.

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## What the ETS's success depends on

**The cap has to bite.** If permits are over-allocated, the price collapses and the scheme does nothing — exactly the EU ETS's early failing. The environmental outcome is only as good as the tightness of the cap and the credibility that it will keep tightening.

**Price volatility undermines investment.** Because the price floats, it can swing with the business cycle and energy markets. Firms deciding whether to invest in long-lived clean technology want a predictable carbon price; a volatile one weakens the very investment incentive the scheme is meant to create. This is where a carbon *tax*, which fixes the price, can be argued to be superior.

**Coverage and leakage.** The scheme covers some sectors and not others, and if high-carbon production simply relocates to regions without a carbon price — carbon leakage — global emissions need not fall. The externality is corrected only within the scheme's borders.

The evaluation move is to weigh certainty of quantity (the cap's strength) against certainty of price (a tax's strength), and to say which matters more given the volatility and leakage risks.

:::

## Quantity-fixed, price-floating — say it

For **AO1/AO2**, get the design right: cap-and-trade since 2005, a cap on total emissions, tradable permits, a market-set carbon price recently around €70–€90 per tonne.

For **AO3 analysis**, run the least-cost chain: trading equalises marginal abatement cost at the permit price, so the cap is met where cuts are cheapest. For **AO4 evaluation**, frame the central trade-off: cap-and-trade fixes the emissions outcome but lets the price swing, whereas a carbon tax fixes the price but leaves the quantity uncertain — and the early EU ETS shows a loose cap can neuter the whole scheme. Naming that trade-off is the top-band move.
