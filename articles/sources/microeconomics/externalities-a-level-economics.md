faq:
  - q: "What is a positive externality?"
    a: "A positive externality is a benefit imposed on a third party who is not part of the market transaction. The market under-provides the good because consumers and producers don't capture the full social benefit — they only see the private benefit. Classic A-level examples: vaccination (other people don't catch the disease either — herd immunity), education (a more skilled workforce raises everyone's productivity), apprenticeship training (the worker takes the skills to other firms). Policy response: subsidies, state provision, mandatory provision."
  - q: "What is a negative externality?"
    a: "A negative externality is a cost imposed on a third party who is not part of the market transaction. The market over-produces the good because the producer pays only the private cost, not the full social cost. Classic A-level examples: factory pollution (downstream residents pay a health cost the factory ignores), passive smoking, congestion (your car slows everyone else's). Policy response: Pigouvian taxes, regulation, tradable pollution permits, property rights enforcement."
  - q: "What's the difference between consumption and production externalities?"
    a: "Production externalities arise from how a good is MADE — a factory's pollution, or an apprenticeship programme's skills spillover. They shift the MSC (marginal social cost) curve away from MPC. Consumption externalities arise from how a good is USED — vaccination (positive, since it protects others) or passive smoking (negative). They shift MSB away from MPB. The DIRECTION of the welfare loss depends on whether the externality is positive or negative; the LAYER of the diagram (cost vs benefit) depends on whether it's production or consumption."
  - q: "Why do Pigouvian taxes work in theory but struggle in practice?"
    a: "In theory, a tax equal to the marginal external cost perfectly internalises the externality — the polluter now pays the full social cost, and produces exactly the socially optimal quantity. In practice: (1) measuring the marginal external cost is hard (what's the £/tonne damage of CO₂?), (2) the politically achievable rate is usually below the optimal rate, (3) elasticity matters — if demand is very inelastic (cigarettes), the tax barely changes behaviour, (4) cross-border externalities (climate) need cross-border taxes that don't exist. The UK SDIL is a partial success because manufacturers reformulated; the UK carbon price is a partial failure because it's set well below the social cost of carbon."

## Externalities are costs the market ignores

An **externality** is a cost or benefit imposed on a third party who is not part of the market transaction. The market price reflects only the PRIVATE cost and benefit of the buyer and seller, so the market ignores externalities — and over- or under-produces accordingly.

The four-type matrix is the structural backbone of every externality essay:

|  | **Consumption (affects users)** | **Production (affects making)** |
|---|---|---|
| **Negative** | Passive smoking, alcohol-fuelled crime | Pollution, traffic congestion |
| **Positive** | Vaccination, education, public transport use | Apprenticeship skills, R&D spillovers |

Each type has its own diagram (MSB vs MPB or MSC vs MPC, shifted in opposite directions for positive vs negative). The mark scheme wants the correct ONE drawn — not just "an externality diagram".

:::

::: section eyebrow="The diagram every essay needs" color="blue" icon="target"

## MSC, MPC, and the deadweight triangle

The canonical negative-production-externality diagram (e.g. factory pollution):

- **Demand curve D** = marginal private benefit (MPB) = marginal social benefit (MSB) — no consumption externality assumed.
- **Supply curve S** = marginal private cost (MPC) — the firm's cost.
- **MSC** sits ABOVE MPC by the value of the external cost — the curve society actually faces.
- **Market equilibrium**: where D meets MPC. Quantity Q* (too high).
- **Social optimum**: where MSB meets MSC. Quantity Q** (lower).
- **Welfare loss**: the triangle between MSC and MSB, from Q** to Q* — society's loss from over-producing.

For positive consumption externalities (vaccination), the diagram flips: MSB sits ABOVE MPB by the external benefit; market equilibrium is too low; the welfare loss is the triangle from Q* (market) to Q** (optimum) above MPB.

Drawing the diagram correctly — with axes labelled, curves named, equilibria marked, and the deadweight triangle shaded — earns roughly half the marks on a typical question. Don't skip it.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Negative externalities — the SDIL case study" color="purple" icon="scale"

## The Soft Drinks Industry Levy did what Pigou predicted

The UK's Soft Drinks Industry Levy (SDIL, 2018) applied a £-per-litre tax on producers based on sugar content (5g/100ml threshold). The mechanism in a textbook Pigouvian frame:

- Sugary drinks have a negative consumption externality (long-run health costs of obesity and diabetes that fall on the NHS, not the consumer).
- The market under-prices that externality — sugary drinks were "too cheap" relative to the social cost.
- SDIL imposed a tax broadly equal to the per-litre external cost (roughly 18p / 24p per litre depending on the sugar tier).
- Manufacturers had a choice: pass the tax through to consumers (consumers bear the cost) or reformulate to fall below the threshold (manufacturers absorb the cost, consumers get less sugar).
- Most chose to reformulate. Coca-Cola Zero became dominant. Lucozade reformulated. Many brands cut sugar.

Result: ~30% reduction in sugar from soft drinks within three years. NHS modelled savings from reduced obesity-related illness over decades.

The textbook would judge SDIL successful precisely because it internalised the externality and the welfare-loss triangle shrank. The strongest essays name SDIL specifically.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Positive externalities — the vaccination case" color="pink" icon="globe"

## Why vaccination is the canonical positive consumption externality

When one person is vaccinated against measles, they receive the private benefit (immunity). But the rest of the community also benefits — reduced transmission, herd immunity protecting unvaccinated infants and immunocompromised adults. The marginal social benefit exceeds the marginal private benefit by the value of that spillover.

Free market outcome: each individual chooses vaccination based only on their personal benefit. Quantity Q* (too low). The welfare gain from extra vaccinations (between Q* and the social optimum Q**) is the deadweight loss the market never captures.

Policy responses that A-level mark schemes recognise:
- **Subsidy** — paying providers per vaccination delivered. NHS does this implicitly.
- **State provision** — making vaccination free at the point of use.
- **Mandatory provision** — e.g. compulsory MMR for school entry in some US states.
- **Information campaigns** — closing the information-gap externality alongside the externality itself.

The UK's universal-vaccination-from-childhood model is essentially full state provision — the externality is internalised by treating vaccination as a public-funded service.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Climate change — the externality that breaks the textbook" color="green" icon="check"

## Why carbon pricing is partial at best

Climate change is the canonical externality of production at a global scale. A tonne of CO₂ emitted in Manchester does damage in Bangladesh, Antarctica, and the next century. The marginal external cost is large; the marginal private cost paid by the emitter is small.

The Pigouvian prescription: a carbon tax equal to the social cost of carbon (~£100–£200/tCO₂ in central estimates) internalises the externality.

In practice:
- The UK Emissions Trading Scheme prices carbon at ~£40/tCO₂ in 2024 — well below the optimal rate.
- A higher rate would face fierce political opposition and cause carbon LEAKAGE (firms move production to countries with no carbon price).
- The Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM, EU 2026+) is a partial fix — but only for imports from carbon-light countries.

The strongest A-level answer notes that climate externalities expose the LIMITS of Pigouvian pricing: cross-border externalities need cross-border coordination, and the politically achievable rate sits below the theoretically optimal rate.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Exam edge" color="green" icon="cap"

## What the mark scheme actually rewards

- **Name the four-type matrix.** Positive/negative × consumption/production. Pick the right cell for the case in the question.
- **Draw the right diagram.** MSC/MPC for production externalities, MSB/MPB for consumption. Shade the deadweight triangle.
- **Match policy to externality.** Pigouvian tax for negative externalities (SDIL, carbon). Subsidy for positive (vaccination, education). Don't mix them.
- **Use a UK case study.** SDIL for the success story; UK carbon pricing for the partial-failure story. Both score AO2 marks immediately.
- **Evaluate the elasticity catch.** A tax on inelastic demand barely changes behaviour. A subsidy that consumers don't notice doesn't lift uptake. Always name PED when discussing the impact of corrective taxes.

:::
