## Cost-benefit analysis tries to price the unpriced

When a private firm decides whether to invest, it compares its own revenues and costs. When a government appraises a project like HS2, it is supposed to go wider: count the **social** costs and benefits, including effects that never show up in anyone's accounts — carbon emissions, noise, time saved by passengers, the boost a city might get from being better connected.

Cost-benefit analysis (CBA) is the framework for doing this. Analysts forecast every cost and benefit over the project's life, convert future pounds into present value by discounting, and divide present-value benefits by present-value costs to get a **benefit-cost ratio (BCR)**. A BCR above 1 means the project pays for itself on the figures used; below 1 means it does not.

The trap is to treat the BCR as objective. The number is only as good as the values fed into it, and the most important values — the worth of a business traveller's hour, the size of any "agglomeration" boost to the north — are exactly the ones reasonable people dispute.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The mechanism · how the appraisal moves" color="purple" icon="target"

## Why the same project gets a different verdict over time

Follow the chain. A railway's costs are largely fixed and incurred up front: land, tunnelling, track, rolling stock. Its benefits are spread over decades: faster journeys, extra capacity that relieves crowding on existing lines, possible regional rebalancing.

Two forces then push the BCR around. First, **costs rise as a project matures** — early estimates are made before the engineering is fully known, and big civil-engineering schemes reliably overrun. Add general inflation in construction and the denominator grows. Second, **benefits get re-estimated**: if appraisers conclude that travellers can work productively on a train (so time "saved" is worth less than assumed), or that demand will be lower than forecast, the numerator shrinks.

A bigger denominator and a smaller numerator mean the ratio can slide from comfortably above 1 to marginal or below — without the railway itself changing. That is the analytical heart of the HS2 story: rising cost estimates colliding with contested benefit assumptions. The opportunity-cost framing matters too — every billion committed to HS2 is a billion not spent on other transport, health or education projects, so the relevant comparison is HS2 against the next-best use of the funds, not HS2 against nothing.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Real world example" color="blue" icon="globe"

## HS2: costs up, scope down

HS2 was conceived as a Y-shaped network linking London to Birmingham and then on to Manchester and Leeds, to add rail capacity and cut journey times. Over the project's life the estimated cost rose substantially above early figures as designs were detailed and inflation took hold, while the appraised benefits came under sustained scrutiny.

The political-economy response was rescoping. The eastern leg to Leeds was scaled back, and in 2023 the government cancelled the Manchester leg, leaving the core London–Birmingham section to proceed. Each cut changed the economics in a specific way: dropping the northern legs removed both their costs *and* a large share of the connectivity and regional-rebalancing benefits that justified the whole network — so cutting the project does not necessarily improve the remaining BCR, because benefits and costs are not independent.

The case illustrates two textbook ideas at once. **Optimism bias**: early cost estimates were too low and timescales too short, exactly the pattern the Treasury's Green Book tells appraisers to correct for with explicit uplifts. **The sunk-cost trap**: with billions already committed, the public debate kept reaching for "we can't waste what we've spent", when the only economically relevant question for any remaining phase is whether its *future* benefits beat its *future* costs.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## CBA is contestable, but the alternative is worse

A strong answer resists both "CBA proves HS2 was a mistake" and "CBA proves it was worth it".

**The numbers depend on assumptions you can argue about.** The value of time savings, the discount rate, the size of agglomeration effects and the carbon price all swing the BCR. Two honest analysts can reach opposite conclusions from the same project. That is a reason to treat the headline ratio with caution — not a reason to abandon structured appraisal, because the alternative is ministers picking projects on instinct and pork-barrel politics.

**Wider benefits are real but hard to bank.** Capacity relief on the existing network and long-run regional rebalancing are genuine potential gains, but they are uncertain and arrive late, so discounting shrinks them. Whether they materialise depends on complementary policy — without housing, local transport and skills, a fast train to a left-behind city may not regenerate it.

**The decision is political as well as economic.** Governments face electoral cycles shorter than a railway's payback, an incentive to announce projects and a different incentive to cut them when budgets tighten. That political economy, as much as the CBA, explains why HS2 was launched grand and delivered partial.

The evaluative move: say what the verdict depends ON — the contested benefit values, the opportunity cost of the funds, and whether complementary policy lets the wider benefits arrive.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## Treat the BCR as an argument, not an answer

For **AO3 analysis**, show the appraisal as a chain: forecast social costs and benefits → discount to present value → divide to get a BCR → compare against alternatives via opportunity cost. Note explicitly why the ratio moved — rising cost estimates, falling benefit assumptions — rather than asserting the project "didn't add up".

For **AO4 evaluation**, name the contestable assumptions (value of time, discount rate, agglomeration) and apply the sunk-cost rule correctly: past spending is irrelevant to whether a remaining phase should proceed. Top-band answers also flag the political economy — optimism bias and electoral incentives — as reasons appraisal and delivery diverge.

:::
