## Two elasticities, two questions

Price elasticity of demand (PED) asks one question: how does demand respond to a good's *own* price? Income and cross elasticity ask two different ones.

**Income elasticity of demand (YED)** asks how demand responds to a change in consumer income. The formula is the percentage change in quantity demanded divided by the percentage change in income.

**Cross elasticity of demand (XED)** asks how demand for one good responds to a change in the price of *another* good. The formula is the percentage change in demand for good A divided by the percentage change in the price of good B.

The single most important habit on this topic is to **read the sign before the size**. With YED and XED the plus or minus tells you the *type* of good or the *relationship* between two goods — which is usually what the question is really asking. The number after the sign only tells you how strong the effect is. Get the sign the wrong way round and the whole answer points in the opposite direction.

## Normal, inferior, luxury — all set by the sign and size

Income elasticity sorts every good into a category, and you read the category straight off the number.

A **positive YED** means demand rises as income rises — a **normal good**. Most things are normal goods. Within them, two cases matter. If YED is between 0 and 1, demand rises with income but less than proportionately: a **necessity** (food, household basics). If YED is greater than 1, demand rises faster than income: an income-elastic **luxury** (foreign holidays, restaurant meals, premium cars).

A **negative YED** means demand *falls* as income rises — an **inferior good**. As people get richer they buy less of it, trading up to something better. Supermarket value ranges, bus travel and instant noodles are classic examples: when incomes rise, consumers switch to branded products, cars and fresh food.

The exam payoff is in the economic cycle. In a boom, demand for luxuries (high positive YED) surges while demand for inferior goods falls; in a recession the pattern flips, and the cheapest options grow as households trade down. A firm that knows the YED of its product knows how the cycle will treat its sales — and can plan capacity, stock and pricing around it.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The big picture · XED" color="blue" icon="globe"

## Substitutes, complements, and the price of a rival's product

Cross elasticity captures the relationship between two goods, and again the sign does the heavy lifting.

A **positive XED** means a rise in good B's price *increases* demand for good A — they are **substitutes**. When the price of Pepsi rises, demand for Coca-Cola rises, so their XED is positive. The larger the positive number, the closer the substitutes.

A **negative XED** means a rise in good B's price *reduces* demand for good A — they are **complements**, bought together. When the price of petrol rises, demand for large petrol cars falls; printers and ink cartridges, game consoles and games, strawberries and cream all show negative XED. The more negative the number, the stronger the complementary link.

An XED near **zero** means the goods are unrelated — a change in one's price has no real effect on the other.

Firms live by these numbers. A supermarket setting the price of own-brand cola watches the XED with branded cola (substitutes). A games-console maker prices the hardware cheaply, even at a loss, because of the strong negative XED with the games it then sells (complements) — the razor-and-blades strategy. Knowing the XED tells a firm which rivals' price moves it must react to, and which products to bundle.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## What the elasticity numbers don't tell you

YED and XED are powerful planning tools, but the top-band evaluation knows their limits.

**Elasticities are not fixed.** A good's YED can change as incomes rise across a society — a product that is a luxury in a poor economy becomes a necessity in a rich one, and an inferior good in between. Estimates based on past data may not hold as the economy develops.

**Classification depends on the consumer.** The same good can be normal for one household and inferior for another, depending on their income level. A national YED figure averages over very different responses, so policy or pricing built on it can mislead for particular groups.

**Other factors move at the same time.** YED and XED assume *ceteris paribus* — everything else held constant. In reality, when incomes change so do tastes, prices, advertising and the availability of substitutes. Isolating the pure income or cross effect from real-world data is hard, so the measured number is an estimate, not a law.

The evaluation move is to use the elasticity to make a prediction, then say what would make that prediction wrong — a shift in incomes, a change in tastes, or a new substitute entering the market.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## The moves that lift your essay

For **AO2 application and AO3 analysis**, always state the sign, classify the good or relationship, then use it. "Holidays have a YED well above 1, so they are an income-elastic luxury; in a recession falling real incomes cause a more-than-proportionate fall in demand, hitting travel firms hard." That single sentence does the calculation, the classification and the consequence in one chain.

For **AO4 evaluation**, attack the assumption rather than the arithmetic. "This relies on the YED estimate staying stable and on *ceteris paribus*, *but* as incomes rise the good may shift from luxury toward necessity, weakening the prediction." Naming inferior, normal and luxury goods precisely, and using the XED sign to identify substitutes and complements correctly, is exactly what the mark scheme rewards.

:::
