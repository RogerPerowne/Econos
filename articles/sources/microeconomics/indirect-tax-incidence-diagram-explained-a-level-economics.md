## An indirect tax shifts supply, not demand

Start with a normal market: demand sloping down, supply sloping up, equilibrium at price P1 and quantity Q1. Label the axes **price (P)** and **quantity (Q)**.

An indirect tax — VAT, fuel duty, the soft drinks levy — is a cost the *seller* must hand over. It raises the cost of supplying every unit, so the **supply curve shifts vertically upward** to a new curve, S + tax. The vertical distance between the old and new supply curves equals the tax per unit. Critically, **demand does not move** — buyers' willingness to pay hasn't changed, only the price they face.

For a **specific** (per-unit) tax the two supply curves are parallel — a constant vertical gap. For an **ad valorem** (percentage) tax they fan apart, because a percentage of a higher price is a bigger absolute amount. That fanning is the one visual difference between the two tax types.

::: section eyebrow="How to read the burden" color="blue" icon="target"

## Find the new equilibrium, then split the gap

Read the new equilibrium first: where **S + tax** crosses demand. Quantity falls from Q1 to Q2, and the price consumers pay rises from P1 to a new, higher price — but by *less than the full tax*, because the producer absorbs part of it.

Now split the tax. Drop a line down from the new quantity to the original supply curve: that tells you the price the producer actually keeps after handing the tax to the government. The tax per unit is the vertical gap between that producer price and the consumer price.

- **Consumer incidence** is the part of that gap **above** the original price P1 — the extra the buyer now pays.
- **Producer incidence** is the part **below** P1 — the drop in what the seller nets.

Shade the two rectangles (each is its share of the tax × the new quantity Q2). Stacked together they form the **government revenue box**: total tax per unit × Q2. Labelling those three areas — consumer burden, producer burden, total revenue — is exactly what the application marks reward.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What decides the split" color="purple" icon="scale"

## Elasticity decides who really pays

The diagram doesn't split the burden evenly — relative **elasticity** does, and this is the analysis examiners are looking for.

The **more inelastic side of the market bears the larger share of the tax**. If demand is inelastic (cigarettes, petrol), buyers keep buying even as price rises, so the seller can pass most of the tax through — the consumer rectangle dominates. If demand is elastic, buyers walk away when price rises, so the seller has to absorb most of the tax to keep selling — the producer rectangle dominates.

The chain to write out: tax imposed → supply shifts up by the tax → price rises but quantity falls → *how far* price rises versus quantity falls depends on PED → an inelastic demand curve means price rises a lot and quantity falls little, so incidence lands on consumers. Drawing a steep demand curve versus a flat one and showing the split change is the cleanest way to demonstrate this on paper.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Common mistakes" color="pink" icon="globe"

## Where the diagram goes wrong

- **Shifting demand instead of supply.** The tax is a seller's cost. Move supply, every time.
- **Treating the whole price rise as the tax.** Price rises by *less* than the tax, because the producer absorbs part of it. The full tax is the gap between the consumer price and the producer's net price, not between the two equilibrium prices.
- **Forgetting quantity falls.** A tax shrinks the market to Q2. Welfare analysis (the deadweight loss triangle) hangs off that fall, so don't draw the new equilibrium at the old quantity.
- **Confusing specific and ad valorem.** Parallel shift for a per-unit tax; fanning shift for a percentage tax.
- **Leaving the revenue box unlabelled.** Government revenue = tax per unit × Q2. It's a free mark if you shade and label it.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Earning the diagram marks" color="green" icon="cap"

## The checklist examiners mark against

- **Shift supply** up by the tax (parallel for specific, fanning for ad valorem) — never demand.
- **Mark the new equilibrium** with a higher consumer price *and* a lower quantity Q2.
- **Split the burden**: consumer share above the old price, producer share below it.
- **Shade and label the revenue box** — tax per unit × Q2.
- **Reference elasticity** to explain *why* the split falls where it does. The inelastic side pays more.

Nail those and you have an AO2/AO3 answer, not just a labelled picture.

:::
