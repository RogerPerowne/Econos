faq:
  - q: "What is market failure in A-level economics?"
    a: "Market failure is the misallocation of resources by a free market — the market produces an outcome that is not allocatively efficient. The free market either over-produces (e.g. cigarettes — too much output relative to the social optimum, given health costs) or under-produces (e.g. vaccinations — too little output, given the herd-immunity benefit). Either way, total welfare is lower than it could be. Government intervention is justified WHEN it can correct the failure without introducing larger problems (government failure)."
  - q: "What are the four main causes of market failure?"
    a: "Externalities (the market ignores third-party costs or benefits — pollution, vaccination, education). Public goods (non-rival, non-excludable goods like lighthouses or street lighting that the market under-provides because of the free-rider problem). Information gaps (asymmetric information — second-hand cars, health insurance, financial products). Market power (monopoly raises prices above marginal cost; producer collusion reduces output). Some specs add inequalities in income / wealth and immobility of factors of production as additional failures."
  - q: "What's the difference between complete and partial market failure?"
    a: "Complete market failure means a missing market — no provision exists at all (the classic example is lighthouses before government provision: privately, no firm could profit because they couldn't exclude ships from the light). Partial market failure means the market provides the good, but in the wrong quantity (cigarettes — provided in excess; vaccinations — provided in deficit). Most A-level questions concern partial failure; complete failures are rarer."
  - q: "What is government failure?"
    a: "Government failure occurs when intervention to correct market failure results in a NET welfare loss — the cure is worse than the disease. Causes include: inadequate information (policymakers don't know the right tax rate), unintended consequences (smoking ban displaces smoking to less regulated venues), administrative costs (the £1bn programme costs £200m to administer), conflicting political objectives, and regulatory capture. The classic A* move is to evaluate proposed market-failure interventions for the risk of government failure as well."

## Market failure is the price mechanism getting it wrong

A free market with no government produces the price and quantity where private demand meets private supply. Most of the time, that's allocatively efficient — price equals marginal cost, and resources flow to their most-valued use.

**Market failure** is what happens when it doesn't. The market produces either too much (negative externalities, monopoly) or too little (positive externalities, public goods) compared to the **social optimum**. The gap is a welfare loss — the deadweight triangle every micro diagram shows.

Four main causes:

- **Externalities** — third-party effects the market ignores (pollution, vaccination, education spillovers).
- **Public goods** — non-rival, non-excludable goods that no firm will supply (lighthouses, national defence, street lighting).
- **Information gaps** — asymmetric information distorts decisions (used cars, health insurance, financial products).
- **Market power** — monopolies and cartels raise price and restrict output below the efficient level.

A complete market-failure answer names which cause is at work, draws the welfare-loss diagram, and proposes (then evaluates) the right intervention.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Externalities — the most-examined cause" color="blue" icon="target"

## Why MSC ≠ MPC produces a wedge

The free market sets quantity where MARGINAL PRIVATE BENEFIT (the demand curve) equals MARGINAL PRIVATE COST (the supply curve). But if production imposes a cost on third parties — pollution, congestion, noise — there's a wedge between the private cost and the SOCIAL cost.

- Private cost line (S = MPC) — what firms see and respond to.
- Social cost line (MSC) — what society actually pays, including the externality.
- Free market equilibrium: where MPC = MPB. Quantity Q* (too high).
- Social optimum: where MSC = MSB. Quantity Q** (lower).

The triangle between Q* and Q**, bounded by MSC and MSB, is the deadweight welfare loss.

Policy responses for negative externalities: indirect tax on the polluter (Pigouvian tax, e.g. the UK SDIL), regulation, tradable pollution permits, property rights.

For positive externalities (vaccination, education) the welfare loss is the opposite direction — too little produced. Policy: subsidy, state provision, mandatory provision.

## Why no firm will sell you lighthouses

Two defining properties of public goods:

- **Non-rival** — one person's consumption doesn't reduce another's. The fact that you see a lighthouse doesn't stop me seeing it.
- **Non-excludable** — you can't stop someone consuming it. Once the light is on, every ship in the area sees it.

Together these create the **free-rider problem**: rational consumers refuse to pay for the good because they can consume it for free if anyone else pays. Result: nobody pays, the market provides zero, and a good people genuinely want goes unsupplied.

Government provision (or government-mandated provision) is the standard response. UK examples: national defence, BBC radio, street lighting, basic flood defences. Each is paid for through taxation precisely because no market mechanism would supply them.

A* point: most "public goods" in casual usage are actually quasi-public goods. They're rival or excludable to a degree (a crowded park is rival; a toll bridge is excludable). Pure public goods are rarer than the textbook suggests.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Information gaps and the lemons problem" color="pink" icon="globe"

## Why the used-car market under-prices good cars

Akerlof's 1970 paper, "The Market for Lemons", is the canonical example. In the second-hand car market:

- Sellers know their car's true quality (good or lemon).
- Buyers can't distinguish good cars from lemons before purchase.
- Buyers therefore price for the AVERAGE quality.
- Sellers of good cars find the market price too low and exit.
- The average quality falls, prices fall further, more good cars exit.
- In the limit, only lemons remain.

Result: a welfare-destroying outcome where the market for good cars collapses. The intervention is to reduce the information asymmetry — warranties, vehicle history checks (HPI), required disclosure. The UK's compulsory MOT certificate is a partial fix.

Similar logic in health insurance (adverse selection), labour markets (employer screening), financial products (the run-up to 2008 turned on opacity around mortgage-backed securities).

:::

::: section eyebrow="Government failure — the evaluation move" color="green" icon="check"

## When the cure is worse than the disease

The strongest market-failure essays don't just propose intervention — they evaluate the RISK of government failure.

- **Inadequate information.** Setting the right tax rate to internalise an externality requires knowing the marginal external cost. Policymakers usually don't.
- **Unintended consequences.** Cigarette taxes work; very high cigarette taxes drive a black market. Minimum wages raise low-end pay; very high minimum wages reduce employment. Every intervention has a tipping point.
- **Administrative cost.** The benefits system costs a small share of total spend to administer (a few percent). A £1bn intervention may genuinely cost more than £1bn once administration is counted.
- **Regulatory capture.** Regulators come from the industry they regulate, and tend to drift toward industry-friendly positions over time (Stigler 1971).
- **Political short-termism.** Five-year electoral cycles + multi-decade climate damage = systematic under-intervention on long-horizon problems.

The classic A* move: name the market failure, propose intervention, then evaluate the SPECIFIC government-failure risk in the proposed intervention. "A carbon tax would internalise the externality, but the politically achievable rate (~£40/tCO₂) sits below the social cost of carbon (~£200/tCO₂), limiting its corrective power" beats "a carbon tax would internalise the externality".

:::

::: section eyebrow="Exam edge" color="green" icon="cap"

## What the mark scheme actually rewards

- **Define precisely.** Market failure = misallocation of resources, free market produces below the social optimum. Examiners want the precise phrase.
- **Name the cause AT WORK.** Don't write "this is market failure". Write "this is a negative externality of production" (or whichever applies).
- **Draw the welfare-loss diagram.** MSB / MSC / MPB / MPC, deadweight triangle shaded. Every micro mark scheme rewards the labelled diagram.
- **Evaluate government failure.** The full answer names the intervention AND the government-failure risk specific to that intervention.

:::
