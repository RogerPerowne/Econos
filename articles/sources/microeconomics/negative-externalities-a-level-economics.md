## A negative externality is a cost the market price hides

The market price reflects what the buyer pays and what the seller receives. A **negative externality** is a cost that someone *outside* the transaction has to bear — and which the price ignores. Because the price ignores it, the market over-produces the good.

A factory dumping waste into a river illustrates the production case. The factory's MPC = wages + materials + power. The MSC = all of those PLUS the cost to downstream fishers + water-treatment plants + ecology. The wedge between MPC and MSC is the marginal external cost (MEC).

A driver in central London illustrates the consumption case. The driver's MPB = their time savings + comfort. The MSB = MPB MINUS the congestion + air-quality cost imposed on every other road user. The wedge here pulls the OTHER way — MSB sits BELOW MPB.

The exam tell: **production externalities shift the cost curves; consumption externalities shift the benefit curves.** Pick the right pair before you draw.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The diagram every essay needs" color="blue" icon="target"

## MSC, MPC, and the deadweight triangle

For a negative production externality (the workhorse case):

- **Demand curve D** = MPB = MSB (no consumption spillover assumed).
- **Supply curve S** = MPC (the private cost the firm faces).
- **MSC** sits ABOVE MPC by MEC — the cost society actually bears.
- **Market equilibrium**: D = MPC. Quantity Q\*, price P\*. The firm's chosen output.
- **Social optimum**: MSB = MSC. Quantity Q\*\*, lower than Q\*.
- **Deadweight loss**: the triangle between MSC and MSB from Q\*\* to Q\*.

Drawing this correctly — axes labelled (P, Q), four curves named (D = MSB = MPB, S = MPC, MSC), two equilibria marked, deadweight triangle shaded — is worth half the marks on any 9- or 12-mark question. Skip the diagram, lose the marks.

## The Soft Drinks Industry Levy worked because it changed firm behaviour

The UK Soft Drinks Industry Levy (SDIL, 2018) is the cleanest UK Pigouvian-style policy on the syllabus. The mechanism:

- Sugary soft drinks have a negative consumption externality — long-run health costs of obesity and type-2 diabetes that fall on the NHS rather than the consumer.
- The levy was tiered: a higher rate above 8g sugar per 100ml, a lower rate between 5–8g, no levy below 5g.
- Manufacturers faced a choice: pay the tax and pass it through (risking lost share to substitutes), or reformulate to fall below the 5g threshold and pay nothing.
- Most chose to reformulate. The sugar content of UK soft drinks fell by around 30% in the three years to 2021.

Why the policy succeeded where many taxes don't: it exploited an elasticity asymmetry. Consumer demand for soft drinks is relatively inelastic in the short run — passing the tax through would have raised price slightly and changed behaviour little. But manufacturers face elastic between-brand consumer response, so undercutting your reformulated rival's price mattered. Faced with that, reformulation was the cheaper margin.

The textbook conclusion: SDIL shrank the welfare-loss triangle by changing the PRODUCT rather than the QUANTITY. The strongest A-level answers name SDIL specifically and explain the elasticity-asymmetry mechanism — that's a Level 4 paragraph.

:::

## Why UK carbon pricing under-corrects

Climate change is the canonical externality of production at global scale. A tonne of CO₂ emitted in Manchester does damage in Bangladesh today and Antarctica next century. MEC is enormous; the marginal private cost paid by the emitter is near zero.

The Pigouvian prescription is a carbon tax equal to the social cost of carbon (central estimates £100–£200/tCO₂). The UK price (~£40/tCO₂ under the UK Emissions Trading Scheme, 2024) sits well below.

Three reasons why:

1. **Measurement uncertainty.** What discount rate to apply to damages a century out? Different assumptions yield a 5× range for the social cost of carbon. The policy-makeable rate sits at the low end of the range.
2. **Carbon leakage.** If the UK rate is much higher than China's, energy-intensive production moves rather than abates. The Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM, EU 2026+) is the standard fix — but only for imports from carbon-light countries.
3. **Distributional politics.** A higher carbon price falls hardest on low-income households (energy + transport are larger budget shares). Without redistribution, it's politically unsellable.

The top-band evaluation point: Pigouvian theory needs functioning markets, accurate damage measurement, and cross-border coordination. Climate externalities violate all three.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Tradable permits — quantity instead of price" color="green" icon="check"

## EU ETS and the cap-and-trade alternative

A tax sets the **price** of the externality and lets the market discover the quantity. A permit scheme sets the **quantity** (a cap on total emissions) and lets the market discover the price (through permit trading).

The EU Emissions Trading Scheme (ETS), running since 2005, caps EU industrial CO₂ at a declining ceiling. Firms holding permits can sell unused allowances to firms that want to emit more — the price equilibrates supply with the cap. In 2024 the EU ETS price hovered around €70/tCO₂, far above the comparable UK figure for most of the past decade.

The textbook trade-off:

- **Tax**: price certainty (firms can invest in abatement knowing the future carbon cost), quantity uncertainty (you don't know if you'll hit the climate target).
- **Permits**: quantity certainty (you DO hit the target — the cap binds), price uncertainty (volatile market price makes long-run abatement riskier to underwrite).

For a binding net-zero target, the quantity certainty of permits is the stronger argument. Hybrid schemes (permits with a price floor) try to deliver both at once.

::: section eyebrow="Exam edge" color="green" icon="cap"

## What the mark scheme actually rewards

- **Production vs consumption first.** Pin the externality to the right cell before you draw. Pollution = production. Passive smoking = consumption. Different diagrams.
- **Shade the deadweight triangle.** Between MSC and MSB, from Q\*\* to Q\*. Without it the diagram earns half the available diagram marks.
- **Match the policy to the externality.** Pigouvian tax for divisible negative externalities you can measure; tradable permits for binding quantity targets; regulation for thresholds; property rights where Coasean bargaining is feasible.
- **Name the UK case study.** SDIL for the success story; UK ETS / carbon pricing for the limits-of-Pigou story.
- **Evaluate elasticity.** A tax on inelastic demand barely changes behaviour. Always interrogate PED in the impact section.

:::
