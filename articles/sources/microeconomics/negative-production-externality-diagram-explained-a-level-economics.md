## This is a "how to draw it" guide

You can define a negative externality in a sentence. The marks come from the *diagram* — and the externality diagrams are where students most often misplace a curve or shade the wrong triangle. This guide is the drawing technique: which curve sits where, why the market over-produces, and exactly where the welfare loss goes.

Get this frame right and you have the template for every negative-externality question — pollution, congestion, passive smoking — because they are all the same diagram.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Frame the curves first" color="purple" icon="scale"

## MPB = MSB, MPC below, MSC above

Start with the axes: **price/cost** on the vertical, **quantity** on the horizontal. Now the curves. For a *production* externality (the cost comes from making the good, not consuming it), the demand curve doubles as **MPB = MSB** — private and social benefit are the same, because there is no consumption externality.

The action is on the cost side. Draw the **marginal private cost (MPC)** curve upward sloping — that is the firm's own cost. Then draw the **marginal social cost (MSC)** curve **above** MPC, because production imposes extra costs on third parties (pollution, noise). The **vertical gap between MSC and MPC is the external cost** per unit. Labelling that gap explicitly is a reliable mark.

:::

## Why the market over-produces, and where the triangle goes

The firm chooses output where **its** marginal cost equals marginal benefit — **MPC = MPB**. It ignores the external cost entirely, because it does not pay it. That gives the **free-market quantity**, which is *higher* than the social optimum.

The **social optimum** is where **MSC = MSB** — the quantity that maximises welfare once the external cost is counted. Because MSC sits above MPC, the optimum quantity is lower than the market quantity. The difference is **over-production**.

Now the welfare loss. Over every unit produced *beyond* the social optimum, MSC exceeds MSB — society loses on each one. Shade the **triangle between the MSC curve and the demand curve, from the social optimum quantity up to the free-market quantity**. That triangle is the deadweight welfare loss. Putting it anywhere else — over the whole output, or from the origin — is the error that costs marks.

:::

::: econ-insight portraitKey="economistPigou" tone="amber" label="Economist insight: Pigou on external costs" attribution="Arthur Pigou, <em>The Economics of Welfare</em> (1920)"
Self-interest will tend to bring about equality between the marginal private net product and the marginal social net product only when these two diverge by nothing.
:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="pink" icon="cap"

## Convert the diagram into marks

Two moves earn the band. First, **name the two output levels and the gap**: "the free market produces Q1 where MPC = MPB, but the social optimum is Q* where MSC = MSB; the economy over-produces by Q1 − Q*." Quantifying the over-production (even with labels, not numbers) shows you can read the diagram, not just draw it.

Second, **point at the welfare loss triangle and explain it**: "the shaded triangle shows the welfare loss, because over these units the cost to society (MSC) exceeds the benefit (MSB)." That sentence is the bridge from the AO3 diagram to the AO4 evaluation, where you would then argue how a Pigouvian tax shifts MPC up toward MSC to close the gap.

:::
