## Contestability is about the door, not the room

The instinct is to judge a market by how many firms are in it. Contestable market theory, associated with William Baumol, says look at the door instead: how easy is it to get *in*, and how easy to get *out*?

A market is **contestable** when entry and exit are cheap — above all when **sunk costs** (costs you can't recover when you leave) are low. The headline claim is striking: in a perfectly contestable market, even a single incumbent is forced to price at normal profit, because any attempt to charge more would invite an entrant to undercut it and then leave again. The discipline comes from the *threat* of entry, not from entry actually happening.

So a market with only a handful of big firms — an oligopoly on the headcount — can still behave competitively if outsiders can get in cheaply. The UK retail energy market is the case study: long dominated by the "Big Six", it was reshaped by entrants once liberalisation lowered the barriers at the door.

## Hit-and-run entry disciplines incumbents

Follow the chain. Suppose the incumbents are earning supernormal profit. If barriers are low and sunk costs minimal, an outsider can enter, undercut, capture some of that profit — and, crucially, leave again cheaply if the incumbents fight back. That is **hit-and-run competition**: entry to grab abnormal profit, exit at little cost when conditions change.

Now run it forward. Rational incumbents know this. So even before anyone enters, they price closer to normal profit to remove the incentive that would draw an entrant in. The threat alone does the work. This is why contestability theory matters: it predicts competitive behaviour — lower prices, better service — in markets that look concentrated.

The decisive variable is sunk costs. If entering means spending heavily on assets you can't resell, exit is expensive, hit-and-run is impossible, and the threat loses its bite. Where entry costs are largely recoverable, the threat is credible and incumbents stay disciplined. Retail energy supply has relatively low physical sunk costs — a supplier buys wholesale energy and bills customers rather than owning power stations — which is precisely why new entrants could threaten the Big Six.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Real world example" color="blue" icon="globe"

## Octopus Energy versus the Big Six

For years the UK retail energy market was dominated by the Big Six — British Gas, EDF, E.ON, npower, Scottish Power and SSE — who between them held most household accounts after the industry was liberalised. Critics argued these incumbents enjoyed sticky, inattentive customers and limited price competition.

Octopus Energy, founded in 2015, is the entrant that tested how contestable the market really was. Rather than owning the network, it competed as a retail supplier — buying energy wholesale, billing customers, and differentiating on price, service and technology. It grew rapidly into one of the largest suppliers, took customers from the incumbents, and later absorbed the customers of suppliers that failed. The relatively low physical sunk costs of retail supply are what made that entry possible; the incumbents could no longer treat their customer base as captive.

But the same market also shows the limits of contestability. When wholesale gas prices surged in 2021, a wave of small suppliers that had entered cheaply collapsed, because they were exposed to volatile costs they had not hedged. Entry was easy; survival was not. Octopus succeeded partly by being better capitalised and better run — which tells you the barriers were low but never zero.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## Contestable, but not perfectly — and that gap is the answer

A strong answer holds two things together: the market was contestable enough to discipline incumbents, but far from the textbook ideal.

**The barriers are real.** Suppliers need a licence, must meet regulatory and capital requirements, carry the cost of acquiring customers, and bear exposure to wholesale price swings. None of these is a wall, but together they mean entry is filtered, not free. Perfect contestability assumes none of this.

**Sunk costs are low but not absent.** Brand-building, IT systems and customer acquisition are partly unrecoverable. The 2021 collapses show that cheap entry did not mean cheap, riskless exit — failed suppliers left customers and unpaid hedges behind, costs ultimately spread across remaining bill-payers.

**Incumbency advantages persist.** Even disciplined by entry, established suppliers retain inertial customers, scale in procurement and recognised brands. The threat narrows the gap between their prices and competitive ones; it does not close it.

The evaluative move is to say *how* contestable the market is and what limits it — regulatory barriers, wholesale-price risk, and incumbency inertia — rather than declaring it either perfectly competitive or a closed oligopoly.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## Lead with the threat of entry, not the headcount

For **AO3 analysis**, make the contestability chain explicit: low sunk costs → credible threat of hit-and-run entry → incumbents price near normal profit even without actual entry. Use Octopus as application: low physical sunk costs in retail supply made entry credible, so the Big Six lost the ability to treat customers as captive.

For **AO4 evaluation**, judge the *degree* of contestability. Name the partial barriers — licensing, capital and hedging requirements, customer-acquisition costs, wholesale-price risk — and use the 2021 supplier collapses to show entry was easy but exit and survival were not. The mark is in "contestable but imperfectly so", with the reasons attached.

:::
