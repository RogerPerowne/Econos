## Oligopoly is about watching your rivals

An oligopoly is a market dominated by a few large firms. But the number of firms is not really the point — the point is **interdependence**. When Tesco considers a price change, it cannot ignore how Asda, Sainsbury's and Morrisons will respond. Each firm's best move depends on what it expects the others to do, and they all know this about each other.

That interdependence produces a distinctive set of behaviours. Prices tend to be **rigid** — they change less often than costs would suggest, because nobody wants to start a price war. So firms compete instead on everything *except* price: branding, advertising, loyalty schemes, product quality, store location. High barriers to entry protect the incumbents, and concentration ratios are high (a handful of firms hold most of the market).

Two tools unlock the topic. The **kinked demand curve** explains the sticky prices. **Game theory** explains the constant temptation to collude — and why collusion keeps breaking down.

## Why oligopoly prices get stuck

The kinked demand curve model, set out by Paul Sweezy in 1939, starts from one asymmetric assumption about how rivals react to a price change.

If a firm **raises** its price, it assumes rivals will *not* follow — they are happy to leave their prices low and steal market share. So the firm loses a lot of customers for a small price rise: demand above the current price is **elastic**, drawn as a relatively flat segment.

If a firm **cuts** its price, it assumes rivals *will* follow — they refuse to let it undercut them. So the firm gains few extra customers, because everyone matches the cut: demand below the current price is **inelastic**, drawn as a steeper segment.

Joining the two segments produces a kink at the current price. And here is the consequence that makes it an exam favourite: the kink in the demand curve creates a **vertical gap in the marginal revenue curve**. Within that gap, marginal cost can rise or fall and still cross marginal revenue at the same output — so the profit-maximising price does not move. That is the model's explanation for price rigidity: firms leave price alone even when their costs change, because both directions look unattractive.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Real world example" color="blue" icon="globe"

## UK supermarkets and the collusion temptation

The UK grocery market is a clean oligopoly. A handful of chains — Tesco, Sainsbury's, Asda, Morrisons, plus the fast-growing discounters Aldi and Lidl — hold most of the market between them. They rarely fight open price wars on headline products; instead they compete ferociously on loyalty cards (Tesco Clubcard, Nectar), "price match" promises, range and store location. That is textbook non-price competition under price rigidity.

The interdependence also creates a standing temptation to collude. If all the supermarkets quietly agreed to hold prices high, every one of them would earn more. This is why competition authorities watch oligopolies so closely. In 2007 the Office of Fair Trading found that several supermarkets and dairy processors had coordinated to raise the prices of milk, butter and cheese, and the firms involved ended up paying tens of millions of pounds in penalties.

That case is the whole topic in miniature. The firms were jointly better off colluding, which is why they did it. But collusion is illegal, hard to police internally, and individually each firm is tempted to cheat by undercutting the cartel to grab share — which is exactly what game theory predicts.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Game theory · the prisoner's dilemma" color="pink" icon="scale"

## Why colluding is tempting and unstable

Game theory models the interdependence head-on. The classic case is the **prisoner's dilemma**, applied to two firms each choosing whether to keep prices high (collude) or cut prices (compete).

Lay out the payoffs. If both keep prices high, both earn high profit. If both cut, both earn low profit. But if one cuts while the other holds, the cutter wins big market share and the holder is hammered. Now reason as one firm: whatever your rival does, you are better off cutting — if they hold, you steal share; if they cut, you must cut to avoid being undercut. Cutting is your **dominant strategy**, and the same logic applies to your rival.

So both firms cut, landing on the **Nash equilibrium** where neither can do better by changing strategy alone — even though both would be more profitable if they had managed to cooperate. That is the dilemma: individually rational behaviour produces a collectively worse outcome.

The model explains two things at once. It shows *why* firms are tempted to collude (the cooperative outcome pays more), and *why* collusion is fragile (each firm has a private incentive to cheat). The escape route is repetition: in a game played over and over, firms can sustain tacit cooperation through the threat of future retaliation — which is roughly how stable oligopoly pricing works in practice, with no formal agreement needed.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## The moves that lift your essay

For **AO3 analysis**, never leave the kinked demand curve as a picture. Spell out the chain: "rivals match price cuts but ignore price rises → demand elastic above the current price, inelastic below → a kink at the current price → a vertical break in MR → profit-maximising price unchanged as MC shifts within the gap → price rigidity." For game theory, identify the dominant strategy and name the Nash equilibrium explicitly.

For **AO4 evaluation**, weigh the consumer effects both ways. Oligopoly can harm consumers through tacit collusion and high prices, *but* the supernormal profits can fund dynamic efficiency — R&D, new products, loyalty rewards — and intense non-price competition can benefit consumers even when prices are sticky. The top-band point is that the outcome depends on whether competition authorities can keep collusion in check and whether the firms reinvest their profits.

:::
