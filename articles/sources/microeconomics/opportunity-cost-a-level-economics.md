faq:
  - q: "What is opportunity cost in A-level economics?"
    a: "Opportunity cost is the value of the next-best alternative that has to be given up when a choice is made. It arises because resources are scarce and wants are unlimited — every economic decision involves giving something up. A student spending three hours revising economics gives up the next-best alternative use of those three hours (e.g. revising maths, working a paid shift, sleeping). The CONCEPT applies to consumers, firms, and government identically."
  - q: "How does opportunity cost link to the PPF?"
    a: "The PPF (production possibility frontier) shows the maximum combinations of two goods an economy can produce with its current resources. Moving along the curve means producing more of one good and less of the other — that lost output is the opportunity cost of the gain. The CONCAVE shape exists because opportunity cost rises: the first units of resources switched from cars to vans are well-suited to both, but eventually you're moving car specialists to van production and losing more car output per van gained."
  - q: "What's the difference between accounting cost and opportunity cost?"
    a: "Accounting cost is the explicit monetary outlay — the £20 you actually paid for the book. Opportunity cost includes the IMPLICIT cost of what you couldn't do with that £20 — the meal forgone. Economists work with opportunity cost because it captures the real cost of a decision; accountants work with accounting cost because it's what shows up in the ledger. The opportunity cost of running your own business includes the salary you could have earned in someone else's employ — accounting cost ignores it."
  - q: "Does opportunity cost apply to public policy?"
    a: "Yes — arguably most powerfully there. If the government spends £1bn on NHS waiting lists, the opportunity cost is the next-best use of that £1bn — defence procurement, school capital projects, debt repayment, tax cuts. Every Budget decision is implicitly a ranking of opportunity costs. The OBR scores each Budget measure partly by asking what the £1bn could have done elsewhere."

## Opportunity cost is the value you DON'T see

The economic problem is that resources are scarce and wants are unlimited. Every yes is therefore also a no. **Opportunity cost** is the value of that no — the next-best alternative you gave up when you said yes to something.

Three contexts the mark scheme keeps testing:

- **Consumer choice.** £20 in your pocket can buy a meal OR a book. Picking the meal costs you the book; picking the book costs you the meal. The opportunity cost is whichever you DIDN'T choose.
- **Producer choice.** A factory's evening shift can produce 50 cars OR 30 vans. Producing the cars costs the firm 30 vans of output.
- **Government choice.** £1bn spent on the NHS is £1bn not spent on defence, schools, or tax cuts. The opportunity cost is the most valuable thing you could have done with the same resources.

The concept is the same in all three — the application differs.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Why opportunity cost rises along the PPF" color="blue" icon="target"

## The bow-shape exists because opportunity cost grows

The production possibility frontier (PPF) shows the maximum output combinations a country can make using all its resources. The curve is almost always drawn **concave** (bowed-out toward the origin) — and there's a precise reason:

Imagine an economy producing cars and vans. Start at the all-cars point. To produce the first few vans, you reassign the workers and capital that are EQUALLY good at both — minimal car output sacrificed.

Keep going. To produce more vans, you have to reassign workers who were SPECIALISED in car production. They're not as good at vans. You lose more car output per van gained.

Eventually, to produce that last van, you're pulling the engineers who design car suspensions. You lose huge amounts of car output for a single extra van.

That's why opportunity cost RISES along the PPF — and why the curve bows outward. The slope at any point IS the opportunity cost at that point: how many cars you give up for the next van.

A* tip: never describe the PPF without naming WHY it's concave.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The three economic agents" color="purple" icon="scale"

## Consumers, firms, government — same logic

| Agent | Decision | Opportunity cost |
|---|---|---|
| Consumer | Spend £20 on a meal | The book they could have bought |
| Worker | Take a £30k job offer | The £35k job they turned down |
| Firm | Use the factory for cars | The vans they didn't produce |
| Government | Spend £1bn on NHS | Schools, defence, or tax cuts |
| Country | Specialise in finance | The manufacturing industry it doesn't develop |

The pattern: every economic decision is a CHOICE among alternatives, and the cost is what you didn't pick.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The UK 2010s policy example" color="pink" icon="globe"

## Austerity as an opportunity-cost story

The UK's post-2010 austerity programme cut government spending significantly. The Treasury framing: "we have to live within our means; lower spending reduces the deficit". The opposing framing — using opportunity cost properly — would say: "the spending cuts have an opportunity cost in lost output, lost public services, and lost human capital from underinvestment in schools and hospitals".

Both framings are coherent. They differ in what they treat as the relevant alternative.

For an A-level essay on fiscal policy, opportunity cost is the right lens for evaluation: every £ of spending IS doing something; cutting it means doing that something less, not nothing. The strongest answers name the specific opportunity cost of the policy being analysed.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Exam edge" color="green" icon="cap"

## What the mark scheme actually rewards

- **Define using "next-best alternative forgone".** Not "what you gave up" — examiners want the precise phrasing.
- **Link to scarcity.** Opportunity cost exists BECAUSE resources are scarce. Mentioning the connection earns the AO1 mark.
- **Apply to the case in the question.** If the question is about government spending, the opportunity cost is the next-best public-spending use of the funds. If it's about firm behaviour, it's the next-best production decision.
- **On PPF questions, explain WHY it's concave.** Resources are not equally productive across uses — that's the underlying mechanism. Most candidates miss this.

:::
