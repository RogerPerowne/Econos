## The commons is over-used, not under-provided

Start with the classification, because it's where the marks begin. A **pure public good** is non-rival and non-excludable, and its problem is *under-provision* — free riders mean the market supplies too little. A **common-pool resource** (or common access resource) is different: it is **rival** but still **non-excludable**. A fish I catch is gone for you; a patch of grass my cattle eat is gone for yours — but no one can easily be fenced out.

That combination flips the failure. Because the resource is rival, using more depletes it; because it's non-excludable, no individual can be stopped. So the classic problem isn't too little, it's too much: the **tragedy of the commons**, where individually rational use adds up to collective over-exploitation.

The standard A-level response offers two fixes — privatise the resource (create property rights) or nationalise it (let the state manage). Elinor Ostrom's work, recognised with the 2009 Nobel Prize, supplies a third: communities can sometimes govern the commons themselves.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The mechanism · why the tragedy happens" color="purple" icon="target"

## Private gain, shared cost

Garrett Hardin's argument is a chain about incentives. Picture a shared pasture. A herder who adds one more animal captures the *whole* benefit of that extra animal — but the cost, the slight extra wear on the common grass, is spread across *every* user. So the private benefit of adding an animal exceeds the private cost the herder bears, even when the cost to the group as a whole is larger.

Every user faces the same arithmetic, so every user adds animals. The grass is over-grazed; the resource degrades; eventually everyone loses. The point is subtle and worth stating precisely for the exam: the over-use is not irrational individually. Each herder is responding sensibly to the incentives. It is the *structure* — rival resource, non-excludable access, divergence between private and social cost — that produces the bad collective outcome.

That is why the obvious fixes target the structure. Privatisation makes the resource excludable, so the owner internalises the depletion cost. State management imposes quotas from outside. Both can work, and both can fail — privatising a fishery requires enforceable rights; state quotas require information the regulator may not have, and risk government failure.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Real world example" color="blue" icon="globe"

## Ostrom's case studies: rules made by the users

Ostrom's contribution was empirical. Rather than assuming the tragedy was inevitable, she went and looked at real common-pool resources around the world and found many that had been managed sustainably for generations — without privatisation and without the state.

Her examples included inshore **fisheries** where local fishers agreed who could fish where and when; alpine **grazing pastures** in Switzerland governed by centuries-old community rules; and **irrigation systems** where farmers collectively decided water-sharing and took turns. What these successful cases shared were design features she set out as principles: clearly defined boundaries (who may use the resource), rules adapted to local conditions, the users themselves having a say in setting the rules, **monitoring** of use, **graduated sanctions** for those who break the rules (a small penalty first, escalating for repeat offenders), cheap conflict-resolution, and recognition of the community's right to organise.

The lesson for the public-goods toolkit is precise: a community that can define its membership, write its own rules, watch each other and enforce proportionate penalties can internalise the depletion cost *socially* — without either property markets or a distant regulator. It is a third institutional route, not a claim that the tragedy never happens.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## Self-governance works under conditions — name them

The Ostrom point is gold in an evaluation, but only if you state its limits.

**It needs the design conditions.** Self-governance worked in Ostrom's cases because boundaries were clear, the group was identifiable, members expected to keep dealing with each other, and monitoring was feasible. Where users are anonymous, mobile, or impossibly numerous, those conditions fail.

**Scale is the binding constraint.** A village irrigation system is one thing; the global atmosphere is another. Climate change is the hardest commons precisely because there is no defined community, no shared monitor and no enforcer of graduated sanctions across nations. Ostrom's principles don't scale to it cleanly, which is why state and market instruments still matter for global commons.

**The alternatives aren't free either.** Privatisation requires enforceable, low-cost property rights, which may not exist for a migratory fish stock. State quotas risk government failure — poor information, regulatory capture, enforcement costs. Ostrom doesn't make these redundant; she adds a third option whose suitability depends on the resource.

The evaluative move is to match the institution to the resource: community governance where boundaries and repeated interaction allow it; property rights where they can be enforced; state action where the commons is too large or anonymous for either.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## Classify first, then offer the third option

For **AO3 analysis**, anchor the answer in the right classification — rival but non-excludable — and run Hardin's incentive chain: private benefit of extra use captured in full, cost of depletion shared, so individually rational use over-exploits the resource. Getting "rival, non-excludable" exactly right separates a common-pool resource from a public good and earns early marks.

For **AO4 evaluation**, deploy Ostrom by name as the third route between market and state, and state the conditions it needs (clear boundaries, local rules, monitoring, graduated sanctions) plus where it breaks down (large-scale, anonymous commons like the climate). The mark is in matching institution to resource, not in declaring one fix universally best.

:::
