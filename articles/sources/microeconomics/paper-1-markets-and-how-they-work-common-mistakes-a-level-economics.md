## Paper 1 fails in the same few places every year

Paper 1 — "Markets and how they work" — is the microeconomic core: demand and supply, price determination, the price mechanism, elasticity, market failure and government intervention. The content is not hard. Yet the same handful of errors show up in examiner reports year after year, and they cost marks across every question that uses a market.

This guide is not new theory. It is the list of recurring Paper 1 mistakes and the fix for each — the things that quietly drag a correct answer down a level. Learn to spot them in your own writing and you bank marks you were already half-earning.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Mistake 1" color="purple" icon="scale"

## Shift versus movement — the diagram error

This is the most-flagged Paper 1 mistake, and it is pure technique. A **movement along** a curve happens *only* when the good's own price changes — you slide up or down a fixed curve. A **shift of the whole curve** happens when a *determinant* changes.

For demand, the determinants are income, tastes, the prices of related goods, expectations and population. For supply, they are costs of production, technology, indirect taxes, subsidies and the number of firms. A health scare changes tastes → the whole demand curve shifts left. The good getting more expensive on its own → a movement up along demand.

The give-away of the error is a student who changes a determinant and then draws an arrow up the existing curve. Drill the question: *did the good's own price change, or did something else change?* Own price means movement; anything else means shift.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Mistake 2" color="blue" icon="spark"

## Wrong curve, and forgotten ceteris paribus

Two application errors travel together.

**Mislabelling which curve a policy moves.** An indirect tax on producers raises their costs, so it shifts *supply* left (up by the size of the tax) — not demand. A subsidy shifts supply right. A change in consumer income shifts demand. Students under pressure often move the wrong curve, which breaks the whole analysis even if the prose is fluent. Before you draw, name the determinant and check which side of the market it acts on.

**Forgetting ceteris paribus.** Each diagram move assumes everything else is held constant. When a question changes two things at once — say a tax *and* a demand shock — handle them as separate, labelled steps rather than smearing them into one ambiguous shift. State the assumption out loud: "holding incomes constant, the tax shifts supply left..." That single clause shows the examiner you are reasoning cleanly.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Mistake 3" color="pink" icon="bulb"

## Elasticity treated as decoration

Many scripts mention elasticity and then ignore it — they quote the definition for AO1 and never use it to change the answer. That wastes the highest-value application move in the paper.

Elasticity is what makes the *magnitude* of an effect predictable, and it routinely flips a conclusion. Tax incidence is the cleanest case: when demand is more inelastic than supply, consumers bear the larger share of an indirect tax, because they keep buying as price rises so producers can pass more of it forward. Change the elasticity and the incidence changes — so "this tax mainly hits consumers" is only true under a stated elasticity assumption.

The same logic runs through subsidies, minimum prices and the revenue effect of a price change. The exam move is to *condition* every market prediction on the relevant elasticity: "the quantity effect is small *because* demand for this good is price inelastic, so the policy raises revenue but does little to cut consumption."

:::

::: section eyebrow="Mistake 4" color="purple" icon="check"

## Analysis with no evaluation — the level cap

On the longer Paper 1 questions the marks split between the analysis strand and the evaluation strand. The recurring error is an answer that builds a strong chain and a tidy diagram, then stops — no weighing, no judgement. That answer is capped below the top level, because the evaluation marks are simply unreachable.

Evaluation here means weighing the market effect you analysed. Reach for a concrete lever: elasticity (how big is the quantity response?), time lag (do consumers adjust slowly?), the assumption behind your diagram, or an unintended consequence (a minimum price creating a surplus, an indirect tax creating a black market). Then judge: "the tax reduces consumption *only if* demand is responsive enough, which for an addictive good it may not be — so the policy is better justified as a revenue raiser than as a behaviour-change tool."

That conditional judgement is the AO4 the longer questions are built to reward, and it is the difference between a capped script and a top-level one.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="green" icon="cap"

## The Paper 1 habits to drill

Before every diagram, ask whether the good's own price changed (movement) or a determinant changed (shift), and check which curve the change acts on. State ceteris paribus when a question throws two changes at once.

Make elasticity do work — condition the size and even the direction of your conclusion on it, rather than quoting the definition and moving on.

And on the longer questions, ring-fence time to evaluate. Weigh the market effect with a concrete lever and finish on a supported judgement. Most Paper 1 marks are lost to these four habits, not to gaps in knowledge — fix the habits and the content you already have starts scoring what it should.

:::
