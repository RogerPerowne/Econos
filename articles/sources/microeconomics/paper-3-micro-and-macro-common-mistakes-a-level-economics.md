## Paper 3 is one paper, not two

Paper 3 — "Microeconomics and macroeconomics" — is the synoptic paper on the Edexcel A specification. It is built around two case studies, and it can reach into any of the four themes. A single question might begin with a firm's costs and market structure (Theme 3 micro) and end at inflation, the trade balance or international competitiveness (Themes 2 and 4 macro).

That word — synoptic — is the whole exam. It means the paper is testing whether you can move between the micro and the macro, not just recite each in turn. The students who lose marks here are usually well prepared on content. Their problem is structural: they answer Paper 3 as if it were two separate exams stitched together, and they manage their time as if only the first essay counted.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Mistake 1" color="purple" icon="target"

## Answering in silos and missing the synoptic link

The defining Paper 3 error is the unmade connection. A case study presents a dominant supermarket or a privatised utility, and the answer treats it as a pure microeconomics question — market structure, profit maximisation, efficiency — when the extract is clearly pointing at a macro consequence.

The higher-band move is to follow the thread. A firm with market power can hold prices above the competitive level; if that firm is large enough or the sector is widespread, that pricing power feeds the general price level (micro to inflation). A monopoly that under-invests can blunt productivity growth (micro to long-run aggregate supply). An exporter's cost advantage shows up as international competitiveness (micro to the current account).

You don't force a macro link where the case doesn't support one. But on Paper 3 the case study almost always leaves a door open between the two halves of the course, and walking through it is where the synoptic marks are earned.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Mistake 2" color="blue" icon="scale"

## Burning the clock on essay one

The second great Paper 3 mistake has nothing to do with economics. It is timing.

The pattern is familiar: a student opens strongly, gets absorbed in the first long answer, polishes it past the point of marginal return, and then meets the second case study with a quarter of the time it needed. The first essay is excellent. The second is two paragraphs and a hopeful conclusion.

Because the marks are spread across both case studies, this is the most expensive habit on the paper. A brilliant first answer cannot compensate for an unfinished second — there is no bonus for over-delivering on one question, and there is a large, easily-won block of marks sitting in the answer you never reached.

The discipline is to budget the clock before you write and to move on when the time for a question is up, even mid-flow. An answer worth 18 marks left at 12 because you ran on elsewhere is a self-inflicted loss.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Mistake 3" color="green" icon="globe"

## Thin evaluation under pressure

Because Paper 3 demands two full case studies, evaluation is often the casualty. Students who evaluate well on a single-context Paper 2 question produce thin, generic "it depends" lines on Paper 3 because the pressure squeezes the judgement out.

The fix is to keep evaluation cheap but real. You do not need a fourth paragraph of new arguments; you need a supported judgement on the arguments already made. Pick one criterion — magnitude (how big is the effect?), time lag (when does it bite?), or which assumption actually holds in this case — and use it to say which side is stronger and why. One sharp, criterion-led judgement per long answer protects your AO4 marks without costing the time the second case study needs.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## The Paper 3 routine

Budget the clock, link the two halves, judge with one criterion.

Before writing, split your time evenly across the two case studies and hold the line. In each answer, read the case for the micro-to-macro door it leaves open and walk through it where it is genuinely there — that synoptic link is the AO3 and AO4 signature of the paper. Keep evaluation lean but decisive: one criterion, one supported judgement, stated outright. The students who finish both case studies with a real conclusion on each out-score the ones who write one perfect essay and abandon the other.

:::
