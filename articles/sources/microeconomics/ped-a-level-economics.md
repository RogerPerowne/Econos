faq:
  - q: "What is price elasticity of demand?"
    a: "PED measures how responsive quantity demanded is to a change in price. It's calculated as the percentage change in quantity demanded divided by the percentage change in price. The result is always negative (because price and quantity move in opposite directions for normal goods) but A-level reports the absolute value. PED > 1 means elastic demand; PED < 1 means inelastic; PED = 1 means unitary."
  - q: "What determines whether demand is elastic or inelastic?"
    a: "Four main determinants. (1) Availability of substitutes — more substitutes, more elastic. (2) Necessity vs luxury — necessities are inelastic, luxuries elastic. (3) Share of income spent on the good — small share, inelastic; large share, elastic. (4) Time horizon — short-run inelastic, long-run more elastic as households can switch. Cigarettes are inelastic on all four counts; restaurant meals are elastic on all four."
  - q: "How does PED affect total revenue?"
    a: "If demand is elastic, a price cut raises total revenue (the quantity gain dominates the price loss). If demand is inelastic, a price rise raises total revenue (the price gain dominates the quantity loss). Unitary elasticity means total revenue is unchanged by price changes. This is why luxury car makers tend to raise prices in downturns (their core demand is inelastic) while budget airlines cut prices (demand is elastic)."
  - q: "How does PED affect tax incidence?"
    a: "When demand is inelastic, consumers bear most of an indirect tax — they buy roughly the same quantity at the higher price. When demand is elastic, producers bear most of the tax — raising price drops sales sharply, so the producer absorbs the tax to maintain volume. The UK Soft Drinks Industry Levy (SDIL) was designed around this: by targeting sugar content tiers, it pushed manufacturers to reformulate (because consumer demand for branded soft drinks is reasonably elastic in the long run) rather than passing the tax through."

## PED is the elasticity students learn, but use badly

`PED = % change in quantity demanded ÷ % change in price`. Always negative (price and quantity move oppositely); A-level reports the absolute value.

Categories:
- **PED > 1**: elastic — quantity responds proportionally MORE than price. Restaurant meals. Holidays. New cars.
- **PED < 1**: inelastic — quantity responds proportionally LESS. Cigarettes. Petrol. Insulin.
- **PED = 1**: unitary — proportional response. Total revenue stays the same.
- **PED = ∞**: perfectly elastic — horizontal demand curve. The price-taker firm in perfect competition.
- **PED = 0**: perfectly inelastic — vertical demand curve. The textbook abstraction; rarely realistic.

The exam isn't testing whether you can calculate. It's testing whether you can apply the four determinants to a case study, predict the PED category, and connect it to revenue or tax incidence.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The four determinants" color="blue" icon="target"

## What makes a good elastic or inelastic

Four factors, each pushing in the same direction:

- **Substitutes.** More close substitutes = more elastic. When Pepsi raises price, consumers switch to Coke. When a monopoly raises price, there's nowhere to go.
- **Necessity vs luxury.** Necessities (food, transport-to-work, basic medication) are inelastic. Luxuries (restaurant meals, designer goods, holidays abroad) are elastic.
- **Share of income.** A 10% rise in salt prices barely registers (tiny share of income → inelastic). A 10% rise in mortgage rates is a household crisis (huge share → very elastic in the long run).
- **Time horizon.** Short-run inelastic; long-run more elastic. After the 2008 oil price spike, demand looked inelastic — drivers had to commute. Five years later, demand was more elastic — hybrids, EVs, rail commuting all emerged.

Cigarettes are inelastic on all four counts: addictive (no real substitute), arguably necessary for addicts, small share of income for most smokers, and behavioural inertia keeps the time horizon short. Restaurant meals are elastic on all four — many substitutes, luxury, sizeable share of income, easy to substitute.

The mark scheme wants you to name the determinants AND apply them to the specific case in the question.

:::

::: section eyebrow="PED and total revenue" color="purple" icon="scale"

## The single rule firms use

`Total revenue = price × quantity`. PED tells you which one dominates when price changes.

- **Elastic demand**. Cutting price raises revenue (quantity rises by more than price falls). Budget airlines built a business on this — endless promotional pricing because demand is highly elastic.
- **Inelastic demand**. Raising price raises revenue (price rises by more than quantity falls). UK water bills, insulin pricing in the US, mid-market tobacco — all examples where price hikes increase revenue.
- **Unitary**. Revenue is unchanged. The price and quantity changes exactly offset.

The classic exam essay: a firm facing elastic demand on its main product. Should it cut prices to chase volume, or raise prices to chase margins? The answer depends on PED at the relevant point on the demand curve — and PED varies along the curve (elastic at high prices, inelastic at low prices is the typical pattern for a linear demand curve).

The elasticity of demand in a market is great or small according as the amount demanded increases much or little for a given fall in price, and diminishes much or little for a given rise in price.

::: section eyebrow="PED and tax incidence" color="pink" icon="globe"

## Why SDIL targeted producers, not consumers

The Soft Drinks Industry Levy (2018) is the cleanest A-level case study on PED and tax incidence.

The traditional logic: cigarettes have inelastic demand → an excise tax falls on consumers → revenue rises, consumption falls modestly. UK tobacco duty has worked this way for decades.

SDIL did something different. It applied a tax on PRODUCERS based on sugar content per litre. Why?

- Manufacturers face moderately elastic LONG-RUN demand for sugary soft drinks (own-brand, water, low-sugar variants are easy substitutes).
- If they pass the tax through fully, they lose volume to competitors who reformulate.
- So instead, they reformulate to avoid the tax — Coca-Cola Zero became the dominant variant; many brands cut sugar to below the 5g/100ml threshold.

Result: sugar consumption from soft drinks fell ~30% in three years. Treasury revenue from the tax came in below forecast because manufacturers reformulated faster than expected. That's the elasticity story playing out exactly as theory predicts.

The mark scheme rewards candidates who name SDIL AND explain the elasticity mechanism.

## What the mark scheme actually rewards

Three moves that lift a PED answer:

- **Apply the four determinants.** Don't just quote "demand is inelastic". Justify: addictive (no substitutes), necessity, small share of income, short time horizon — all four push PED below 1.
- **Connect to revenue OR to tax incidence.** PED is interesting because of the consequences. Either show how a firm's pricing decision depends on PED, or show how tax incidence does.
- **Name a UK case study.** SDIL for tax incidence + reformulation. Petrol price hikes 2022 for short-run inelastic / long-run elastic. UK water bills for inelastic-demand pricing power.
