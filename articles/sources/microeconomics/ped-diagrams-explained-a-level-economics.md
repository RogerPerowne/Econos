## The PED diagram is a two-curve comparison, not one line

The single most common mistake is drawing *one* demand curve and asserting it is elastic or inelastic. Elasticity is a comparison, so the examiner wants to see the contrast on the page.

Set up the axes first: **price (P) on the vertical axis, quantity (Q) on the horizontal**. Label both with units where the question gives them. Then draw two demand curves on the same diagram — a **steep** one and a **flat** one — and title them clearly. The steep curve is relatively **inelastic**; the flat curve is relatively **elastic**.

That is the whole framework. Everything else is reading a price change off the two curves and noting how differently quantity responds.

::: section eyebrow="How to read it" color="blue" icon="target"

## Drop the same price change onto both curves

Once the two curves are drawn, the reading is mechanical. Pick a starting price and mark it across both curves. Now apply the *same* price change — say a rise from P1 to P2 — and trace down to the quantity axis from each curve.

The flat (elastic) curve gives a **large** fall in quantity: the percentage change in quantity exceeds the percentage change in price, so PED is greater than one. The steep (inelastic) curve gives a **small** fall in quantity: the percentage change in quantity is smaller than the percentage change in price, so PED is less than one.

The exam move is to annotate the quantity axis with the two different Q responses (ΔQ_elastic and ΔQ_inelastic) so the examiner can see you understand that *the same price change produces different quantity reactions depending on the curve*. That annotation is where the analysis marks live.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The link examiners reward" color="purple" icon="scale"

## The total revenue test built into the diagram

PED diagrams earn their best marks when you connect them to **total revenue** (price × quantity), shown as a rectangle under the price point.

When price rises along an **inelastic** demand curve, quantity barely falls, so the revenue rectangle grows — revenue rises. When price rises along an **elastic** demand curve, quantity falls sharply, so the rectangle shrinks — revenue falls. Reverse both for a price cut.

The shorthand to drill: **price and revenue move together when demand is inelastic, and in opposite directions when demand is elastic.** Show the two revenue rectangles changing area on the diagram and you have turned a definition into applied analysis — exactly the AO3 step the mark scheme is hunting for. This is also why a firm with pricing power wants to know its PED before it changes price.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Slope is not elasticity" color="pink" icon="globe"

## Why a straight line changes elasticity along its length

Here is the trap that catches strong students. On a straight-line demand curve the **slope is constant**, but the **elasticity is not**.

Slope measures the absolute change in price over the absolute change in quantity. Elasticity measures the *proportionate* change — percentage change in quantity over percentage change in price. The same one-unit step down the curve is a much larger percentage where price is high and quantity is low (the top of the curve) than where price is low and quantity is high (the bottom).

So a single straight demand curve is **elastic at the top, unit elastic at the midpoint, and inelastic at the bottom**. If a question asks you to label regions of one curve, this is the answer — and writing "the slope is the same so elasticity is the same" is a guaranteed lost mark. Genuinely flat or vertical curves are the special cases: perfectly elastic (horizontal, PED = ∞) and perfectly inelastic (vertical, PED = 0).

:::

::: section eyebrow="Earning the diagram marks" color="green" icon="cap"

## The checklist examiners mark against

- **Label the axes** P and Q with units, and title each curve D (or D_elastic / D_inelastic).
- **Draw both curves** on one set of axes — a steep one and a flat one. One curve alone is not a PED diagram.
- **Apply one price change** to both and annotate the *different* quantity responses on the Q axis.
- **Show the revenue rectangles** changing area to connect elasticity to total revenue.
- **Never equate slope with elasticity** on a straight line — say where on the curve you are reading.

Get those five right and the diagram does the heavy lifting for the whole answer.

:::
