## Perfect competition is a model, not a place

No real market is perfectly competitive. The model is a deliberately extreme case built on four assumptions, and its job is to give economists a clean benchmark.

The four assumptions are: **many buyers and sellers**, each too small to move the price; a **homogeneous product**, so buyers see no difference between sellers; **freedom of entry and exit**, with no barriers; and **perfect information**, shared by everyone.

Stack those together and a striking result falls out: the individual firm is a **price taker**. It is so tiny relative to the whole market that it can sell as much as it likes at the going price, but not a penny more — charge above the market price and buyers simply go to an identical rival. So while the *market* demand curve slopes down in the usual way, the *firm's* demand curve is horizontal at the market price. That horizontal line is also its average revenue and its marginal revenue, which is what makes the firm's profit-maximising decision so simple.

::: section eyebrow="The big picture · short run to long run" color="purple" icon="target"

## How supernormal profit gets competed away

In the short run a perfectly competitive firm can make supernormal profit. It produces where marginal cost equals marginal revenue (which here equals price), and if that price sits above average total cost, the firm earns more than normal profit.

But the short run is unstable, and the engine that destroys it is free entry. Supernormal profit is a signal. With no barriers, new firms pour into the industry to grab a share. As they do, market supply shifts right, and the market price falls. Each existing firm's horizontal demand curve drops with it.

Entry continues for as long as there is supernormal profit to chase. It only stops when price has fallen to the point where firms earn just normal profit — that is, where price equals minimum average total cost. The mirror image works for losses: if firms are making a loss, some exit, supply falls, price rises, and the survivors return to normal profit.

So the long-run equilibrium has a precise location: **P = MR = MC = minimum ATC**. Every firm covers its costs including normal profit, and not a penny more. The exam point is that this is not a coincidence or an assumption — it is forced by the entry-and-exit mechanism that the "no barriers" assumption sets in motion.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Real world example" color="blue" icon="globe"

## Nothing is perfect, but some markets get close

Agricultural commodity markets are the textbook near-example, and for good reason. A single UK wheat farmer sells a product close to identical to every other farmer's, faces a price set by global supply and demand, and cannot nudge that price by withholding their own crop. They are, to a useful approximation, a price taker.

Foreign exchange markets push closer still. The market for major currency pairs has enormous numbers of participants, a genuinely homogeneous product (a pound is a pound), near-instant information, and no one trader large enough to set the rate. It is one of the few real markets where the horizontal-demand-curve intuition almost holds.

But notice what is missing even here. Branding, location, switching costs, patents, regulation and economies of scale all create the barriers and product differences that the model assumes away. Most markets a student will write about — supermarkets, energy supply, mobile networks — are nothing like perfectly competitive. That gap is not a flaw in the model; it is the point. The model tells you what efficient looks like so you can measure how far a real market falls short.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## The benchmark has blind spots

Perfect competition delivers allocative efficiency (P = MC, so the last unit is valued by consumers exactly at its cost) and productive efficiency (minimum ATC). That double efficiency is why it is the benchmark. But the top-band evaluation asks whether the benchmark is the *right* one.

**No funds for innovation.** Firms earning only normal profit have nothing left to invest in research and development. Schumpeter argued that the supernormal profits of larger firms — anathema to the perfect-competition ideal — are exactly what finance the dynamic efficiency that raises living standards over time. A perfectly competitive industry might be statically efficient and dynamically stagnant.

**No economies of scale.** With many tiny firms, the model rules out the cost savings that come from producing at scale. In industries with high fixed costs — energy networks, railways, telecoms — many small competing firms would be more expensive, not cheaper.

**The assumptions never hold.** Perfect information, homogeneous products and zero barriers are heroic. Once you relax any of them you leave perfect competition behind. So using it as a literal policy target makes little sense; using it as a yardstick does.

The evaluation move: accept that perfect competition is statically efficient, then ask what it costs — dynamic efficiency, scale economies, and realism — before treating it as the goal.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## The moves that lift your essay

For **AO3 analysis**, drive the long-run result through the entry mechanism rather than asserting it. "Supernormal profit → no barriers → new firms enter → market supply shifts right → price falls → firm's horizontal demand curve drops → entry stops only when P = minimum ATC, leaving normal profit." Drawing the firm and the market side by side, and showing the firm's demand curve falling, is the analysis examiners want.

For **AO4 evaluation**, compare perfect competition against monopoly on the right dimensions. "Perfect competition wins on static efficiency *because* P = MC and output is at minimum ATC, but it may lose on dynamic efficiency *because* normal-profit firms cannot fund R&D." Naming allocative, productive and dynamic efficiency by name, and saying which the model achieves and which it sacrifices, separates the top band from a list of assumptions.

:::
