## This is a "how to draw it" guide

The positive consumption externality diagram is the mirror image of the negative one — and that is exactly why students muddle them. They shift the wrong curve, or shade the welfare loss on the wrong side. This guide is the drawing technique: which curve splits, why the market under-consumes, and where the welfare loss actually goes.

Nail this frame and every positive-externality question — vaccination, education, healthcare — runs off the same template.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Frame the curves first" color="purple" icon="scale"

## MPC = MSC, MPB below, MSB above

Axes first: **price/cost** vertical, **quantity** horizontal. For a *consumption* externality (the extra benefit comes from consuming the good), the supply curve doubles as **MPC = MSC** — private and social cost are the same, because there is no production externality. Keep the cost side together; this is the half students wrongly try to move.

The action is on the **benefit** side. Draw the **marginal private benefit (MPB)** curve downward sloping — that is what the consumer personally values. Then draw the **marginal social benefit (MSB)** curve **above** MPB, because consumption benefits third parties too (a vaccinated person protects others; an educated worker raises everyone's productivity). The **vertical gap between MSB and MPB is the external benefit** per unit. Label it.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Under-consumption and the welfare loss" color="blue" icon="bulb"

## Why the market under-consumes, and where the triangle goes

Consumers buy where **their** marginal benefit equals the price — **MPB = MPC**. They ignore the external benefit, because it accrues to other people. That gives the **free-market quantity**, which is *lower* than the social optimum.

The **social optimum** is where **MSB = MSC** — the welfare-maximising quantity once the external benefit is counted. Because MSB sits above MPB, the optimum quantity is higher than the market quantity. The difference is **under-consumption**.

Now the welfare loss. Over every unit between the market quantity and the social optimum, MSB exceeds MSC — society would gain from consuming them, but doesn't. Shade the **triangle between the MSB curve and the supply curve, from the free-market quantity up to the social optimum**. That triangle, sitting to the *right* of the market quantity, is the welfare loss from under-consumption. Shading it on the left (as if over-consuming) reverses the entire argument.

:::

::: econ-insight portraitKey="economistPigou" tone="amber" label="Economist insight: Pigou on external benefits" attribution="Arthur Pigou, <em>The Economics of Welfare</em> (1920)"
Where the marginal social net product exceeds the marginal private net product, the value of the resources devoted to that use falls short of what would best serve the national interest.
:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="pink" icon="cap"

## Convert the diagram into marks

Two moves earn the band. First, **name the two quantities and the gap**: "the free market consumes Q1 where MPB = MPC, but the social optimum is Q* where MSB = MSC; the economy under-consumes by Q* − Q1." Stating the under-consumption explicitly proves you can read the diagram, not just reproduce it.

Second, **point at the welfare loss triangle and explain it the right way round**: "the shaded triangle shows welfare lost, because over these units the benefit to society (MSB) exceeds the cost (MSC), yet the market does not produce them." That is the launchpad into the policy evaluation — a subsidy shifts MPB up toward MSB (or lowers MPC) to raise consumption toward the optimum.

:::
