## A positive externality is a benefit the market price hides

When someone vaccinates against measles, they receive the **private benefit** of personal immunity. But the rest of the community also benefits — reduced transmission, herd immunity protecting unvaccinated infants and the immunocompromised. The market price they pay (typically zero in the UK) reflects the private benefit. It does NOT reflect the social benefit that includes the spillover.

Because the price is below MSB, the market quantity sits **below** the socially optimal quantity. The textbook conclusion: positive externalities mean the market under-consumes (or under-produces) the good.

Two flavours, mirroring the negative case:

- **Consumption externalities** — the benefit comes from USE. Vaccination, education, public transport (your bus journey reduces road congestion for everyone else). MSB sits above MPB.
- **Production externalities** — the benefit comes from MAKING. Apprenticeship training (skills transfer when the worker changes firm), R&D (knowledge spillovers across the industry). MSC sits below MPC.

The exam tell: positive externalities shift the BENEFIT curve up (consumption case) or the COST curve down (production case). Pick the right pair before you draw.

:::

## MSB, MPB, and the welfare gain that never happens

For a positive consumption externality (the workhorse case):

- **Supply curve S** = MPC = MSC (no production spillover assumed).
- **MPB** = the private demand curve (what consumers will pay).
- **MSB** sits ABOVE MPB by the marginal external benefit.
- **Market equilibrium**: S = MPB. Quantity Q\*, price P\*. Where the market lands without policy.
- **Social optimum**: MSB = MSC. Quantity Q\*\*, higher than Q\*.
- **Welfare loss**: the triangle between MSB and MSC, from Q\* to Q\*\* — the welfare gain society fails to capture by under-consuming.

Drawing this correctly — with axes labelled (P, Q), three curves named (MPB, MSB, S = MSC), two equilibria marked, and the deadweight triangle shaded BETWEEN MSB and MSC ACROSS the Q\*–Q\*\* range — is what the diagram marks reward. The most common error is shading the triangle BELOW MPB instead of BETWEEN MSB and MSC. Practise this.

::: section eyebrow="Vaccination — the canonical case" color="purple" icon="globe"

## Why universal vaccination is closer to state provision than a subsidy

The textbook policy response to a positive consumption externality is "a subsidy equal to MEB". That works on paper, but UK childhood vaccination is closer to *full state provision* — the NHS delivers the service free at the point of use under a population programme.

Why the policy moved from subsidy to state provision:

1. **Herd-immunity thresholds** require near-universal uptake. A subsidy that lifts coverage from 80% to 90% may not cross the threshold for measles (~95%); state provision combined with the school-entry programme does.
2. **Information frictions** dominate the private decision. Even when vaccination is free, vaccine-hesitancy reduces uptake. The MMR–autism controversy (Wakefield, 1998 onwards) is the textbook case. A subsidy cannot fix a misperception of risk; targeted information + active outreach can.
3. **Externalities of consumption interact with externalities of refusal.** An unvaccinated child exposes vulnerable contacts. The marginal external cost of NOT vaccinating is potentially large. State provision plus social norms is the policy that captures it.

A subsidy is the right tool when private uptake is responsive to price (e.g. for adult booster vaccines) and the externality is incremental. State provision is the right tool when the externality has a threshold (herd immunity) or when private demand barely responds to price at all.

The exam-ready point: the choice of policy lever depends on the SHAPE of the externality, not just the SIGN.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Education and R&D — production-side positives" color="pink" icon="scale"

## Why education is partly under-provided despite massive subsidies

UK higher education is heavily subsidised — student loans, research grants, the apprenticeship levy. Yet the policy debate is still about *under-investment*. Three reasons that mirror the diagram:

- **External benefits** are large and slow to mature. A more educated workforce raises productivity across the economy, increases innovation, and reduces unemployment-related costs to government. These spillovers don't show up in the private return graduates capture; they show up in tax receipts decades later.
- **Capital-market failure** caps private consumption even with subsidies. A teenager from a low-income family cannot borrow against future graduate earnings on commercial terms — collateral is the problem credit markets can't solve. UK income-contingent loans are a state response to that failure.
- **Network and signalling effects** complicate the analysis. If education is partly a signal of innate ability rather than a creator of human capital, the SOCIAL gain from raising participation is smaller than the PRIVATE gain. The honest answer is "some of both" — and the policy bundle (loans + grants + targeted access) reflects that uncertainty.

R&D is the cleaner production case. A private firm captures the licensing revenue from a patent but cannot capture the downstream knowledge spillover — engineers move firms, ideas diffuse, competitors learn from public filings. R&D tax credits (HMRC schemes) are the UK Pigouvian fix on the production side.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Policy choice — the four levers" color="green" icon="check"

## Pick the lever to match the externality

| Policy | Where it fits |
|---|---|
| **Subsidy** | Price-responsive demand, divisible externality (free dental care, R&D tax credits). |
| **State provision** | Threshold externality, near-universal uptake required (NHS vaccination, primary education). |
| **Mandatory consumption** | Where private incentives fall far below social — compulsory schooling, MMR for school entry in some US states. |
| **Information campaign** | Where the externality is poorly understood by consumers (anti-smoking, public-health messaging). |

The mark scheme distinguishes A* answers from B answers in two places: (1) naming the right lever for the specific case; (2) evaluating WHY the standard subsidy doesn't deliver Q\*\* — capital constraints, information frictions, threshold effects.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Exam edge" color="green" icon="cap"

## What the mark scheme actually rewards

- **Pin the consumption vs production cell.** Vaccination = consumption. Apprenticeship spillovers = production. Different diagrams.
- **Shade the right triangle.** Between MSB and MSC, FROM Q\* to Q\*\*. Not below MPB.
- **Name the UK case study.** NHS vaccination programme + UK higher-education subsidies + HMRC R&D tax credits — one each side of the consumption/production line.
- **Evaluate the lever choice.** Subsidies fail when demand is unresponsive (vaccination), when capital constraints bind (education), or when the externality has a threshold (herd immunity).
- **Link to merit goods.** Positive externalities often overlap with merit goods — but the syllabus treats them as separate concepts (the merit-good label adds an information asymmetry the externality framework alone doesn't capture).

:::
