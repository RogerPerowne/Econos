## This is a "how to draw it" guide

The PPF is usually the first diagram an A-level student meets — and the first one they under-use. It looks simple, so it gets drawn carelessly and the diagram marks evaporate. This guide is the technique: how to label it, what each region means, and how to read opportunity cost, efficiency and growth straight off the curve.

Master it early and you have a tool that reappears across the whole specification — scarcity, choice, efficiency, growth and trade all lean on this one frame.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Frame the axes first" color="purple" icon="scale"

## Two goods, a bowed curve, three regions

Put **a good on each axis** — capital goods versus consumer goods is the classic pairing because it sets up growth and investment later. Draw the frontier as a curve **bowed outward** from the origin (concave to the origin). That shape is not decoration: it shows opportunity cost rising as you specialise, because resources are not equally suited to both goods.

Then mark the three regions explicitly. A point **on** the curve is **productively efficient** — every resource is in use. A point **inside** the curve means **spare capacity or unemployment** (inefficiency). A point **beyond** the curve is **unattainable** with current resources. Labelling all three is exactly what converts a sketch into a mark-earning diagram.

## Reallocation moves along it; growth shifts it

A **movement along** the PPF is a reallocation of existing resources between the two goods. It always has an **opportunity cost**: to gain more of one good you give up some of the other, and the bowed curve shows that cost rising as you push further into one corner. Read the opportunity cost directly — the units of Y sacrificed for the extra X.

A **shift of the whole PPF** changes the economy's **productive capacity**. An **outward** shift is economic growth: more workers, more or better capital, new technology, or improved education raise potential output. An **inward** shift is a loss of capacity — a natural disaster, a war, a shrinking labour force.

The exam discipline: when resources are merely reallocated, draw a point moving along the curve; when the quantity or quality of resources changes, shift the whole frontier and label it PPF1.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="pink" icon="cap"

## Convert the diagram into marks

Two moves lift the PPF from a sketch to an argument. First, **read a specific opportunity cost off the curve in the prose**: "moving from point A to point B, the economy gains X consumer goods but sacrifices Y capital goods — that sacrifice is the opportunity cost." Naming the trade-off in numbers (even illustrative ones) shows you understand what the diagram represents.

Second, **distinguish actual from potential output**. A point inside the curve is the gap between what the economy produces and what it could produce — the natural way to discuss unemployment or a recession — while an outward shift is the long-run growth story. Examiners reward candidates who use the same diagram to separate a short-run demand problem (a point moving toward the curve) from a long-run supply gain (the curve shifting out).

:::
