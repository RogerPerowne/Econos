## Three functions, one price

Adam Smith's "invisible hand" runs on a single thing: the market price. In any competitive market, that single number is doing three jobs at once.

- **Rationing.** When supply is limited and demand is high, the price RISES — and that rise ALLOCATES the scarce supply to those who value it most. Without a rising price, demand would exceed supply forever and the queue would never clear.
- **Signalling.** A high price tells producers "more is wanted here"; a low price tells them "this is over-supplied". The information is conveyed without anyone having to gather and broadcast it. A consumer's willingness to pay £5 for a coffee in central London — and only £2 in Stoke — is communicated to suppliers worldwide by the prices they observe.
- **Incentivising.** Prices don't just convey information; they CHANGE BEHAVIOUR. A high oil price triggers fracking in Texas, electric-vehicle investment in Munich, and bike-commuting in Berlin. A low price for solar panels triggers rooftop installations across Europe. Resources move where prices say they're most valued — without central direction.

The exam-ready trick: every market shock makes all three functions visible at once. Trace them in sequence and you've written a Level 4 paragraph.

## All three functions, one event

When global semiconductor demand outran supply in 2021–22, the price mechanism allocated the limited chips across competing uses.

**Rationing.** Chip prices rose. The highest-margin uses (servers for cloud-computing firms, premium-priced smartphones) kept buying at the higher price. Lower-margin uses (mid-range cars, white goods) lost out — Ford and GM cut production rather than pay the new prices for chips that went straight into a £25,000 vehicle. The chips were rationed *to the buyers willing to pay most.*

**Signalling.** The price spike told every chip fabricator on the planet "build more capacity here". TSMC announced $100bn of new investment; Intel restarted its US foundry strategy; Samsung accelerated South Korean expansion. The high price didn't just sit there — it told the global supply base where the value was.

**Incentivising.** Existing fabs ran 24/7 (incentive to producers). Designers reformulated boards to use older, cheaper chips (incentive to substitution). Carmakers signed long-term supply contracts (incentive to lock in capacity). The shortage resolved over about 18 months not because anyone planned the recovery but because the price mechanism drove the behaviour changes that closed it.

A central planner could not have managed this. The information about who values chips most, where new capacity is cheapest to build, and which board designs can substitute is decentralised across millions of agents. The price aggregates it.

## The four cases the price mechanism can't fix alone

The price mechanism works only when the price actually reflects social cost and benefit. Four cases where it doesn't:

- **Externalities.** The market price ignores third-party costs/benefits. The factory's price for a tonne of CO₂ is zero; society's price is hundreds of pounds. Rationing, signalling and incentive all misfire because the input price is wrong. The fix is a Pigouvian tax that puts the externality INTO the price.
- **Public goods.** Non-excludability means a market price never forms. No one will pay for what they can get for free, so the supply is missing entirely.
- **Asymmetric information.** When one side of the trade knows more than the other (used cars, health insurance), the price can't signal quality. Adverse selection drives high-quality sellers out of the market.
- **Market power.** A monopolist can set price above MC without losing all customers. The price no longer reflects competitive scarcity; the rationing function over-rations.

Each case is a separate topic on the syllabus, but they share a structural diagnosis: the price doesn't carry the right information. Naming this connection earns AO3 marks across micro essays.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The central-planning critique" color="pink" icon="globe"

## Why Hayek's argument still matters

Friedrich Hayek's 1945 essay *The Use of Knowledge in Society* is the named-economist reference examiners reward at top band on this topic.

Hayek's argument: a central planner attempting to allocate resources directly would need to gather and process information about every consumer's preferences, every producer's costs, every input substitution possibility, every regional difference — instantaneously and continuously. No planner has ever had this information. The price mechanism, by contrast, distributes the information-processing across the entire economy. Each agent acts on their local knowledge; the price aggregates it.

This isn't a defence of market outcomes as FAIR (Hayek was clear that markets distribute brutally). It's a defence of markets as INFORMATIONAL — they communicate dispersed knowledge in a way no central body can replicate.

The standard A-level evaluation:
1. Markets fail when prices don't reflect social cost/benefit (the four cases above).
2. Where prices DO carry good information (most consumer markets), decentralised pricing tends to beat central allocation.
3. Hybrid systems (mostly market, with targeted state intervention where the price fails) dominate pure planning AND pure laissez-faire in every real economy.

Pairing the three functions with Hayek's knowledge argument is the structural backbone of an A* answer.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Exam edge" color="green" icon="cap"

## What the mark scheme actually rewards

- **Name all three functions and DEFINE each.** Rationing (clearing scarcity), signalling (conveying information), incentive (changing behaviour).
- **Trace them through one concrete market shock.** 2021 chip shortage, 2022 gas spike, 2008 oil price collapse — pick one and walk through.
- **Cite Hayek by name.** The 1945 essay is the syllabus reference. Bare "decentralised information" doesn't earn the AO4 mark.
- **Connect to market failure.** When the price doesn't reflect external cost/benefit, all three functions misfire — the same diagnosis runs across externalities, public goods, asymmetric information and market power.
- **Evaluate distributionally.** The mechanism is efficient (allocative); it isn't fair (distributive). A high oil price rations to the highest-value use, not the most-needy user.

:::
