## A price floor that isn't about surpluses

Scotland introduced minimum unit pricing for alcohol in May 2018, setting a legal floor of 50p per unit. In 2024, after a review, the floor was raised to 65p per unit. Below that price, a unit of alcohol simply cannot be sold.

The instinct is to reach for the agricultural price-floor diagram — surplus, government buys the excess. Resist it. Alcohol MUP is a price floor whose job is to *reduce demand*, so there is no surplus for the state to mop up. The quantity bought falls, and the policy succeeds by selling less, not by storing more.

And crucially, because it is a minimum price rather than a tax, the extra money consumers pay stays with retailers and producers. The government collects nothing.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The mechanism" color="blue" icon="target"

## Why per-unit pricing targets the harm

Alcohol is a **demerit good** with **negative externalities** — the social costs (health-service use, crime, lost productivity) exceed the private costs the drinker weighs up. Left alone, the market over-consumes. A minimum price corrects this by setting the floor above the free-market price for the cheapest products.

The chain runs: MUP set per unit of pure alcohol → cheap, high-strength drinks see the biggest price rise → those products are disproportionately bought by the heaviest drinkers → consumption falls most among the highest-risk group → alcohol-related harm and its external costs fall.

The targeting is the clever part. A bottle of mid-range wine may already cost more than the floor implies, so it barely moves. A cheap, strong cider priced below 50p (later 65p) per unit must rise sharply. So the price signal lands hardest exactly where the external cost is concentrated — something a flat ad valorem tax, which raises all prices proportionally, does less precisely.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Real world example" color="purple" icon="globe"

## Scotland, 2018 to 2024

Scotland's MUP began at 50p per unit in May 2018 after a long legal battle in which the policy was challenged and ultimately upheld. In 2024 the floor was uprated to 65p per unit, both to keep pace with inflation eroding the real value of the original figure and in response to evidence on consumption.

The case is valuable because it isolates the price-floor mechanism in the real world. The cheapest, strongest products — the ones the policy was aimed at — saw the steepest price rises, while many mainstream products were unaffected because they already sold above the floor. That uneven bite is the point: the policy reshapes the bottom of the market rather than nudging the whole price level.

The revenue distinction is where naming MUP earns marks. Under an equivalent tax, the higher price paid by drinkers would flow to the Treasury. Under MUP, it is retained by shops and producers. That makes MUP politically awkward — it can look like a windfall for retailers — and it is the single sharpest contrast you can draw between a minimum price and a tax in an exam answer.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## What MUP's success depends on

**The revenue goes to the wrong place.** A tax of the same size would raise revenue the government could spend on treatment or wider services. MUP hands that money to sellers. If you believe the externality is best tackled by funding healthcare, a tax dominates; if you believe the priority is simply cutting the consumption of the highest-risk group at lowest political cost, MUP's targeting may win.

**Cross-border and substitution leaks.** Drinkers near a border can buy cheaper alcohol elsewhere, and the most dependent drinkers may cut spending on other essentials rather than on alcohol — so the demand response among the very heaviest users can be smaller than hoped.

**Regressivity and elasticity.** The floor bites hardest on low-income heavy drinkers. Whether that is justified depends on whether the health gains accrue to the same group — which, for alcohol-related illness, they largely do.

The evaluation move is to say what the case turns on: the price elasticity of demand among the heaviest drinkers, and whether you value the targeting of MUP above the revenue of a tax.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## Floor, not tax — say it explicitly

For **AO2 application**, get the numbers and the type right: a price floor, 50p per unit from 2018, 65p from 2024, set per unit of pure alcohol.

For **AO3 analysis**, run the targeting chain: per-unit floor → cheap strong drinks rise most → heaviest drinkers face the biggest increase → consumption and external costs fall. For **AO4 evaluation**, lead with the revenue contrast — under MUP the extra money goes to sellers, under a tax it goes to the government — because that single distinction is what separates a generic "minimum price" answer from one anchored in the real policy.

:::
