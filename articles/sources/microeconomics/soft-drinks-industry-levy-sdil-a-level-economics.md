## A tax built to be avoided

The Soft Drinks Industry Levy — the "sugar tax" — came into force in April 2018. It charges manufacturers a fixed amount per litre of sugary drink, and it has two tiers: a lower rate for drinks containing 5g to 8g of sugar per 100ml, a higher rate above 8g, and nothing at all below 5g.

That threshold structure is the whole story. A flat tax on every fizzy drink would simply raise prices. A *tiered* tax says to every manufacturer: cut the sugar below 5g per 100ml and your liability disappears. The government wanted firms to reformulate, and it built the incentive straight into the tax.

So the headline effect wasn't the price on the shelf. It was the recipe in the can.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The mechanism" color="blue" icon="target"

## The indirect-tax chain — with a twist

Start with the textbook chain for a specific indirect tax. A per-unit levy raises the cost of supplying each unit, so the **supply curve shifts vertically upward by the size of the tax**. At the new equilibrium, the price consumers pay rises, the quantity traded falls, and the government collects revenue equal to the tax per unit times the quantity sold.

How that burden splits — the **incidence** — depends on elasticity. For a sugary drink with close substitutes (water, diet versions, other brands), demand is relatively price-elastic, so producers struggle to pass the full tax on and absorb more of it themselves.

Now the twist the SDIL adds. Because the tax only bites *above* a sugar threshold, firms have a third option beyond "raise price" or "absorb cost": **change the product**. Reformulate below 5g per 100ml → zero liability → no upward supply shift at all. The chain becomes: tiered tax announced → reformulation cheaper than paying the levy → sugar content cut → lower sugar consumption with little price change. The behaviour examiners reward you for spotting is the one the simple diagram hides.

:::

## What actually happened after 2018

The most striking outcome of the SDIL is that a large share of the response came *before* the tax was even collected, because manufacturers reformulated in anticipation. Major brands cut the sugar in popular drinks to fall below or into the lower tier, and the government has reported a substantial reduction in the sugar content of soft drinks subject to the levy.

This is the case study working in your favour. A simple "tax raises price, consumers buy less" answer applies a model. Naming the SDIL lets you say something sharper: the policy targeted **producers**, not consumers, and the most powerful lever was the threshold, not the rate. Firms that reformulated faced no levy and could keep their shelf price competitive; firms that did not passed some of the charge on and risked losing price-elastic customers to reformulated rivals.

There is also a revenue point worth making. Because the tax was designed to be avoided, success on the health objective means *lower* revenue over time — a deliberate feature, not a failure. A tax intended to raise money and a tax intended to change behaviour pull in opposite directions, and the SDIL is squarely the second kind. The revenue raised has been linked to school sport and healthy-eating programmes, but the primary metric of success is reformulation, not receipts.

:::

A tax set equal to the marginal external cost of an activity can align private incentives with the social interest — making the polluter, or here the producer, face the full cost their product imposes on society.

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## Where the SDIL gets complicated

The strongest evaluation does not say "it might not work." It says what the result *depends on*.

**Substitution leaks the externality.** Sugary drinks are one source of dietary sugar among many. If consumers switch to untaxed juices, sweets or syrups, the external cost is only partly corrected — the wedge between private and social cost shrinks for drinks but persists across the diet. The optimum is restored for the taxed good, not for sugar overall.

**Regressivity.** A specific tax takes a larger share of income from poorer households, who spend a higher proportion of their budget on the taxed goods. Whether that is acceptable depends on whether the health gains land most heavily on the same groups — which, for diet-related illness, they may.

**Elasticity and the reformulation route.** The cleaner the substitute and the easier the reformulation, the smaller the price effect and the larger the product-change effect. The SDIL worked partly because reformulating fizzy drinks is technically straightforward; the same tiered design would behave very differently for a product that cannot easily be reformulated.

The evaluation move is to identify which channel — price, quantity, or product change — actually does the work, and then to ask whether the leftover external cost (the sugar that simply moves elsewhere) is large or small.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## The marks live in the threshold

For **AO2 application**, name the tier structure, not just "the sugar tax." Saying the levy applied above 5g and 8g per 100ml and that this is what triggered reformulation shows you understand the specific policy, not a generic tax.

For **AO3 analysis**, run the full chain: specific tax → supply shifts up by the tax → but tiered design → reformulation cheaper than paying → sugar cut with little price change. For **AO4 evaluation**, make the substitution point conditional: the policy corrects the externality *only to the extent* that consumers do not replace the lost sugar from untaxed sources.

Diagnose the lever before you judge the policy. With the SDIL, the lever is the threshold.

:::
