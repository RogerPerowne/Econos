## Supply and demand is the first model — and the most misused

The supply-and-demand model says two things. **One**, demand and supply each respond to price. **Two**, the price adjusts to clear the market.

The **law of demand** says quantity demanded falls as price rises, ceteris paribus. The reasoning: income effect (a higher price makes you effectively poorer), substitution effect (rivals look cheaper), and diminishing marginal utility (the second cup of coffee is worth less than the first).

The **law of supply** says quantity supplied rises as price rises, ceteris paribus. The reasoning: higher prices cover the rising marginal cost of bringing extra units to market and attract new entrants.

The crucial phrase is *ceteris paribus* — "other things equal". When other things AREN'T equal, the curve **shifts**. When price changes (and nothing else does), you **move along** the curve. The exam separates students at exactly this distinction.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Movement vs shift — the single biggest error" color="purple" icon="target"

## Conditions of demand and supply

A change in **price** causes movement along the curve. A change in any **non-price condition** shifts the curve.

The conditions of demand (mnemonic **PIRATES**):

- **P**opulation — more buyers, demand shifts right.
- **I**ncome — for normal goods, higher income shifts demand right; for inferior goods (Lidl basics), higher income shifts demand left.
- **R**elated goods — a fall in the price of a substitute (Coke for Pepsi) shifts demand for Pepsi left; a fall in the price of a complement (printers for ink cartridges) shifts demand for cartridges right.
- **A**dvertising — successful campaigns shift demand right.
- **T**astes — health concerns shifted beef demand left; the rise of veganism shifts plant-milk demand right.
- **E**xpectations — expected future price rises shift current demand right (panic buying).
- **S**easons — ice-cream demand rises in summer.

The conditions of supply (mnemonic **PINTSWC**, or just "costs of production"):

- **P**roductivity — better tech / training shifts supply right.
- **I**ndirect taxes — VAT or specific duties shift supply LEFT.
- **N**umber of firms — entry shifts supply right.
- **T**echnology — innovation shifts supply right.
- **S**ubsidies — shift supply RIGHT.
- **W**eather / external shocks — bad weather shifts agricultural supply left.
- **C**osts of inputs — wage rises, energy spikes shift supply left.

A question that says "the price of coffee rose because a frost destroyed half the Brazilian crop" is asking about a SUPPLY shift (weather → costs → supply curve left), with a subsequent MOVEMENT ALONG the demand curve as price rises. Naming both halves correctly is what mark schemes call AO3 analysis.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The four-step framework" color="blue" icon="scale"

## Initial equilibrium → shift → disequilibrium → new equilibrium

Every market-mechanism question follows the same four-step structure. Memorise it.

1. **Initial equilibrium.** Demand meets supply at price P₁, quantity Q₁. State this on the diagram.
2. **The shift.** Some condition of demand or supply changes. Draw the new curve. Be explicit about WHICH conditions (income? input costs?) and the direction (right or left).
3. **Disequilibrium at the old price.** At P₁, planned demand no longer equals planned supply. Identify the surplus or shortage. (Frost shifts supply left → excess demand at P₁.)
4. **New equilibrium.** Price adjusts (rises in excess-demand cases, falls in excess-supply cases) until planned demand again equals planned supply at P₂, Q₂. State the direction of P and Q change.

A mark scheme rewards the four-step chain. A bare "supply falls, price rises" is worth a single mark; the chain through disequilibrium is worth three or four.

## Price floors and ceilings — disequilibrium by design

The market-clearing assumption fails whenever a non-market force fixes price away from equilibrium.

- **Maximum prices (price ceilings)** sit BELOW equilibrium. They create excess demand — shortages, queues, secondary markets, rationing. Rent controls in New York and Berlin are the standard A-level case study; the welfare loss is the deadweight triangle plus the misallocation cost of non-price rationing.
- **Minimum prices (price floors)** sit ABOVE equilibrium. They create excess supply — wine lakes, butter mountains, surplus production. The EU Common Agricultural Policy at its 1980s peak is the textbook example. The Scottish minimum unit price on alcohol (£0.50/unit, 2018; raised to £0.65 in 2024) is the modern case.

Both pin price at a level the market wouldn't choose. Both create welfare losses through the deadweight triangle. Both create distributional consequences that depend on who's on each side of the market.

The standard A-level evaluation is to weigh the policy's STATED goal (affordability for tenants, harm reduction for alcohol) against the WELFARE COST (deadweight loss + black-market substitution + misallocation). Each policy has a defender; the diagram is the same.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Exam edge" color="green" icon="cap"

## What the mark scheme actually rewards

- **Name movement vs shift explicitly.** "A change in income shifts demand right" not "demand goes up".
- **Identify the disequilibrium step.** Without it, the chain breaks and the analysis mark goes with it.
- **Label both axes and both curves on every diagram.** P on Y, Q on X; D₁, D₂, S₁ as needed.
- **Use the conditions mnemonic.** PIRATES for demand, costs of production for supply — naming the specific condition that shifted the curve is worth a mark in itself.
- **Apply to a UK example.** The 2022 energy price spike (supply-side shock), the post-pandemic used-car boom (demand-side shift), or the dairy market under CAP minimum prices — UK-relevant case studies score AO2.

:::
