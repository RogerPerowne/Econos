## Water is a *natural* monopoly, not just a big firm

A natural monopoly exists where one firm can supply the whole market at a lower average cost than two or more firms could. The reason is cost structure: laying the pipes, building the reservoirs and treatment works costs billions in fixed capital, while piping an extra litre to one more home costs almost nothing. So average cost keeps falling across the entire range of regional demand — economies of scale never run out before the market is served.

Lay a second, competing set of water mains down every street and you have doubled the fixed cost to serve the same households. That is why nobody proposes competition "in the market" for water pipes. Thames Water is a regional monopoly by the logic of its costs, not by predatory conduct.

The exam consequence: you cannot apply the standard "competition will discipline the firm" story. There is no competition to do the disciplining, so a regulator has to stand in for it.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The big picture · why regulate at all" color="purple" icon="target"

## An unregulated natural monopoly overcharges and underinvests

Left alone, a profit-maximising monopoly sets output where marginal revenue equals marginal cost, charges a price above marginal cost, and earns supernormal profit. Allocative efficiency is lost: price exceeds the marginal cost of the last unit, so some consumers who value water above what it costs to supply are priced out, and a deadweight loss appears.

There is a second danger specific to essential utilities. A monopoly facing no competitive threat has weak incentives to invest in quality — in Thames Water's case, in fixing leaks and upgrading sewage treatment — because customers cannot switch supplier when service is poor. The market signal that would punish a competitive firm is missing.

That is the case for Ofwat. The regulator's job is to push price down towards average cost (so the firm covers its costs but earns little excess) and to set investment and quality obligations the firm would otherwise skimp on. The chain examiners want: natural monopoly → no competitive discipline → risk of high prices, allocative inefficiency and underinvestment → regulation substitutes for competition.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The mechanism · how Ofwat does it" color="blue" icon="scale"

## Price caps, RPI-X, and the five-year settlement

Ofwat regulates through a price cap rather than by regulating profits directly. The historic formula was RPI-X: companies could raise prices by inflation (RPI) minus an efficiency factor X. If a firm could cut costs faster than X assumed, it kept the difference as profit; if it lagged, it bore the loss. That is the clever part — a price cap leaves the firm with a sharp incentive to become efficient, because the gains from beating the cap accrue to it, at least until the next review.

Every five years Ofwat resets the cap in a "price review" (the 2024 settlement covered 2025–2030), recalibrating allowed bills, required investment and the cost of capital it lets companies recover. Between reviews the firm is residual claimant on its own efficiency.

The weakness is information. Ofwat has to estimate the efficient cost of running a water network, but Thames Water knows its own costs far better than the regulator does — a classic principal–agent information asymmetry. Set X too soft and the firm pockets easy rent; set it too hard and investment collapses. Getting it wrong in the firm's favour, repeatedly, is what regulatory capture would look like in practice.

:::

::: econ-insight tone="amber" label="Economist insight: Stigler on regulatory capture" attribution="George Stigler, <em>The Theory of Economic Regulation</em> (1971)"
As a rule, regulation is acquired by the industry and is designed and operated primarily for its benefit.
:::

::: section eyebrow="Real world example" color="green" icon="globe"

## Thames Water: debt, dividends and the capture question

Thames Water was privatised with the rest of the industry in 1989, debt-free. By the 2020s it carried debts of roughly £15bn — among the highest in the sector — while customers faced sewage discharges into rivers and persistent leakage. By 2024 the company was warning it could run short of cash, and government contingency planning for a possible temporary nationalisation (a "special administration regime") was openly discussed.

How a debt-free regional monopoly with captive customers and inflation-linked revenue ended up near insolvency is the heart of the case. Over the years owners extracted dividends while the network aged; debt was loaded onto the regulated company; and Ofwat's settlements allowed a cost of capital and a structure that critics argue rewarded financial engineering over investment in pipes and treatment.

Walk the chain through Stigler's lens: a regulator with weaker information than the firm → settlements that under-demanded investment and tolerated high gearing → returns flowing to shareholders and lenders rather than into the network → the regulated outcome looking closer to what the industry wanted than what customers needed. Whether that is genuine *capture* or simply a regulator that was too lenient is exactly the kind of distinction a top-band answer draws rather than asserts.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## It might be weak regulation, not capture — and the counterfactual matters

The capture story is powerful, but a strong answer pushes back three ways.

**Capture is hard to prove.** The same facts — high debt, low investment, dividends — are consistent with a regulator that was simply too trusting or under-resourced, not one that was captured in Stigler's sense of being run *for* the industry. Distinguishing deliberate alignment from ordinary regulatory failure matters, because the policy fix differs: tougher rules versus a different institution entirely.

**The counterfactual to private monopoly is public monopoly, which has its own failures.** Pre-1989 the water industry was state-run and chronically underfunded, which is part of why it was privatised. Nationalising Thames Water removes the dividend leakage but reintroduces the soft-budget and political-investment-cycle problems that public monopolies are prone to. "Regulation failed, therefore nationalise" skips the comparison the question is really asking for.

**Price caps create their own incentive distortions.** A cap that rewards cost-cutting can reward cutting the *wrong* costs — deferring maintenance to beat the efficiency target this period and dumping the consequences on the next. The design flaw may sit in the instrument, not in capture.

The evaluative move is to say what the capture claim depends ON: evidence that Ofwat's settlements were shaped by the industry's interests rather than merely lenient, and a credible comparison with the realistic alternative.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## The marks are in 'natural' and in the counterfactual

For **AO3 analysis**, build the natural-monopoly chain explicitly: enormous fixed network costs → falling average cost over the whole market → one firm is least-cost → no competition to discipline price or quality → regulation must substitute. A bare "water is a monopoly so Ofwat regulates it" earns a fraction of the marks the linked chain does.

For **AO4 evaluation**, name what your judgement depends on. Capture versus weak regulation is a distinction, not a synonym; and any "nationalise it" recommendation has to be weighed against the public-monopoly failures privatisation was meant to cure. Reference Stigler by name — examiners reward the candidate who knows capture is a specific theory, not a vague complaint that the regulator was useless.

:::
