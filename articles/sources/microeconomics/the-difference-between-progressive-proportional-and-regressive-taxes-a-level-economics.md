## One test sorts all three

Progressive, proportional and regressive describe *how the burden of a tax changes with income*. The single test that separates them is what happens to the **average tax rate** — total tax paid divided by income — as income rises.

- **Progressive:** the average rate **rises** with income. Higher earners pay a larger *proportion* of their income.
- **Proportional:** the average rate stays **constant**. Everyone pays the same proportion.
- **Regressive:** the average rate **falls** as income rises. Lower earners pay a larger proportion.

The trap is to look at the cash amount. A higher earner usually pays more *pounds* under almost any tax — that tells you nothing. Progressivity is always about the *share of income*, never the absolute sum. Apply the average-rate test and the categories sort themselves.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The big picture · the three in UK terms" color="purple" icon="target"

## Income tax, a flat tax, and VAT

Put a UK example to each.

**Progressive — income tax.** UK income tax is charged in rising bands: a tax-free personal allowance, then a basic rate, then higher and additional rates on income above each threshold. As someone's income climbs into higher bands, the *average* rate they pay across all their income rises. So a high earner hands over a larger share of income than a low earner — the textbook progressive structure. (Marginal rate, the rate on the next pound, also rises, which is what drives the average up.)

**Proportional — a flat tax.** Imagine a tax that takes a constant 20% of everyone's income, with no bands. A worker on £20,000 pays £4,000 (20%); one on £200,000 pays £40,000 — ten times the cash, but the *same* 20% share. The average rate is flat, so the tax is proportional.

**Regressive — VAT.** VAT is a flat 20% on most spending, which sounds proportional. The catch is the link between income and spending: lower-income households spend almost all their income and save little, while higher-income households save a larger slice. Because VAT only bites on what is *spent*, it captures a larger proportion of a poorer household's income. The average rate, measured against income, *falls* as income rises — making VAT, and indirect taxes generally, regressive.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The payoff · why the label drives the policy" color="blue" icon="scale"

## Equity versus the spending base

The reason the distinction matters is that the structure of a tax shapes income distribution and the equity-efficiency trade-off.

A **progressive** system redistributes: it narrows the gap between post-tax incomes and funds public services, which improves vertical equity (those with more ability to pay, pay proportionally more). The cost is potential disincentive effects — very high marginal rates may blunt the incentive to work, earn or invest at the top.

A **regressive** tax like VAT does the opposite to the income distribution, taking proportionally more from the poor. But it has offsetting advantages governments value: it is harder to avoid, it does not directly tax effort or saving, and it has a broad, stable base. That is why the UK leans on VAT for revenue while using zero-rating and exemptions (food, children's clothing, domestic energy at a reduced rate) to blunt the regressive bite on essentials.

The category, in short, is not a verdict — it is the start of an equity-versus-efficiency argument.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## Measured how, and over what period?

Whether a tax is regressive depends on *how you measure it*, and saying so is a strong evaluation move.

VAT looks clearly regressive against *current annual income*. But measured against *spending* — or against lifetime income, since people borrow when young and save in middle age — it looks closer to proportional, because lifetime income and lifetime spending track each other more closely than a single year's snapshot. So "VAT is regressive" is true on the standard annual-income measure but softens on a lifetime view.

The progressive side has its own "however". Headline marginal rates can overstate progressivity if the system is riddled with allowances, reliefs and avoidance routes that the highest earners use most. A nominally progressive income tax can be less progressive in practice than the band structure suggests.

So the evaluation is not "progressive good, regressive bad" but "the real burden depends on the measure used, the time horizon, and how much avoidance the system permits." Stating which of those you are assuming is what turns a definition into a judgement.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## Track the average rate, name the example

For **AO1 knowledge**, define each term by the **average tax rate**, not the cash paid: progressive means the average rate rises with income, proportional means it is constant, regressive means it falls. Pairing each with a UK example — income tax, a flat tax, VAT — shows application alongside the definition.

For **AO4 evaluation**, push on the measure: "VAT is regressive against annual income, *but* closer to proportional over a lifetime, so the equity case for zero-rating essentials depends on which measure policymakers prioritise." That move — questioning *how* progressivity is measured rather than just labelling the tax — is the judgement that reaches the top band.

:::
