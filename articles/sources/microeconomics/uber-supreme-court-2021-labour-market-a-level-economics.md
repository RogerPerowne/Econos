## A court redraws the gig labour market

In February 2021 the UK Supreme Court ruled in *Uber BV v Aslam* that Uber drivers are **workers**, not self-employed contractors. Worker status is an intermediate category in UK law: it does not make someone a full employee, but it does carry the **national minimum wage** and **paid holiday**.

The court went further on a detail that matters economically: it held that a driver's working time runs from when they log on to the app and are available, not only when they have a passenger in the car. That widens the period that must be paid.

In economic terms, a regulator decided the gig labour market was not a clean meeting of free agents but one in which a dominant platform set the terms — and imposed a floor on the price of that labour.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The mechanism" color="blue" icon="target"

## Competitive market or monopsony?

The whole analysis hinges on one question: what kind of labour market is this?

In a **competitive labour market**, wages sit at the level where labour supply meets labour demand. Impose a wage floor above that point and the standard result follows: the quantity of labour demanded falls, the quantity supplied rises, and you get excess supply — unemployment. This is the chain students reach for by default: minimum wage up → labour more expensive → firms hire fewer → unemployment.

In a **monopsony**, a single dominant buyer of labour faces the whole upward-sloping supply curve, so hiring one more worker raises the wage for everyone. That makes the marginal cost of labour rise faster than the wage. A profit-maximising monopsonist hires where marginal cost of labour equals marginal revenue product — which leaves the wage *below*, and employment *lower than*, the competitive level. Here a wage floor changes the picture: it caps the marginal cost of labour, so the firm can raise the wage *and* hire more, up to the competitive point. Floor up → wage and employment both up.

If a platform with a dominant position was effectively setting pay below the competitive level, the monopsony case applies — and a regulatory floor can improve both pay and employment rather than destroy jobs.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Real world example" color="purple" icon="globe"

## What the ruling changed

After the 2021 judgment, the entitlements of worker status — minimum-wage pay and holiday — applied to drivers who had previously been treated as self-employed. The decision that available log-on time counts as working time was economically significant because it lengthened the paid period, raising the effective cost per active hour of engaging drivers.

For the firm, this is a rise in the cost of labour. The response options are the textbook ones: adjust fares (passing cost to consumers, depending on demand elasticity), adjust the number of drivers engaged, or change the balance between flexibility and guaranteed pay. The net effect on the number of drivers depends on how elastic the platform's demand for labour is and how much wage-setting power it held before the ruling.

The reason this case is exam gold is that it forces you off autopilot. You cannot just assert "minimum wage causes unemployment," because the gig economy is precisely the setting where a dominant buyer of labour may have been suppressing wages. The ruling is a real test of whether the market was competitive or monopsonistic — and the honest answer is that it sits somewhere between, which is exactly the nuance top-band answers carry.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## What the employment effect depends on

**Elasticity of labour demand.** If demand for drivers is highly elastic — riders are very price-sensitive and the platform can easily reduce the number of drivers — a cost floor risks cutting engagement. If demand is inelastic, the floor mostly raises pay with little change in numbers.

**Degree of monopsony power.** The stronger the platform's grip on pay before the ruling, the more room there is for the floor to raise wages without reducing employment. The weaker that grip, the closer we are to the competitive case where a floor costs jobs.

**The value of flexibility.** Gig work trades security for flexibility, and some workers value that flexibility highly. Regulation that raises pay but reduces flexibility is not unambiguously a gain — the distributional and welfare effects depend on what workers themselves prefer.

The evaluation move is to refuse the blanket verdict and say what it turns on: how much monopsony power existed, and how elastic labour demand is. That conditional is the difference between a Level 2 assertion and a Level 4 judgment.

:::

For **AO2 application**, anchor the case: *Uber v Aslam*, 2021, Supreme Court, drivers ruled workers entitled to minimum wage and holiday, with available time counted as working time.

For **AO3 analysis**, set up both diagrams — the competitive labour market (floor → unemployment) and the monopsony (floor → higher wage and employment). For **AO4 evaluation**, commit to a conditional: in a monopsonistic gig market a regulatory floor can raise pay without destroying jobs, *provided* labour demand is not too elastic. Diagnose the market structure before you predict the employment effect — that diagnosis is the evaluation.
