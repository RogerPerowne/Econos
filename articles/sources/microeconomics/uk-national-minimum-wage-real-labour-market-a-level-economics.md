## The model said jobs would go. They mostly didn't.

The UK introduced a national minimum wage in 1999, and since 2016 a higher **National Living Wage** has applied to older workers. The rates are recommended each year by the independent **Low Pay Commission**, which weighs raising low pay against the risk of harming jobs.

The basic competitive model is blunt about what a wage floor above the market-clearing wage should do: create excess supply of labour — in plain terms, unemployment. That prediction is what most students write, and it is exactly what more than two decades of UK evidence has largely failed to confirm. Pay at the bottom rose; the large job losses did not materialise.

That gap between the textbook prediction and the real outcome is the whole point of this case — and the explanation is the structure of low-wage labour markets.

:::

## Two diagrams, two opposite predictions

Everything depends on which labour market you assume.

In a **competitive labour market**, the wage sits where labour supply meets labour demand. Set a floor above it and the chain is: minimum wage up → wage above equilibrium → quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded → excess supply → unemployment. Higher pay for those who keep their jobs, fewer jobs overall.

In a **monopsony** — a market with a dominant buyer of labour, or more realistically employers with some wage-setting power — the picture flips. A monopsonist faces the whole upward-sloping labour supply curve, so hiring one more worker raises the wage for all. That makes the marginal cost of labour rise faster than the wage, and the firm hires where marginal cost of labour equals marginal revenue product — setting the wage *below* and employment *lower than* the competitive level. Now a wage floor caps the marginal cost of labour, so the firm can raise the wage *and* hire more, up to the competitive point. The chain becomes: floor up → marginal cost of labour capped → wage and employment both rise.

So the same policy can destroy jobs or create them. The mark-scheme move is to decide which market you are in before you predict the effect.

:::

::: econ-insight portraitKey="economistGoldin" tone="amber" label="Economist insight: Goldin on wages and labour markets" attribution="Claudia Goldin, Nobel laureate, on the economics of work and pay"
Pay is shaped not only by supply and demand but by the structure of the market and the bargaining power within it — which is why a single wage floor can have very different effects depending on how that market is organised.
:::

::: section eyebrow="Real world example" color="purple" icon="globe"

## What the UK evidence actually shows

When the minimum wage was introduced in 1999, a common fear was significant job losses among low-paid workers. The cautious, evidence-led approach of the Low Pay Commission — raising the floor in steps and watching the data — was built precisely to avoid that. Over the following decades, repeated upratings, including the introduction of the higher National Living Wage in 2016, raised pay at the bottom of the distribution while the large employment losses predicted by the competitive model generally did not appear.

This is why the UK minimum wage is such a powerful exam example. It lets you say more than "a floor causes unemployment." You can argue that the real-world outcome — higher pay, broadly maintained employment — is consistent with low-wage labour markets having **monopsony features**: employers with enough wage-setting power that pay was held below the competitive level, leaving room for a floor to raise wages without cutting jobs.

That does not mean a minimum wage can be set arbitrarily high with no consequences. Push the floor far enough above the competitive wage and even a monopsony model predicts employment falls. The Low Pay Commission's role is to find the level that captures the pay gains without crossing into that territory — which is itself an applied lesson in the limits of the monopsony argument.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## What the employment effect depends on

**How high the floor is set.** Even in a monopsony, a wage floor above the competitive level eventually reduces employment. The benign result holds only over the range where the floor is correcting employer wage-setting power, not exceeding the competitive wage.

**The degree of monopsony power.** The stronger the employer's grip on pay, the more room there is to raise wages without job losses. In genuinely competitive low-wage markets, the floor behaves more like the textbook case.

**Firm responses beyond headcount.** Firms can absorb higher wages through lower profits, higher prices, reduced hours, less training, or productivity gains. "No fall in employment" can hide adjustments on other margins, and the distributional effect depends on which margin moves.

The evaluation move is to say what the result turns on — the height of the floor relative to the competitive wage, and how much wage-setting power employers hold — rather than asserting the minimum wage is simply good or bad for jobs.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## Diagnose the market, then predict

For **AO2 application**, anchor the case: minimum wage from 1999, National Living Wage from 2016, rates recommended by the Low Pay Commission, and the broad evidence of higher pay without large job losses.

For **AO3 analysis**, draw both diagrams — competitive (floor → unemployment) and monopsony (floor → higher wage and employment). For **AO4 evaluation**, commit to a conditional: in a low-wage market with monopsony features, a moderate minimum wage can raise pay without costing jobs, *provided* it is not set above the competitive wage. Decide the market structure before you predict the effect — that is the evaluation.

:::
