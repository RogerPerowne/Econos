## 'Evaluate' is a judgement, not a balance

"Evaluate" is the command word that decides your grade on the long questions, and it is the one most often misread. Students see it and reach for a familiar shape: a paragraph of arguments for, a paragraph against, a polite conclusion that the issue is "complex". That shape feels balanced, and balance feels fair — so it is a genuine surprise when a fluent for-and-against answer scores in the middle bands.

The problem is that "evaluate" does not mean "give both sides". On the Edexcel A specification it is the command word for AO4, and AO4 rewards a *reasoned, supported judgement*. The mark scheme reserves its top bands for answers that weigh the arguments and come down on one side, with a reason. Balance is the raw material; judgement is the thing being assessed. An answer that gathers the material and never makes the judgement has done the homework and skipped the exam.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The criteria" color="purple" icon="target"

## Magnitude, time lag, context

Judgement is not a vibe. You make it by applying a criterion — a yardstick that tells you which argument should win. Three do most of the work, and naming the one you are using is half the mark.

**Magnitude — how big is the effect?** Two effects pull in opposite directions; the one that matters is the larger. A subsidy might raise output a little and cost the taxpayer a lot, or the reverse. Saying which effect dominates, and roughly why, is evaluation.

**Time lag — when does it bite?** Many policies have a short-run cost and a long-run benefit, or vice versa. Supply-side reforms may depress demand now and lift productivity later. If the question is about the next year, the lag changes the verdict; if it is about a decade, it changes it the other way. The judgement turns on which horizon matters.

**Context — which assumption actually holds?** Most chains rest on an assumption: a particular elasticity, a state of confidence, a degree of competition. Evaluation interrogates that assumption against the case in front of you. If demand is inelastic, a tax raises revenue but barely cuts consumption; if it is elastic, the reverse. The answer depends on which holds here — so say which.

Pick one of these, apply it, and you have a reason for your judgement instead of a hunch.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The empty version" color="blue" icon="scale"

## 'It depends' — on what?

The phrase that masquerades as evaluation is "it depends". On its own it is worth nothing: it gestures at conditionality without supplying any. Every answer in economics "depends" on something. The mark is for naming the *something*.

Compare two sentences. "Whether the sugar tax works depends on a lot of factors" is an empty hedge — it could be glued onto any policy answer in the subject. "Whether the sugar tax cuts consumption depends on the price elasticity of demand for sugary drinks: because demand is fairly inelastic, the tax raises revenue effectively but changes consumption only modestly, so the revenue motive dominates the health motive here" is evaluation. It names the decisive condition (elasticity), reads it against the case, and uses it to prioritise one effect over the other.

The test for any "it depends" sentence: have you said what it depends ON, and have you then committed? If not, it is not yet doing AO4 work.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Landing the judgement" color="green" icon="globe"

## The conclusion has to choose

The final move is the one students most often dodge: the conclusion has to choose. A conclusion that restates both sides and shrugs leaves AO4 unfinished. A conclusion that picks a side, on a stated criterion, completes it.

You are not pretending the losing argument disappears. You are saying that, weighed on magnitude or time lag or context, one side is stronger *in this case*. "On balance, because demand for the good is inelastic, the tax is more effective as a revenue measure than as a tool to cut consumption — so if the government's primary aim is public health, a tax alone is unlikely to be sufficient and would need pairing with information provision." That sentence judges, justifies, and even points forward. It is the difference between an answer that ends and an answer that concludes.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## The evaluation routine

Weigh on a named criterion, say what it depends on, and commit.

For **AO4**, stop treating "evaluate" as "list both sides". Build the balance, then judge it: pick magnitude, time lag, or context as your yardstick and apply it explicitly. Convert every "it depends" into "it depends on X, and here X means Y". Then write a conclusion that chooses a side and gives the reason — the supported judgement the mark scheme reserves its top bands for. The students who reach those bands are not the most balanced; they are the ones who balance and then decide.

:::
