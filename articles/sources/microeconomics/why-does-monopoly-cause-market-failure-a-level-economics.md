::: section eyebrow="The confusion" color="green" icon="bulb"

## "High prices" is not the answer

Asked why monopoly causes market failure, most students reach for "because prices are high". That cannot be the whole story — a perfectly competitive industry with high input costs also has high prices, and nobody calls that a market failure. High prices are a symptom, not the mechanism.

The real failure is precise and it is the source of the marks: a monopoly sets **price above marginal cost** and **restricts output below the level that maximises society's welfare**. Because resources are not flowing to where they are most valued, the market **misallocates** them — and misallocation is exactly what "market failure" means. Get to *P > MC* and *restricted output* and you have named the failure; stop at "prices are high" and you have described a symptom.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The mechanism" color="purple" icon="target"

## Allocative inefficiency: price above marginal cost

Start from how a monopoly chooses output. Like any profit-maximising firm it produces where **marginal revenue equals marginal cost** (MR = MC). But a monopoly faces the whole downward-sloping demand curve, so its **marginal revenue is below its average revenue** (the price). When it sets output at MR = MC and then reads the price off the demand curve above that output, the **price ends up above marginal cost**: P > MC.

That single inequality is the failure. The price measures what consumers value an extra unit at; marginal cost measures what it costs society in resources to make it. When **P > MC**, there are units that consumers value *more* than they cost to produce — society would gain by making them — but the monopoly does not, because expanding output would erode its margin. The chain is:

monopoly faces downward demand → MR < AR → produces where MR = MC → output restricted → P > MC → units consumers value above their resource cost go unmade → welfare lost.

Compare a perfectly competitive market, which produces where **P = MC** — the **allocatively efficient** point, where the value of the last unit exactly equals its cost. The monopoly stops short of it. The lost consumer and producer surplus on the units between the monopoly output and the competitive output is the **deadweight welfare loss** — the standard triangle in the exam diagram, and the visible measure of the misallocation.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The other failures" color="blue" icon="globe"

## Productive and X-inefficiency

Allocative inefficiency is the headline, but a monopoly typically fails on cost grounds too.

**Productive inefficiency.** A productively efficient firm produces at the bottom of its average cost curve, where AC is minimised. A monopoly maximising profit where MR = MC has no reason to land at that minimum-cost output, so it usually produces at a higher average cost than is technically necessary — wasting resources per unit.

**X-inefficiency.** Sheltered from competition, a monopoly faces weak pressure to keep costs down. Slack creeps in: overstaffing, gold-plated head offices, low-effort management. Costs drift *above* the lowest attainable curve. This is X-inefficiency — a failure that exists precisely *because* there is no rival to discipline it, which a contestable or competitive market would punish.

Taken together — allocative inefficiency (P > MC, restricted output, deadweight loss), productive inefficiency (not at minimum AC), and X-inefficiency (slack costs) — these are the routes by which monopoly misallocates and wastes society's resources. That is the market failure, stated in full.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## When monopoly might not be a failure at all

The top band qualifies the claim rather than asserting it. Three counter-arguments do the heavy lifting.

**Economies of scale.** A large monopoly can spread fixed costs over huge output, reaching lower average costs than many small firms could. For a **natural monopoly** (water, rail track, the grid) splitting the industry would *raise* costs — so a single firm, properly regulated, may deliver lower prices than fragmentation. Static inefficiency can be outweighed by scale.

**Dynamic efficiency.** Supernormal profit is not pure waste; it can fund research and development and innovation. Schumpeter argued that the prospect of monopoly profit is precisely what drives firms to innovate, and that the resulting "creative destruction" delivers gains a static diagram misses entirely. Pharmaceutical patents are a deliberate, temporary monopoly granted for exactly this reason.

**Regulation changes the verdict.** A monopoly disciplined by a regulator (Ofgem, Ofwat) or threatened by **contestable-market** entry may be pushed towards competitive prices and efficiency without the costs of fragmentation.

So the evaluation says what the verdict *depends on*: the size of the deadweight loss versus the scale economies, whether profit actually funds innovation, and how effective the regulatory regime is. "Monopoly causes market failure" is the default; whether it is a *net* failure is the judgement.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## Name P > MC, then weigh static against dynamic

For AO3 analysis, the marks live in the inequality: state that the monopoly produces where MR = MC, that because MR < AR this gives P > MC, and that restricted output below the allocatively efficient point creates a deadweight welfare loss. A labelled diagram with the loss triangle shaded clinches it.

For AO4 evaluation, refuse to treat the failure as automatic. The strongest answers weigh the **static** inefficiency (allocative, productive, X-inefficiency) against **dynamic and scale** considerations (economies of scale, R&D-funded innovation, the effect of regulation or contestability) and reach a conditional judgement — saying *what* the net welfare effect depends on, not merely that "it depends".

:::
