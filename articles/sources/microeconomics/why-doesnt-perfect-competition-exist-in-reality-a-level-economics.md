## A model built from four strict assumptions

Perfect competition is defined by four assumptions, and the reason it does not exist is that real markets break at least one of them:

1. **A large number of buyers and sellers**, each so small that no one can move the market price — every firm is a *price taker*.
2. **Homogeneous products** — every firm's good is identical, so buyers have no reason to prefer one seller.
3. **Freedom of entry and exit** — no barriers stop new firms entering or existing firms leaving.
4. **Perfect information** — every buyer and seller knows prices, products and technology fully.

Each assumption is demanding on its own. Requiring all four to hold *at once* is what makes the model an idealisation. Real firms advertise to make their products feel different, guard information for advantage, and operate behind entry barriers — so the conditions never line up perfectly.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The big picture · why each assumption fails" color="purple" icon="target"

## The assumptions break one by one

Walk through how the real world violates each condition.

**Homogeneous products** is the first to go. Branding, advertising and product differentiation exist precisely to make a firm's good feel distinct — so a customer pays more for Coca-Cola than a supermarket cola that is chemically similar. The moment a product is differentiated, the firm gains some power to set its own price, and the price-taker assumption falls with it.

**Perfect information** rarely holds. Sellers usually know more about their product's quality than buyers (think used cars or insurance), and firms guard cost and technology data as a competitive edge. Information asymmetry is the norm, not the exception.

**Freedom of entry and exit** is limited by **barriers to entry** — high start-up costs, patents, brand loyalty, economies of scale, regulation. These let incumbents earn supernormal profit without being competed away, which the model says is impossible in the long run.

**A large number of small sellers** holds in some markets but not most; many industries are dominated by a few large firms (oligopoly) or one (monopoly). When even one assumption fails, the market is no longer perfectly competitive — and in practice several fail at once.

So the closest real cases — foreign exchange, some agricultural commodity markets — are *near*-perfect at best. None is the genuine article.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The payoff · why teach a model of nothing" color="blue" icon="scale"

## It's a benchmark, not a description

If no market is perfectly competitive, why does every A-level course start there? Because the model is the **efficiency benchmark** against which every other structure is judged.

In long-run equilibrium, a perfectly competitive market achieves two prized outcomes at once. It is **allocatively efficient**: price equals marginal cost, so the value society places on the last unit equals what it costs to produce — resources go where consumers want them. And it is **productively efficient**: competition forces firms to the lowest point of their average cost curve, so nothing is wasted. On top of that, free entry competes away supernormal profit, so consumers are not overcharged.

That ideal is the ruler. When you analyse a monopoly restricting output and charging P above MC, or an oligopoly with stable prices, the comparison that earns marks is *against perfect competition*: how much allocative and productive efficiency is lost to market power. Teaching the model that does not exist is what lets you measure the cost of the markets that do.

:::

::: section eyebrow="Evaluation · the 'however'" color="pink" icon="scale"

## The benchmark has blind spots

Using perfect competition as the gold standard is itself open to challenge, and saying so lifts an answer into the top band.

First, perfect competition assumes away **economies of scale and dynamic efficiency**. Tiny price-taking firms may be too small to exploit scale economies or to fund research and development. A monopoly or oligopoly, for all its allocative inefficiency, may achieve lower long-run costs or invest more in innovation — so "perfectly competitive" is not automatically "best for society over time".

Second, the model is **static**. It judges efficiency at a point in time and ignores the role supernormal profit can play in funding investment and product improvement. Schumpeter's argument that competition is really a process of innovation by large firms directly contradicts the perfect-competition ideal.

So the evaluation is not "the benchmark is useless" but "it captures static efficiency well and dynamic efficiency badly." Whether perfect competition is the right yardstick depends on whether the market in question is driven by price and quantity (where it shines) or by innovation and scale (where it misleads).

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## Name the broken assumption, then the cost

For **AO2 application**, when a question gives you a real market, the move is to identify *which* assumption fails and why — "the supermarket sector breaks the homogeneous-product and large-number assumptions, so firms are price makers, not takers." Resist the temptation to call any market perfectly competitive; examiners reward "near-perfect" with the caveat far more than a false claim.

For **AO4 evaluation**, use perfect competition explicitly as the benchmark — "compared with the perfectly competitive ideal of P=MC, this monopoly is allocatively inefficient" — and then qualify the benchmark itself: "*however*, perfect competition ignores economies of scale and dynamic efficiency, so the monopoly may still deliver lower costs or more innovation over time." That two-step is the judgement the model is designed to support.

:::
