::: section eyebrow="The confusion" color="green" icon="bulb"

## The rule you can't explain

You already know the rule: for a monopoly, marginal revenue lies below average revenue. The trouble starts when an examiner asks you to *explain why* — and the honest answer most students give is a slightly louder restatement of the rule.

The reason is short and it is the whole article. A monopoly is a **price maker** facing the **downward-sloping market demand curve**. There is no separate "firm demand" sitting flat at the market price, because the firm *is* the market. So if it wants to sell one more unit, it has to move down that demand curve — and that means cutting the price not just on the extra unit, but on every unit it was already selling.

That price cut on the existing units is the missing piece. It is why MR is dragged below AR.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The definitions, tightly" color="purple" icon="target"

## AR is the price; MR is the change in total revenue

Two definitions do all the work.

**Average revenue (AR)** is total revenue divided by quantity. Total revenue is price × quantity, so AR = (P × Q) ÷ Q = P. For a firm that charges a single price, **AR equals the price**, which means the AR curve is literally the demand curve. When you draw a monopoly's demand curve, you have drawn its AR curve.

**Marginal revenue (MR)** is the change in total revenue from selling one more unit. For a price taker in perfect competition, the firm can sell as much as it likes at the going price, so each extra unit adds exactly the price to revenue — MR equals AR equals price, all flat.

A monopoly is different precisely because it does not take the price as given. It sets price by choosing where on the demand curve to sit.

:::

::: section eyebrow="The mechanism" color="blue" icon="scale"

## Why the price cut on all units pulls MR down

Walk a concrete case. Suppose a monopoly can sell 10 units at £10 each, so total revenue is £100. To sell an 11th unit, the demand curve says it must drop the price to, say, £9 — and that £9 applies to all 11 units, not just the new one.

Now total revenue is 11 × £9 = £99. Selling one *more* unit has *reduced* total revenue by £1, so marginal revenue here is negative. The arithmetic splits into two opposing effects:

- The **output effect**: the extra unit sells for £9, adding revenue. Positive.
- The **price effect**: the £1 price cut applies to the 10 units already being sold, losing £10 of revenue. Negative.

MR is the *net* of those two. Because the price effect bites on every existing unit, MR is always below the price (AR) once output is beyond the first unit. The more units already being sold, the bigger the price-effect loss, so MR falls faster than AR as output rises.

For a straight-line demand curve this is exact. If demand is P = a − bQ, then TR = aQ − bQ², and MR = a − 2bQ. MR shares demand's intercept (a) but has *twice* the slope (2b). That is why, in every textbook diagram, the MR line starts at the same point on the price axis as demand but hits the horizontal axis at half the quantity.

For a price taker, by contrast, there is no price effect — the firm never has to cut price to sell more — so MR stays equal to AR. The gap between MR and AR is exactly the cost of the monopolist's market power showing up in the revenue side.

:::

::: section eyebrow="What examiners actually want" color="purple" icon="cap"

## Name the price effect, then use it

For AO1/AO2, state the definitions cleanly: AR equals price equals the demand curve; MR is the change in total revenue per extra unit. For the AO3 explanation of why MR < AR, the marks are in the **price effect** — "to sell one more unit the monopolist must lower price on all units already sold, so the revenue gained on the extra unit is partly offset by revenue lost on the rest, dragging MR below AR."

The follow-on move that separates a top answer: link MR to the profit-maximising rule. A monopoly produces where MR = MC, but because MR < AR, the profit-maximising price (read off the demand curve above that output) sits *above* MR and above MC — which is the root of the allocative inefficiency you will be asked about next.

:::
