:::section eyebrow="The definition" color="green" icon="bulb"

## What inflation actually is

**Inflation is a sustained rise in the general price level** — and, equivalently, a fall in the purchasing power of money. The key word is *sustained*: a one-off jump in the price of one good is not inflation; a broad, ongoing rise across the economy is.

It is measured as a **percentage change over 12 months**. So "inflation is 4%" means the average price level is 4% higher than a year ago — the same shopping that cost £100 then costs about £104 now.

Two related ideas are worth keeping straight:

- **Disinflation** — inflation that is still positive but *slowing* (say 9% down to 4%). Prices are still rising, just less quickly.
- **Deflation** — a *negative* rate: the price level is actually falling. That sounds appealing, but it can be dangerous, because households delay purchases and the real burden of debt rises.

:::

:::section eyebrow="Measurement" color="blue" icon="scale"

## How it is measured: CPI

The UK's headline measure is the **Consumer Prices Index (CPI)**. The Office for National Statistics:

1. Tracks the price of a representative **basket** of around 700 goods and services.
2. **Weights** each item by its share of typical household spending — so a 10% rise in rent moves CPI far more than a 10% rise in postage stamps.
3. Reports the **percentage change** in that weighted index over the past year.

Because the basket is weighted, the headline rate reflects what households actually spend their money on — and the ONS updates the basket each year as habits change.

:::

:::section eyebrow="Cause 1" color="pink" icon="spark"

## Demand-pull inflation

Demand-pull inflation is "too much money chasing too few goods." When **aggregate demand (AD)** rises faster than the economy can expand supply, the extra spending bids prices up:

:::econos-diagram svgKey="adAsDemandPull" caption="Demand-pull inflation: a rise in any component of AD (C, I, G or X−M) shifts AD right along an upward-sloping AS, lifting real output and the price level from P₁ to P₂."

Typical triggers are a consumer-spending boom, a surge in investment, higher government spending, a tax cut, or rapid credit growth. The common thread: the pressure starts on the **demand** side.

:::

:::section eyebrow="Cause 2" color="amber" icon="cap"

## Cost-push inflation

Cost-push inflation comes from the **supply** side: the costs of production rise, so firms raise prices to protect their margins — even when demand is flat. On a diagram, short-run aggregate supply shifts *left*, raising the price level while output falls:

:::econos-diagram svgKey="adAsCostPush" caption="Cost-push inflation: higher production costs shift SRAS left, so the price level rises from P₁ to P₂ while real output contracts."

Classic drivers include:

- **Energy and commodity shocks** — the 2022 surge in gas prices is the textbook case.
- **Higher wages** not matched by productivity.
- **A weaker pound**, which raises the price of imported raw materials.
- **Higher taxes on production**, such as VAT or carbon levies.

:::callout variant="note"

**Exam move:** the same headline rate can have opposite cures. Demand-pull inflation responds to tighter demand-side policy; cost-push inflation may need supply-side measures, because cooling demand into a supply shock risks recession. Always *diagnose the cause before you prescribe the policy.*

:::

:::

:::section eyebrow="Why it matters" color="purple" icon="target"

## The costs of inflation

A little inflation is healthy — the target is 2%, not 0%. It is **high or volatile** inflation that does damage:

- **Erodes real incomes** if wages lag behind prices, hitting low-income households hardest.
- **Creates uncertainty**, so firms delay investment and the economy's long-run growth suffers.
- **Hurts savers and lenders** while quietly benefiting borrowers, redistributing wealth arbitrarily.
- **Worsens competitiveness** if domestic prices rise faster than those of trading partners.

:::

:::section eyebrow="The policy response" color="blue" icon="globe"

## How the Bank of England controls it

The Bank of England has a mandate to keep CPI inflation at **2%**. Its main lever is **Bank Rate**, set each month by the Monetary Policy Committee. Step through the two cases:

:::econos-diagram svgKey="adAsMonetary" interactive="true" caption="Inflation above target → raise Bank Rate to pull AD left and cool prices. Inflation too low with rates near zero → quantitative easing shifts AD right."

When inflation is **too high**, the MPC raises Bank Rate: borrowing becomes dearer, saving more attractive, and the pound tends to strengthen — together cooling aggregate demand. When inflation is **too low**, it cuts rates, or uses **quantitative easing** if rates are already near zero.

:::econ-insight portraitKey="economistFriedman" tone="amber" label="Economist insight" attribution="Milton Friedman"

Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon, in the sense that it is and can be produced only by a more rapid increase in the quantity of money than in output.

:::

The monetarist view is a useful counterweight: control the growth of the money supply and you control inflation. In practice central banks target the interest rate rather than the money stock — but the underlying discipline is the same.

:::
