## Qualification at a glance

The AS (one-year, 7135) and the linear A-level (two-year, 7136) are stand-alone qualifications that can be taught together or independently.

- **Section 1 (AS) — The operation of markets and market failure** (microeconomics)
- **Section 2 (AS) — The national economy in a global context** (macroeconomics)

## Section 3 — Subject content (AS)

### 3.1 The operation of markets and market failure

Primarily microeconomics. Students apply microeconomic models (demand and supply, the price mechanism, market failure) to current problems and issues, with critical awareness of model assumptions and their limitations — including environmental market failures and the influence of macroeconomic developments on individual markets.

#### 3.1.1 Economic methodology and the economic problem

- **3.1.1.1 Economic methodology** — (a) economics as a social science. (b) similarities to and differences in methodology from natural and other sciences; how thinking as an economist may differ from other forms of scientific enquiry. (c) the difference between positive and normative statements. (d) how value judgements influence economic decision making and policy. (e) people's views concerning the best option are influenced by the positive consequences of different decisions and by moral and political judgements.
- **3.1.1.2 The nature and purpose of economic activity** — (a) the central purpose of economic activity is the production of goods and services to satisfy needs and wants. (b) the key economic decisions are: what to produce, how to produce, and who is to benefit from the goods and services produced.
- **3.1.1.3 Economic resources** — (a) the economists' classification of economic resources into land, labour, capital and enterprise — the factors of production. (b) the environment is a scarce resource.
- **3.1.1.4 Scarcity, choice and the allocation of resources** — (a) the fundamental economic problem is scarcity, resulting from limited resources and unlimited wants. (b) scarcity means choices have to be made about how scarce resources are allocated between different uses. (c) choices have an opportunity cost.
- **3.1.1.5 Production possibility diagrams** — (a) production possibility diagrams illustrate different features of the fundamental economic problem: resource allocation, opportunity cost and trade-offs, unemployment of economic resources, economic growth. (b) why all points on the boundary are productively efficient but not all points on the boundary are allocatively efficient.

#### 3.1.2 Price determination in a competitive market

- **3.1.2.1 The determinants of the demand for goods and services** — (a) the factors which determine the demand for a good or service, including price, income, wealth, the price of substitutes and complementary goods, and individual preferences; recognition that decisions are also influenced by social and emotional factors. (b) a demand curve shows the relationship between price and quantity demanded. (c) the causes of shifts in the demand curve.
- **3.1.2.2 Price, income and cross elasticities of demand** — (a) calculation of price, income and cross elasticities of demand. (b) the relationship between income elasticity of demand and normal and inferior goods. (c) the relationship between cross elasticity of demand and substitute and complementary goods. (d) the relationships between price elasticity of demand and firms' total revenue (total expenditure). (e) the factors that influence these elasticities of demand. (f) interpretation of numerical values.
- **3.1.2.3 The determinants of the supply of goods and services** — (a) the factors which determine the supply of a good or service. (b) a supply curve shows the relationship between price and quantity supplied. (c) higher prices imply higher profits and provide the incentive to expand production. (d) the causes of shifts in the supply curve.
- **3.1.2.4 Price elasticity of supply** — (a) calculation of price elasticity of supply. (b) the factors that influence price elasticity of supply. (c) interpretation of numerical values.
- **3.1.2.5 The determination of equilibrium market prices** — (a) how the interaction of demand and supply determines equilibrium prices in a market economy. (b) the difference between equilibrium and disequilibrium. (c) why excess demand and excess supply lead to changes in price. (d) application of the basic demand and supply model to a variety of real-world markets.
- **3.1.2.6 The interrelationship between markets** — (a) changes in a particular market are likely to affect other markets. (b) the implications of joint demand, demand for substitute goods, composite demand, derived demand and joint supply.

#### 3.1.3 Production, costs and revenue

- **3.1.3.1 Production and productivity** — (a) production converts inputs, or the services of factors of production such as capital and labour, into final output. (b) the meaning of productivity, including labour productivity.
- **3.1.3.2 Specialisation, division of labour and exchange** — (a) the benefits of specialisation and division of labour. (b) why specialisation necessitates an efficient means of exchanging goods and services, such as the use of money as a medium of exchange.
- **3.1.3.3 Costs of production** — (a) the difference between the short run and the long run. (b) the difference between fixed and variable costs. (c) the difference between average and total costs; calculation from given data. (d) the short-run average cost curve is likely to be U-shaped; the shape of the long-run average cost curve is determined by economies and diseconomies of scale.
- **3.1.3.4 Economies and diseconomies of scale** — (a) the difference between internal and external economies of scale, with examples. (b) reasons for diseconomies of scale. (c) the relationship between economies of scale, diseconomies of scale and the shape of the long-run average cost curve.
- **3.1.3.5 Average revenue, total revenue and profit** — (a) the difference between average and total revenue. (b) why the average revenue curve is the firm's demand curve. (c) profit is the difference between total revenue and total costs. (d) calculation of average revenue, total revenue and profit from given data.

#### 3.1.4 Competitive and concentrated markets

- **3.1.4.1 Market structures** — (a) there is a range of market structures. (b) factors such as the number of firms, the degree of product differentiation and ease of entry are used to distinguish between different market structures. (Traditional theory-of-the-firm diagrams not required at AS.)
- **3.1.4.2 The objectives of firms** — (a) profit is an important objective of most firms. (b) firms may also have other objectives such as survival, growth and increasing their market share; the objectives of a firm will affect its behaviour.
- **3.1.4.3 Competitive markets** — (a) the main characteristics of a perfectly competitive market. (b) in such markets the price is determined by the interaction of demand and supply. (c) why profits are likely to be lower in a competitive market than in a market dominated by a few large firms. (Formal model and diagrams of perfect competition not required.)
- **3.1.4.4 Monopoly and monopoly power** — (a) the difference between pure monopoly and monopoly power; few examples of pure monopoly but many firms have monopoly power. (b) monopoly power is influenced by factors such as barriers to entry, the number of competitors, advertising and the degree of product differentiation. (c) concentration ratios and how to calculate a concentration ratio. (d) the basic model of monopoly suggests that higher prices and profits and inefficiency may result in a misallocation of resources compared to a competitive market — use of a demand curve to show that raising price above the competitive level reduces output. (e) potential benefits from monopoly, e.g. economies of scale (illustrated via the long-run average cost curve) and possibly more invention and innovation.
- **3.1.4.5 The competitive market process** — (a) firms compete on more than price — they strive to improve products, reduce costs, improve service quality. (b) recognition that many large firms compete vigorously but monopoly power may lead to consumer exploitation.

#### 3.1.5 The market mechanism, market failure and government intervention in markets

- **3.1.5.1 How markets and prices allocate resources** — (a) the rationing, incentive and signalling functions of prices in allocating resources and coordinating buyers and sellers in a market economy. (b) the price mechanism is the way in which the basic economic problem is resolved in a market economy.
- **3.1.5.2 The meaning of market failure** — (a) market failure occurs whenever a market leads to a misallocation of resources. (b) what is meant by a misallocation of resources. (c) the difference between complete market failure (resulting in a missing market) and partial market failure (where a market exists but contributes to misallocation). (d) how public goods, positive and negative externalities, merit and demerit goods, monopoly and other market imperfections, and inequalities in income and wealth can lead to market failure.
- **3.1.5.3 Public goods, private goods and quasi-public goods** — (a) pure public goods are non-rival and non-excludable; significance of these characteristics. (b) the difference between a public good and a private good. (c) circumstances when a public good takes on characteristics of a private good and becomes a quasi-public good. (d) the significance of technological change (e.g. television broadcasting is now excludable). (e) the free-rider problem.
- **3.1.5.4 Positive and negative externalities in consumption and production** — (a) externalities exist when there is a divergence between private and social costs and benefits. (b) why negative externalities are likely to result in over-production and positive externalities in under-production; illustrated using demand and supply diagrams (MSC/MSB diagrams not required at AS).
- **3.1.5.5 Merit and demerit goods** — (a) the classification of merit and demerit goods depends upon a value judgement. (b) such products may be subject to positive and negative externalities in consumption. (c) how under-provision of merit goods and over-provision of demerit goods may also result from imperfect information. (d) not all products with positive or negative externalities in consumption are either merit or demerit goods.
- **3.1.5.6 Market imperfections** — (a) why imperfect and asymmetric information can lead to market failure. (b) why the existence of monopoly and monopoly power can lead to market failure. (c) why the immobility of factors of production can lead to market failure.
- **3.1.5.7 An inequitable distribution of income and wealth** — (a) in the absence of government intervention, the market mechanism is likely to result in a very unequal and inequitable distribution. (b) an individual's ability to consume depends upon income and wealth, so an inequitable distribution is likely to lead to a misallocation of resources and hence market failure. (c) awareness that progressive taxes and government spending are used to reduce inequality.
- **3.1.5.8 Government intervention in markets** — (a) market failure provides an argument for government intervention. (b) governments influence resource allocation through public expenditure, taxation and regulations. (c) governments have a range of objectives that shape how they intervene in a mixed economy. (d) the use of indirect taxation, subsidies, price controls, state provision and regulation to correct market failure. (e) evaluation of the case for and against intervention and the relative merits of different methods.
- **3.1.5.9 Government failure** — (a) government failure occurs when intervention leads to a misallocation of resources. (b) inadequate information, conflicting objectives and administrative costs as possible sources. (c) governments may create, rather than remove, market distortions. (d) intervention can lead to unintended consequences. (e) asymmetric information between consumers and producers may contribute to markets operating inefficiently.

### 3.2 The national economy in a global context

Primarily macroeconomics, but underpinned by microeconomic principles (e.g. PED is essential when analysing how a fall in the exchange rate affects exports). Students should know UK developments and government policies over the past fifteen years and understand external influences on the UK economy.

#### 3.2.1 The measurement of macroeconomic performance

- **3.2.1.1 The objectives of government economic policy** — (a) main objectives: economic growth, price stability, minimising unemployment, a stable balance of payments on current account. (b) possibility of conflict, at least in the short run, when pursuing these objectives. (c) other possible objectives — balancing the budget, achieving an equitable distribution of income; the relative importance of objectives changes over time.
- **3.2.1.2 Macroeconomic indicators** — (a) data commonly used to measure economic performance: real GDP, real GDP per capita, CPI/RPI, measures of unemployment, productivity, the balance of payments on current account.
- **3.2.1.3 Uses of index numbers** — (a) how index numbers are calculated and interpreted, including the base year and the use of weights. (b) how index numbers are used to measure changes in the price level and other economic variables; awareness of CPI/RPI features such as the "average family" and a "basket of goods and services".

#### 3.2.2 How the macroeconomy works: the circular flow of income, AD/AS analysis and related concepts

- **3.2.2.1 The circular flow of income** — (a) what national income measures. (b) the difference between nominal and real income. (c) real national income as an indicator of economic performance. (d) the circular-flow concept; the identity income = output = expenditure; equilibrium and full-employment income. (e) the difference between injections and withdrawals. (f) the effect of changes in injections and withdrawals on national income.
- **3.2.2.2 Aggregate demand and aggregate supply analysis** — (a) changes in the price level are represented by movements along the AD and AS curves. (b) the factors that shift the AD curve and the short-run AS curve. (c) the factors which affect long-run AS, distinguishing them from those that affect short-run AS. (d) underlying economic growth is represented by a rightward shift in the long-run AS curve. (e) using AD/AS diagrams to illustrate macroeconomic equilibrium. (f) how both demand-side and supply-side shocks affect the macroeconomy; how global economic events can affect the domestic economy.
- **3.2.2.3 The determinants of aggregate demand** — (a) what is meant by AD. (b) determinants of consumption, investment, government spending, exports and imports. (c) the basic accelerator process (no calculations required). (d) determinants of savings. (e) the difference between saving and investment. (f) how changes in net exports affect AD and how global events affect economic performance.
- **3.2.2.4 Aggregate demand and the level of economic activity** — (a) the role of AD in influencing the level of economic activity. (b) the multiplier process and why an initial change in expenditure may lead to a larger impact on local or national income; calculation of the multiplier from an initial change in injections and the resulting change in national income (no MPC/MPW-based calculation required at AS).
- **3.2.2.5 Determinants of short-run aggregate supply** — (a) the price level and production costs are the main determinants. (b) changes in money wage rates, raw-material prices, business taxation and productivity will shift the short-run AS curve.
- **3.2.2.6 Determinants of long-run aggregate supply** — (a) fundamental determinants: technology, productivity, attitudes, enterprise, factor mobility, economic incentives. (b) the position of the vertical long-run AS curve represents the normal-capacity level of output. (At AS, long-run AS is assumed vertical.)

#### 3.2.3 Economic performance

- **3.2.3.1 Economic growth and the economic cycle** — (a) the difference between short-run and long-run growth, illustrated via PPC and AD/AS diagrams. (b) demand-side and supply-side determinants of short-run growth and the long-run trend rate of growth. (c) the economic cycle and the use of a range of indicators (real GDP, inflation, unemployment, investment) to identify its phases. (d) the difference between positive and negative output gaps. (e) how demand-side and supply-side shocks, including global ones, affect domestic economic activity.
- **3.2.3.2 Employment and unemployment** — (a) main UK measures: claimant count and the Labour Force Survey. (b) the terms seasonal, frictional, structural and cyclical unemployment; appropriate policies depend on the cause. (c) how employment and unemployment may be determined by both demand-side and supply-side factors. (d) how changes in the rest of the world affect UK employment and unemployment.
- **3.2.3.3 Inflation and deflation** — (a) the concepts of inflation and deflation; disinflation as a falling rate of inflation versus deflation as a falling price level. (b) demand-pull and cost-push influences on the price level. (c) how changes in world commodity prices affect domestic inflation. (d) how changes in other economies can affect UK inflation. (e) deflationary policies reduce AD and do not necessarily result in deflation.
- **3.2.3.4 The balance of payments on current account** — (a) the importance of international trade for an economy such as the UK. (b) components of the current account: trade in goods, trade in services, primary income, secondary income. (c) the meaning of a deficit and a surplus on current account. (d) the factors that influence a country's current-account balance such as productivity, inflation, the exchange rate and activity in other countries.
- **3.2.3.5 Possible conflicts between macroeconomic policy objectives** — (a) how negative and positive output gaps relate to unemployment and inflationary pressures. (b) how economic policies may be used to reconcile possible policy conflicts in both the short run and the long run, analysed via macroeconomic models including AD/AS.

#### 3.2.4 Macroeconomic policy

- **3.2.4.1 Monetary policy** — (a) monetary policy involves the central bank influencing interest rates, the supply of money and credit, and the exchange rate. (b) the current objectives of monetary policy set by the government. (c) the role of the Monetary Policy Committee of the Bank of England and how it uses changes in bank rate to achieve those objectives, including the government's target rate of inflation. (d) the factors considered by the MPC when setting bank rate. (e) how changes in the exchange rate affect AD and the various macroeconomic policy objectives.
- **3.2.4.2 Fiscal policy** — (a) fiscal policy involves the manipulation of government spending, taxation and the budget balance. (b) fiscal policy can have both macroeconomic and microeconomic functions. (c) how fiscal policy can be used to influence AD. (d) how fiscal policy can be used to influence AS. (e) how government spending and taxation can affect the pattern of economic activity; awareness of main UK taxes and main categories of government spending (defence, health, education, welfare). (f) the difference between direct and indirect taxes. (g) the difference between progressive, proportional and regressive taxes. (h) the relationship between the budget balance and the national debt; awareness that governments may deliberately run deficits and surpluses to influence AD.
- **3.2.4.3 Supply-side policies** — (a) the difference between supply-side policies and supply-side improvements in the economy. (b) how supply-side policies can help achieve supply-side improvements; recognition that improvements often originate in the private sector through productivity, innovation and investment. (c) how tax changes designed to change personal incentives may increase potential output and improve the underlying trend rate of growth. (d) how supply-side policies can affect unemployment, the rate of change of prices and UK external performance (current account). (e) examples of supply-side policies: government spending on education and training, cuts in income and corporation tax, welfare reform, industrial policy.

## Section 4 — Subject content (A-level)

The A-level extends and deepens the AS content. Section 4.1 is primarily microeconomics; Section 4.2 is primarily macroeconomics. Students should appreciate that micro and macro are not entirely distinct — microeconomic principles often provide fundamental insights into the macroeconomy, and many economic issues contain both dimensions.

### 4.1 Individuals, firms, markets and market failure

Microeconomic models — demand and supply, perfect competition, monopoly, the price mechanism, causes of market failure — are central. Students need a realistic understanding of firms' decisions and how behaviour is shaped by industry structure. Other key models cover labour-market operation, wage determination and inequality. Students must engage with traditional rational-agent theory and with behavioural models that recognise more complex influences on consumers and firms. They should assess the impact of EU and global developments on UK microeconomic behaviour and apply their knowledge to environmental and labour-market failures.

#### 4.1.1 Economic methodology and the economic problem

- **4.1.1.1 Economic methodology** — (a) economics as a social science. (b) similarities to and differences in methodology from natural and other sciences; how thinking as an economist may differ from other forms of scientific enquiry. (c) the difference between positive and normative statements. (d) how value judgements influence economic decision making and policy. (e) people's views concerning the best option are influenced by the positive consequences of different decisions and by moral and political judgements.
- **4.1.1.2 The nature and purpose of economic activity** — (a) the central purpose of economic activity is the production of goods and services to satisfy needs and wants. (b) the key economic decisions are: what to produce, how to produce and who is to benefit from the goods and services produced.
- **4.1.1.3 Economic resources** — (a) the economists' classification of economic resources into land, labour, capital and enterprise — the factors of production. (b) the environment is a scarce resource.
- **4.1.1.4 Scarcity, choice and the allocation of resources** — (a) the fundamental economic problem is scarcity, resulting from limited resources and unlimited wants. (b) scarcity means choices have to be made about how scarce resources are allocated between different uses. (c) choices have an opportunity cost.
- **4.1.1.5 Production possibility diagrams** — (a) PPDs illustrate features of the economic problem: resource allocation, opportunity cost and trade-offs, unemployment of resources, economic growth. (b) why all points on the boundary are productively efficient but not all points on the boundary are allocatively efficient.

#### 4.1.2 Individual economic decision making

- **4.1.2.1 Consumer behaviour** — (a) rational economic decision making and economic incentives. (b) utility theory: total and marginal utility, and the hypothesis of diminishing marginal utility (which supports a downward-sloping demand curve — equi-marginal utility not required). (c) utility maximisation. (d) the importance of the margin when making choices.
- **4.1.2.2 Imperfect information** — (a) the importance of information for decision making. (b) the significance of asymmetric information; imperfect information makes rational decisions difficult and is a potential source of market failure.
- **4.1.2.3 Aspects of behavioural economic theory** — (a) bounded rationality and bounded self-control. (b) biases in decision making: rules of thumb, anchoring, availability and social norms. (c) the importance of altruism and perceptions of fairness; behavioural economists question the assumption of fully rational utility-maximising agents.
- **4.1.2.4 Behavioural economics and economic policy** — (a) choice architecture and framing. (b) nudges. (c) default choices, restricted choice and mandated choice — insights from behavioural economics can help governments and other agencies influence economic decision making.

#### 4.1.3 Price determination in a competitive market

- **4.1.3.1 The determinants of the demand for goods and services** — (a) a demand curve shows the relationship between price and quantity demanded. (b) the causes of shifts in the demand curve.
- **4.1.3.2 Price, income and cross elasticities of demand** — (a) calculation of price, income and cross elasticities of demand. (b) the relationship between income elasticity of demand and normal and inferior goods. (c) the relationship between cross elasticity of demand and substitute and complementary goods. (d) the relationships between PED and firms' total revenue (total expenditure). (e) the factors that influence these elasticities of demand. (f) interpretation of numerical values.
- **4.1.3.3 The determinants of the supply of goods and services** — (a) a supply curve shows the relationship between price and quantity supplied. (b) higher prices imply higher profits and provide the incentive to expand production. (c) the causes of shifts in the supply curve. (d) under perfect competition the supply curve is the marginal cost curve.
- **4.1.3.4 Price elasticity of supply** — (a) calculation of PES. (b) the factors that influence PES. (c) interpretation of numerical values.
- **4.1.3.5 The determination of equilibrium market prices** — (a) how the interaction of demand and supply determines equilibrium prices in a market economy. (b) the difference between equilibrium and disequilibrium. (c) why excess demand and excess supply lead to changes in price. (d) application of the basic demand-and-supply model to a variety of real-world markets; awareness of the model's assumptions.
- **4.1.3.6 The interrelationship between markets** — (a) changes in a particular market are likely to affect other markets. (b) the implications of joint demand, competitive demand, composite demand, derived demand and joint supply.

#### 4.1.4 Production, costs and revenue

- **4.1.4.1 Production and productivity** — (a) production converts inputs, or the services of factors of production such as capital and labour, into final output. (b) the meaning of productivity, including labour productivity.
- **4.1.4.2 Specialisation, division of labour and exchange** — (a) the benefits of specialisation and division of labour. (b) why specialisation necessitates an efficient means of exchange, such as the use of money as a medium of exchange.
- **4.1.4.3 The law of diminishing returns and returns to scale** — (a) the difference between the short run and the long run. (b) the difference between marginal, average and total returns. (c) the law of diminishing returns. (d) returns to scale. (e) the difference between increasing, constant and decreasing returns to scale. Both diminishing returns and returns to scale explain relationships between inputs and output and have implications for costs of production.
- **4.1.4.4 Costs of production** — (a) the difference between fixed and variable costs. (b) the difference between marginal, average and total costs; calculation from given data. (c) the difference between short-run and long-run costs. (d) the reasons for the shape of the marginal, average and total cost curves. (e) how factor prices and productivity affect firms' costs of production and their choice of factor inputs.
- **4.1.4.5 Economies and diseconomies of scale** — (a) the difference between internal and external economies of scale, with examples. (b) reasons for diseconomies of scale. (c) the relationship between returns to scale and economies or diseconomies of scale. (d) the relationship between economies of scale, diseconomies of scale and the shape of the long-run average cost curve. (e) the L-shaped long-run average cost curve. (f) the concept of the minimum efficient scale of production and its significance for industry structure and barriers to entry.
- **4.1.4.6 Marginal, average and total revenue** — (a) the difference between marginal, average and total revenue; calculation from given data. (b) why the average revenue curve is the firm's demand curve. (c) the relationship between average and marginal revenue. (d) the relationship between marginal revenue and total revenue.
- **4.1.4.7 Profit** — (a) profit is the difference between total revenue and total costs. (b) the difference between normal and abnormal (supernormal) profit. (c) the role of profit in a market economy.
- **4.1.4.8 Technological change** — (a) the difference between invention and innovation. (b) technological change can affect methods of production, productivity, efficiency and firms' costs of production. (c) technological change can lead to new products, the development of new markets and may destroy existing markets. (d) technological change can influence the structure of markets, linked to the process of creative destruction.

#### 4.1.5 Perfect competition, imperfectly competitive markets and monopoly

- **4.1.5.1 Market structures** — (a) the spectrum of competition, ranging from perfect competition to pure monopoly. (b) factors such as the number of firms, the degree of product differentiation and ease of entry are used to distinguish between different market structures.
- **4.1.5.2 The objectives of firms** — (a) the traditional theory of the firm assumes firms aim to maximise profits. (b) the profit-maximising rule (MC = MR). (c) reasons for and consequences of a divorce of ownership from control — affecting objectives, conduct and performance. (d) firms have a variety of other possible objectives including survival, growth, quality, maximising sales revenue and increasing market share. (e) the satisficing principle.
- **4.1.5.3 Perfect competition** — (a) the formal diagrammatic analysis of the perfectly competitive model in the short and long run. (b) the implications of large numbers of producers, identical products, freedom of entry and exit, and perfect knowledge for firm and industry behaviour. (c) firms operating in perfectly competitive markets are price takers. (d) the proposition that, given certain assumptions (e.g. no externalities), perfect competition will result in an efficient allocation of resources — critically assessed; perfect competition as a yardstick for judging real-world market efficiency.
- **4.1.5.4 Monopolistic competition** — (a) the formal diagrammatic analysis of the monopolistically competitive model in the short and long run. (b) the main characteristics of monopolistically competitive markets. (c) such markets will be subject to non-price competition.
- **4.1.5.5 Oligopoly** — (a) the main characteristics of oligopolistic markets; recognition that oligopolies vary in number of firms, degree of product differentiation and ease of entry. (b) oligopoly defined in terms of market structure or market conduct (behaviour). (c) concentration ratios and how to calculate one. (d) the difference between collusive and non-collusive oligopoly. (e) the difference between cooperation and collusion. (f) the kinked demand curve model, used as an illustration of interdependence rather than as the sole model of oligopoly. (g) the reasons for non-price competition, the operation of cartels, price leadership, price agreements, price wars and barriers to entry. (h) the factors which influence prices, output, investment, expenditure on research and advertising in oligopolistic industries. (i) the significance of interdependence and uncertainty in oligopoly; collusion may allow oligopolists to act as a monopolist and maximise joint profits. (j) the advantages and disadvantages of oligopoly.
- **4.1.5.6 Monopoly and monopoly power** — (a) the formal diagrammatic analysis of the monopoly model. (b) monopoly power is influenced by factors such as barriers to entry, the number of competitors, advertising and the degree of product differentiation; firms in monopolistic competition and oligopoly are price makers with varying degrees of monopoly power. (c) the advantages and disadvantages of monopoly.
- **4.1.5.7 Price discrimination** — (a) the conditions necessary for price discrimination. (b) the advantages and disadvantages of price discrimination, with diagrammatic analysis and real-world examples; impact on producers and consumers.
- **4.1.5.8 The dynamics of competition and competitive market processes** — (a) both the short-run and long-run benefits which are likely to result from competition. (b) firms compete on more than price — improving products, reducing costs, improving service quality. (c) the process of creative destruction as a fundamental feature of how competition operates in a market economy: monopoly power and large profits attract new entrants and innovation to overcome existing barriers.
- **4.1.5.9 Contestable and non-contestable markets** — (a) the significance of market contestability for the performance of an industry. (b) concepts such as sunk costs and hit-and-run competition.
- **4.1.5.10 Market structure, static efficiency, dynamic efficiency and resource allocation** — (a) the difference between static efficiency and dynamic efficiency. (b) the conditions required for productive efficiency (minimising average total costs) and allocative efficiency (price = marginal cost). (c) dynamic efficiency is influenced by R&D, investment in human and non-human capital and technological change. (d) using conduct and performance indicators to compare market structures.
- **4.1.5.11 Consumer and producer surplus** — (a) application of these concepts when discussing economic efficiency and welfare issues such as price discrimination and the dead-weight losses associated with monopoly; diagrammatic analysis expected.

#### 4.1.6 The labour market

- **4.1.6.1 The demand for labour, marginal productivity theory** — (a) the demand for a factor is derived from the demand for the product. (b) the marginal productivity theory of the demand for labour. (c) the demand curve for labour shows the relationship between the wage rate and the number of workers employed. (d) the causes of shifts in the demand curve for labour. (e) the determinants of the elasticity of demand for labour.
- **4.1.6.2 Influences upon the supply of labour to different markets** — (a) the supply of labour to a particular occupation is influenced by monetary and non-monetary considerations. (b) non-monetary considerations include job satisfaction and dissatisfaction and working conditions. (c) the supply curve for labour shows the relationship between the wage rate and the number of workers willing to work in an occupation. (d) the causes of shifts in the market supply curve for labour (individual supply curve and backward-bending supply curve not required).
- **4.1.6.3 The determination of relative wage rates and levels of employment in perfectly competitive labour markets** — (a) the model of wage determination in a perfectly competitive labour market. (b) the role of market forces in determining relative wage rates; appreciation that real-world labour markets are imperfectly competitive to a greater or lesser extent.
- **4.1.6.4 The determination of relative wage rates and levels of employment in imperfectly competitive labour markets** — (a) how factors such as monopsony power, trade unions and imperfect information contribute to imperfections in a labour market. (b) how, in a monopsony labour market, the employer can use market power to reduce both the relative wage rate and the level of employment below those of a perfectly competitive labour market; relevant diagrams expected.
- **4.1.6.5 The influence of trade unions in determining wages and levels of employment** — (a) the factors that affect the ability of trade unions to influence wages and levels of employment in different labour markets. (b) how wages and employment are likely to be affected by the introduction of a trade union into a previously perfectly competitive labour market and into a monopsony labour market; relevant diagrams expected.
- **4.1.6.6 The National Minimum Wage** — (a) the effects of a national minimum wage upon labour markets. (b) the advantages and disadvantages of a national minimum wage.
- **4.1.6.7 Discrimination in the labour market** — (a) the conditions necessary for wage discrimination. (b) the impact of gender, ethnicity and other forms of discrimination on wages, levels and types of employment; advantages and disadvantages of wage discrimination for workers, employers and the economy as a whole.

#### 4.1.7 The distribution of income and wealth: poverty and inequality

- **4.1.7.1 The distribution of income and wealth** — (a) the difference between income and wealth. (b) the various factors which influence the distribution of income and wealth; some knowledge of UK household distribution expected. (c) the difference between equality and equity in relation to the distribution of income and wealth — equity involves a value judgement. (d) the Lorenz curve and Gini coefficient (interpretation of the Gini coefficient expected, calculation not required). (e) the likely benefits and costs of more equal and more unequal distributions; excessive inequality is both a cause and a consequence of market failure; value judgements shape views of equitable distribution and policy.
- **4.1.7.2 The problem of poverty** — (a) the difference between relative and absolute poverty. (b) the causes and effects of poverty.
- **4.1.7.3 Government policies to alleviate poverty and to influence the distribution of income and wealth** — (a) the policies available to influence the distribution of income and wealth and to alleviate poverty. (b) the economic consequences of such policies; evaluation of redistributive approaches recognising moral and political perspectives.

#### 4.1.8 The market mechanism, market failure and government intervention in markets

- **4.1.8.1 How markets and prices allocate resources** — (a) the rationing, incentive and signalling functions of prices in allocating resources and coordinating buyers and sellers. (b) the advantages and disadvantages of the price mechanism and of extending its use into new areas of activity; the price mechanism as an impersonal method of allocation; consequences when markets enter fields where they may change the nature of the activity (e.g. a market for blood).
- **4.1.8.2 The meaning of market failure** — (a) market failure occurs whenever a market leads to a misallocation of resources. (b) the difference between complete market failure (a missing market) and partial market failure (a market exists but contributes to misallocation). (c) how public goods, positive and negative externalities, merit and demerit goods, monopoly and other market imperfections, and inequalities in income and wealth can lead to market failure.
- **4.1.8.3 Public goods, private goods and quasi-public goods** — (a) pure public goods are non-rival and non-excludable. (b) the difference between a public good and a private good. (c) circumstances when a public good takes on characteristics of a private good and becomes a quasi-public good. (d) the significance of technological change (e.g. television broadcasting is now excludable). (e) the free-rider problem. (f) the tragedy of the commons and its relevance to environmental market failures.
- **4.1.8.4 Positive and negative externalities in consumption and production** — (a) externalities exist when there is a divergence between private and social costs and benefits. (b) why negative externalities are likely to result in over-production and positive externalities in under-production, illustrated using diagrams of marginal private and social cost and benefit curves. (c) why the absence of property rights leads to externalities in both production and consumption and hence market failure.
- **4.1.8.5 Merit and demerit goods** — (a) the classification of merit and demerit goods depends upon a value judgement. (b) such products may be subject to positive and negative externalities in consumption — illustrated with MSC/MSB diagrams. (c) how under-provision of merit goods and over-provision of demerit goods may also result from imperfect information; not all products with consumption externalities are merit or demerit goods.
- **4.1.8.6 Market imperfections** — (a) why imperfect and asymmetric information can lead to market failure. (b) why the existence of monopoly and monopoly power can lead to market failure. (c) why the immobility of factors of production can lead to market failure.
- **4.1.8.7 Competition policy** — (a) the general principles of UK competition policy and some awareness of EU competition policy. (b) the costs and benefits of such policies (detailed knowledge of UK and EU competition law not required).
- **4.1.8.8 Public ownership, privatisation, regulation and deregulation of markets** — (a) the arguments for and against the public ownership of firms and industries. (b) the arguments for and against the privatisation of state-owned enterprises. (c) the arguments for and against the regulation of markets. (d) the arguments for and against the deregulation of markets. (e) the problem of regulatory capture.
- **4.1.8.9 Government intervention in markets** — (a) market failure provides an argument for government intervention. (b) governments influence resource allocation through public expenditure, taxation and regulation. (c) governments have a range of objectives that shape how they intervene in a mixed economy. (d) the use of indirect taxation, subsidies, price controls, state provision and regulation, the extension of property rights and pollution permits to correct market failure. (e) evaluation of the case for and against intervention in particular markets and the relative merits of different methods of intervention.
- **4.1.8.10 Government failure** — (a) government failure occurs when intervention leads to a misallocation of resources. (b) inadequate information, conflicting objectives and administrative costs as possible sources. (c) governments may create, rather than remove, market distortions. (d) intervention can lead to unintended consequences; even when there is market failure, intervention will not necessarily improve economic welfare.

### 4.2 The national and international economy

Primarily macroeconomics, with microeconomic principles underpinning macroeconomic behaviour (e.g. PED is essential for analysing exchange-rate effects). Students should know UK developments and government policies of the past fifteen years, be aware of world economy developments including the EU and how these have affected the UK, and appreciate that different macro models offer different insights — with critical awareness of assumptions and limitations. They should propose, analyse and evaluate solutions to macroeconomic problems and assess current and alternative government policies.

#### 4.2.1 The measurement of macroeconomic performance

- **4.2.1.1 The objectives of government economic policy** — (a) main objectives: economic growth, price stability, minimising unemployment, a stable balance of payments on current account. (b) possibility of conflict, at least in the short run, when pursuing these objectives. (c) governments may also pursue other objectives, such as balancing the budget and achieving an equitable distribution of income; the importance attached to objectives changes over time.
- **4.2.1.2 Macroeconomic indicators** — (a) data used to measure economic performance: real GDP, real GDP per capita, CPI/RPI, measures of unemployment, productivity, the balance of payments on current account.
- **4.2.1.3 Uses of index numbers** — (a) how index numbers are calculated and interpreted, including the base year and the use of weights. (b) how index numbers are used to measure changes in the price level and other economic variables; awareness of CPI/RPI features such as the "average family" and a "basket of goods and services".
- **4.2.1.4 Uses of national income data** — (a) the use and limitations of national income data to assess changes in living standards over time. (b) the use and limitations of national income data to compare differences in living standards between countries. (c) the importance of using purchasing-power-parity (PPP) exchange rates when making international comparisons of living standards.

#### 4.2.2 How the macroeconomy works: the circular flow of income, AD/AS analysis and related concepts

- **4.2.2.1 The circular flow of income** — (a) what national income measures. (b) the difference between nominal and real income. (c) real national income as an indicator of economic performance. (d) the circular-flow concept; the identity income = output = expenditure; equilibrium and full-employment income. (e) the difference between injections and withdrawals. (f) the effect of changes in injections and withdrawals on national income.
- **4.2.2.2 Aggregate demand and aggregate supply analysis** — (a) changes in the price level are represented by movements along the AD and AS curves. (b) the factors that shift the AD curve and the short-run AS curve. (c) the factors which affect long-run AS, distinguishing them from those that affect short-run AS. (d) underlying economic growth is represented by a rightward shift in the long-run AS curve. (e) using AD/AS diagrams to illustrate macroeconomic equilibrium. (f) how both demand-side and supply-side shocks affect the macroeconomy; how global economic events can affect the domestic economy.
- **4.2.2.3 The determinants of aggregate demand** — (a) what is meant by AD. (b) the determinants of consumption, investment, government spending, exports and imports. (c) the basic accelerator process (no calculations required). (d) the determinants of savings. (e) the difference between saving and investment; how changes in net exports affect AD and economic performance.
- **4.2.2.4 Aggregate demand and the level of economic activity** — (a) the role of AD in influencing the level of economic activity. (b) the multiplier process and why an initial change in expenditure may lead to a larger impact on local or national income. (c) the concept of the marginal propensity to consume and use of MPC to calculate the size of the multiplier. (d) why the size of MPC determines the magnitude of the multiplier effect (calculations from marginal propensities to withdraw not required).
- **4.2.2.5 Determinants of short-run aggregate supply** — (a) the price level and production costs are the main determinants of short-run AS. (b) changes in money wage rates, raw-material prices, business taxation and productivity will shift the short-run AS curve.
- **4.2.2.6 Determinants of long-run aggregate supply** — (a) fundamental determinants: technology, productivity, attitudes, enterprise, factor mobility, economic incentives. (b) the position of the vertical long-run AS curve represents the normal-capacity level of output. (c) the importance of the institutional structure of the economy, including the role of the banking system in providing business investment funds. (d) the Keynesian AS curve.

#### 4.2.3 Economic performance

- **4.2.3.1 Economic growth and the economic cycle** — (a) the difference between short-run and long-run growth, illustrated via PPC and AD/AS diagrams. (b) demand-side and supply-side determinants of short-run growth and the long-run trend rate of growth. (c) the costs and benefits of economic growth. (d) the impact of growth on individuals, the economy and the environment; sustainability of growth. (e) the economic cycle and the use of a range of indicators (real GDP, inflation, unemployment, investment) to identify its phases. (f) the difference between positive and negative output gaps. (g) the causes of changes in the various phases of the economic cycle, including global and domestic demand-side and supply-side shocks; causes of cyclical instability such as excessive credit and debt growth, asset-price bubbles, destabilising speculation, animal spirits and herding.
- **4.2.3.2 Employment and unemployment** — (a) the main UK measures of unemployment: claimant count and Labour Force Survey. (b) the concepts of voluntary and involuntary unemployment. (c) the terms seasonal, frictional, structural and cyclical unemployment. (d) how employment and unemployment may be determined by both demand-side and supply-side factors; cyclical unemployment links to negative output gap; supply-side causes shift the long-run AS curve. (e) the concept of, and factors determining, real-wage unemployment. (f) the concept of, and factors determining, the natural rate of unemployment. (g) the consequences of unemployment for individuals and for the performance of the economy.
- **4.2.3.3 Inflation and deflation** — (a) the concepts of inflation, deflation and disinflation (deflation is a falling price level; disinflation is a falling rate of inflation). (b) demand-pull and cost-push influences on the price level. (c) Fisher's equation of exchange MV = PQ and the Quantity Theory of Money in relation to the monetarist model (T may be used instead of Q; PQ as nominal national income avoids difficulties with intermediate transactions). (d) the effects of expectations on changes in the price level. (e) the consequences of inflation for individuals and the performance of the economy. (f) the consequences of deflation for individuals and the performance of the economy; deflationary policies reduce AD and do not necessarily result in deflation. (g) how changes in world commodity prices affect domestic inflation. (h) how changes in other economies can affect UK inflation.
- **4.2.3.4 Possible conflicts between macroeconomic policy objectives** — (a) how negative and positive output gaps relate to unemployment and inflationary pressures. (b) both the short-run Phillips curve and the long-run, L-shaped (vertical long-run) Phillips curve. (c) the implications of the short-run and long-run Phillips curves for economic policy. (d) how economic policies may be used to reconcile possible policy conflicts in the short run and the long run, including the monetarist/supply-side view that major macroeconomic objectives are compatible in the long run.

#### 4.2.4 Financial markets and monetary policy

- **4.2.4.1 The structure of financial markets and financial assets** — (a) the characteristics and functions of money. (b) definitions of the money supply and the distinction between narrow and broad money. (c) the difference between the money market, the capital market and the foreign-exchange market. (d) the role of financial markets in the wider economy, including how firms raise finance (issuing shares, issuing corporate bonds, borrowing from a bank). (e) the difference between debt and equity. (f) why there is an inverse relationship between market interest rates and bond prices; the terms coupon and maturity in relation to government bonds and how to calculate the yield on a government bond.
- **4.2.4.2 Commercial banks and investment banks** — (a) the difference between a commercial bank and an investment bank; awareness that many banks engage in both activities and that this may increase systemic risk. (b) the main functions of a commercial bank. (c) the structure of a commercial bank's balance sheet. (d) the objectives of a commercial bank — liquidity, profitability and security. (e) potential conflicts between these objectives. (f) how banks create credit (credit-multiplier calculation not required).
- **4.2.4.3 Central banks and monetary policy** — (a) the main functions of a central bank. (b) monetary policy involves the central bank taking action to influence interest rates, the supply of money and credit and the exchange rate. (c) the current objectives of monetary policy set by the government. (d) the role of the MPC and how it uses changes in bank rate to achieve the objectives of monetary policy, including the government's target rate of inflation. (e) the factors considered by the MPC when setting bank rate. (f) how changes in the exchange rate affect AD and the various macroeconomic policy objectives. (g) the monetary-policy transmission mechanism, including the relationship between changes in interest rates and the exchange rate. (h) how the Bank of England can influence the growth of the money supply; current and recent instruments of monetary policy such as quantitative easing, Funding for Lending and forward guidance.
- **4.2.4.4 The regulation of the financial system** — (a) regulation of the financial system in the UK, including the role of the Bank of England, the Prudential Regulation Authority (PRA), the Financial Policy Committee (FPC) and the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) — appreciating their role in maintaining financial stability. (b) why a bank might fail, including the risks of lending long term and borrowing short term. (c) liquidity ratios and capital ratios and how they affect the stability of a financial institution (calculation not required). (d) moral hazard. (e) systemic risk and the impact of problems in financial markets upon the real economy.

#### 4.2.5 Fiscal policy and supply-side policies

- **4.2.5.1 Fiscal policy** — (a) fiscal policy involves the manipulation of government spending, taxation and the budget balance. (b) fiscal policy can have both macroeconomic and microeconomic functions. (c) how fiscal policy can be used to influence AD. (d) how fiscal policy can be used to influence AS. (e) how government spending and taxation can affect the pattern of economic activity. (f) the types of and reasons for public expenditure. (g) why governments levy taxes. (h) the difference between direct and indirect taxes. (i) the difference between progressive, proportional and regressive taxes. (j) the principles of taxation, such as equity. (k) the role and relative merits of different UK taxes. (l) the relationship between the budget balance and the national debt. (m) cyclical and structural budget deficits and surpluses. (n) the consequences of budget deficits and surpluses for macroeconomic performance; assessing measures used to rebalance the budget. (o) the significance of the size of the national debt. (p) the role of the Office for Budget Responsibility.
- **4.2.5.2 Supply-side policies** — (a) the difference between supply-side policies and supply-side improvements in the economy; recognition that improvements often originate in the private sector through productivity, innovation and investment. (b) how supply-side policies can help achieve supply-side improvements. (c) how supply-side policies, such as tax changes designed to change personal incentives, may increase potential output and improve the underlying trend rate of growth. (d) how supply-side policies can affect unemployment, the rate of change of prices and UK external performance (current account). (e) the role of supply-side policies in reducing the natural rate of unemployment. (f) free-market supply-side policies: tax cuts, privatisation, deregulation and some labour-market reforms. (g) interventionist supply-side policies: government spending on education and training, industrial policy, subsidising R&D — including intervention to deal with market failures such as short-termism. (h) supply-side policies can have microeconomic as well as macroeconomic effects.

#### 4.2.6 The international economy

- **4.2.6.1 Globalisation** — (a) the causes of globalisation. (b) the main characteristics of globalisation. (c) the consequences of globalisation for less-developed and more-developed countries. (d) the role of multinational corporations in globalisation.
- **4.2.6.2 Trade** — (a) the model of comparative advantage, with simple numerical illustration. (b) the distinction between comparative and absolute advantage. (c) the model shows that specialisation and trade can increase total output. (d) other economic benefits of trade, such as exploiting economies of scale and increased competition. (e) the costs of international trade. (f) the reasons for changes in the pattern of trade between the UK and the rest of the world. (g) the nature of protectionist policies, such as tariffs, quotas and export subsidies, illustrated with a diagram showing the effect of imposing a tariff on imports. (h) the causes and consequences of countries adopting protectionist policies. (i) the main features of a customs union. (j) the main characteristics of the Single European Market (SEM). (k) the role of the World Trade Organisation (WTO).
- **4.2.6.3 The balance of payments** — (a) the difference between the current, capital and financial accounts on the balance of payments — detailed knowledge of the current account is expected; general appreciation of the other sections. (b) the current account comprises trade in goods, trade in services, primary income and secondary income. (c) the meaning of a deficit and a surplus on the current account. (d) the factors that influence a country's current-account balance such as productivity, inflation and the exchange rate. (e) the consequences of investment flows between countries — distinguishing foreign direct investment (FDI) and portfolio investment. (f) the policies that might be used to correct a balance-of-payments deficit or surplus. (g) expenditure-switching and expenditure-reducing policies. (h) the effect such policies may have upon other macroeconomic policy objectives. (i) the significance of deficits and surpluses for an individual economy. (j) the implications for the global economy of a major economy or economies with imbalances deciding to take corrective action.
- **4.2.6.4 Exchange rate systems** — (a) how exchange rates are determined in freely floating exchange-rate systems. (b) how governments can intervene to influence the exchange rate. (c) the advantages and disadvantages of fixed and floating exchange-rate systems. (d) advantages and disadvantages for a country of joining a currency union, e.g. the eurozone.
- **4.2.6.5 Economic growth and development** — (a) the difference between growth and development. (b) the main characteristics of less-developed economies. (c) the main indicators of development, including the Human Development Index (HDI). (d) factors that affect growth and development, such as investment, education and training. (e) barriers to growth and development, such as corruption, institutional factors, poor infrastructure, inadequate human capital, lack of property rights. (f) policies that might be adopted to promote economic growth and development — comparing market-based and interventionist strategies. (g) the role of aid and trade in promoting growth and development; links to globalisation, trade, the determinants of economic growth and inequality.
