## Qualification at a glance

Four themes, three externally examined papers.

- **Theme 1 — Introduction to markets and market failure** (microeconomics)
- **Theme 2 — The UK economy: performance and policies** (macroeconomics)
- **Theme 3 — Business behaviour and the labour market** (microeconomics)
- **Theme 4 — A global perspective** (macroeconomics)

| Paper | Coverage | Format | Weight |
|---|---|---|---|
| Paper 1 — Markets and business behaviour (9EC0/01) | Themes 1 & 3 | 2h · 100 marks · MCQ/short + data response + extended essay | 35% |
| Paper 2 — The national and global economy (9EC0/02) | Themes 2 & 4 | 2h · 100 marks · MCQ/short + data response + extended essay | 35% |
| Paper 3 — Microeconomics and macroeconomics (9EC0/03) | All four themes (synoptic) | 2h · 100 marks · two data-response sections with extended essay choices | 30% |

## Theme 1 — Introduction to markets and market failure

### 1.1 Nature of economics

- **1.1.1 Economics as a social science** — (a) thinking like an economist; the process of building models, including the need to make assumptions. (b) ceteris paribus. (c) the inability to run scientific experiments in economics.
- **1.1.2 Positive and normative economic statements** — (a) distinction between positive and normative statements. (b) the role of value judgements in decision making and policy.
- **1.1.3 The economic problem** — (a) scarcity: unlimited wants vs finite resources. (b) renewable vs non-renewable resources. (c) opportunity cost for consumers, producers and government.
- **1.1.4 Production possibility frontiers** — (a) use of PPFs to depict max productive potential, opportunity cost (marginal analysis), economic growth/decline, efficiency, possible/unobtainable production. (b) movements along vs shifts of PPCs. (c) capital vs consumer goods.
- **1.1.5 Specialisation and the division of labour** — (a) Adam Smith on specialisation and division of labour. (b) advantages/disadvantages. (c) advantages/disadvantages of specialising to trade. (d) functions of money (medium of exchange, measure of value, store of value, deferred payment).
- **1.1.6 Free market, mixed and command economies** — (a) distinction with reference to Adam Smith, Friedrich Hayek and Karl Marx. (b) advantages/disadvantages of free-market and command economies. (c) the role of the state in a mixed economy.

### 1.2 How markets work

- **1.2.1 Rational decision making** — (a) underlying assumptions: consumers maximise utility, firms maximise profit.
- **1.2.2 Demand** — (a) movements along vs shifts. (b) conditions of demand. (c) diminishing marginal utility and the demand-curve shape.
- **1.2.3 Price, income and cross elasticities of demand** — (a) understanding. (b) formula calculations. (c) interpreting numerical values (PED unitary/elastic/inelastic; YED inferior/normal/luxury; XED substitutes/complements/unrelated). (d) factors influencing elasticities. (e) significance to firms and government (indirect taxes & subsidies, changes in real income, changes in substitute/complement prices). (f) PED and total revenue (with calculation).
- **1.2.4 Supply** — (a) movements along vs shifts. (b) conditions of supply.
- **1.2.5 Elasticity of supply** — (a) understanding PES. (b) formula. (c) interpreting numerical values. (d) factors influencing PES. (e) short-run vs long-run distinction and significance for PES.
- **1.2.6 Price determination** — (a) equilibrium price and quantity. (b) supply-demand diagrams showing excess supply/demand. (c) market forces eliminating excess. (d) diagrammatic real-world shifts.
- **1.2.7 Price mechanism** — (a) functions: rationing, incentive, signalling. (b) price mechanism in local, national and global markets.
- **1.2.8 Consumer and producer surplus** — (a) distinction. (b) diagrammatic illustration. (c) how shifts affect surplus.
- **1.2.9 Indirect taxes and subsidies** — (a) supply-demand analysis with elasticities: impact and incidence on consumers/producers/government; area of producer/consumer subsidy.
- **1.2.10 Alternative views of consumer behaviour** — (a) reasons consumers may behave irrationally: influence of others, habit, weakness at computation.

### 1.3 Market failure

- **1.3.1 Types of market failure** — (a) understanding. (b) types: externalities, under-provision of public goods, information gaps.
- **1.3.2 Externalities** — (a) private/external/social costs. (b) private/external/social benefits. (c) diagram of external costs of production (marginal analysis, market equilibrium vs social optimum, welfare loss area). (d) diagram of external benefits of consumption (welfare gain area). (e) impact on economic agents and government intervention.
- **1.3.3 Public goods** — (a) public vs private goods via non-rivalry and non-excludability. (b) why public goods may not be provided by the private sector: the free-rider problem.
- **1.3.4 Information gaps** — (a) symmetric vs asymmetric information. (b) imperfect information and misallocation.

### 1.4 Government intervention

- **1.4.1 Government intervention in markets** — (a) purpose with diagrams: indirect taxation (ad valorem & specific), subsidies, maximum and minimum prices. (b) other methods: tradable pollution permits, state provision of public goods, provision of information, regulation.
- **1.4.2 Government failure** — (a) intervention resulting in net welfare loss. (b) causes: distortion of price signals, unintended consequences, excessive administrative costs, information gaps. (c) government failure across markets.

## Theme 2 — The UK economy: performance and policies

### 2.1 Measures of economic performance

- **2.1.1 Economic growth** — (a) rates of change of real GDP as a measure. (b) real vs nominal, total vs per capita, value vs volume. (c) GNI. (d) comparison between countries and over time. (e) PPPs and PPP-adjusted international comparisons. (f) limitations of GDP for living standards. (g) national happiness — UK national wellbeing; relationship between real incomes and subjective happiness.
- **2.1.2 Inflation** — (a) inflation, deflation, disinflation. (b) calculating the UK rate via CPI. (c) limitations of CPI. (d) RPI as alternative. (e) causes: demand-pull, cost-push, growth of the money supply. (f) effects on consumers, firms, government, workers.
- **2.1.3 Employment and unemployment** — (a) measures: claimant count, ILO/UK Labour Force Survey. (b) unemployment vs under-employment. (c) significance of changes in employment, unemployment, inactivity rates. (d) causes: structural, frictional, seasonal, demand-deficient/cyclical, real-wage inflexibility. (e) significance of migration and skills. (f) effects on consumers, firms, workers, government, society.
- **2.1.4 Balance of payments** — (a) components, current account, trade in goods and services. (b) current-account deficits and surpluses. (c) relationship with other macro objectives. (d) interconnectedness of economies through trade.

### 2.2 Aggregate demand (AD)

- **2.2.1 Characteristics of AD** — (a) components C + I + G + (X − M). (b) relative importance. (c) the AD curve. (d) movement along vs shift.
- **2.2.2 Consumption (C)** — (a) disposable income's influence. (b) savings–consumption relationship. (c) other influences: interest rates, consumer confidence, wealth effects.
- **2.2.3 Investment (I)** — (a) gross vs net investment. (b) influences: economic growth, business expectations & confidence, Keynes's "animal spirits", demand for exports, interest rates, access to credit, government & regulation.
- **2.2.4 Government expenditure (G)** — (a) main influences: trade cycle, fiscal policy.
- **2.2.5 Net trade (X − M)** — (a) main influences: real income, exchange rates, world economy, protectionism, non-price factors.

### 2.3 Aggregate supply (AS)

- **2.3.1 Characteristics of AS** — (a) the AS curve. (b) movement along vs shift. (c) short-run vs long-run AS.
- **2.3.2 Short-run AS** — (a) factors: raw material/energy costs, exchange rates, tax rates.
- **2.3.3 Long-run AS** — (a) shapes: Keynesian, classical. (b) factors: technology, relative productivity, education & skills, government regulation, demographics & migration, competition policy.

### 2.4 National income

- **2.4.1 National income** — (a) the circular flow of income. (b) income vs wealth.
- **2.4.2 Injections and withdrawals** — (a) impact of injections into, and withdrawals from, the circular flow.
- **2.4.3 Equilibrium levels of real national output** — (a) the concept of equilibrium real national output. (b) AD/AS diagrams showing how shifts in AD or AS change the equilibrium price level and real national output.
- **2.4.4 The multiplier** — (a) multiplier ratio. (b) multiplier process. (c) effects on the economy. (d) marginal propensities and their effects (MPC, MPS, MPT, MPM). (e) calculations using 1/(1−MPC) and 1/MPW where MPW = MPS + MPT + MPM. (f) significance for shifts in AD.

### 2.5 Economic growth

- **2.5.1 Causes of growth** — (a) factors that could cause growth. (b) actual vs potential growth. (c) importance of trade for export-led growth.
- **2.5.2 Output gaps** — (a) actual growth rates vs long-term trend. (b) positive and negative output gaps and measurement difficulty. (c) AD/AS diagram of output gap / spare capacity.
- **2.5.3 Trade (business) cycle** — (a) understanding. (b) boom characteristics. (c) recession characteristics.
- **2.5.4 The impact of economic growth** — (a) benefits/costs and impact on consumers, firms, government, current and future living standards.

### 2.6 Macroeconomic objectives and policies

- **2.6.1 Possible macroeconomic objectives** — economic growth; low unemployment; low and stable inflation; balance-of-payments equilibrium on current account; balanced government budget; protection of the environment; greater income equality.
- **2.6.2 Demand-side policies** — (a) monetary vs fiscal. (b) monetary instruments: interest rates, QE. (c) fiscal instruments: government spending and taxation. (d) budget deficit vs surplus. (e) direct vs indirect taxation. (f) AD/AS diagrams. (g) Bank of England & MPC. (h) demand-side policies in the Great Depression and 2008 GFC — different interpretations, US/UK responses. (i) strengths and weaknesses.
- **2.6.3 Supply-side policies** — (a) market-based vs interventionist. (b) policies to: increase incentives, promote competition, reform the labour market, improve skills/quality of labour, improve infrastructure. (c) AD/AS diagrams. (d) strengths and weaknesses.
- **2.6.4 Conflicts and trade-offs** — (a) potential conflicts/trade-offs between objectives. (b) short-run Phillips curve. (c) policy conflicts/trade-offs.

## Theme 3 — Business behaviour and the labour market

### 3.1 Business growth

- **3.1.1 Sizes and types of firms** — (a) why some firms remain small and others grow. (b) divorce of ownership from control: principal-agent problem. (c) public vs private sector organisations. (d) profit vs not-for-profit.
- **3.1.2 Business growth** — (a) organic growth; forward/backward vertical integration; horizontal integration; conglomerate integration. (b) advantages/disadvantages of each. (c) constraints: market size, access to finance, owner objectives, regulation.
- **3.1.3 Demergers** — (a) reasons. (b) impact on businesses, workers, consumers.

### 3.2 Business objectives

- **3.2.1 Business objectives** — (a) profit maximisation, revenue maximisation, sales maximisation, satisficing. (b) diagrams and formulae for profit/revenue/sales maximisation.

### 3.3 Revenues, costs and profits

- **3.3.1 Revenue** — (a) total, average, marginal revenue formulae & relationships. (b) PED's relationship to revenue (calculation required).
- **3.3.2 Costs** — (a) total cost, total fixed cost, total variable cost, average (total) cost, average fixed cost, average variable cost, marginal cost. (b) derivation of short-run cost curves from diminishing marginal productivity. (c) relationship between short-run and long-run average cost curves.
- **3.3.3 Economies and diseconomies of scale** — (a) types. (b) minimum efficient scale. (c) internal vs external economies.
- **3.3.4 Normal profits, supernormal profits and losses** — (a) profit-maximisation condition. (b) normal, supernormal profit, losses. (c) short-run and long-run shut-down points (diagrammatic).

### 3.4 Market structures

- **3.4.1 Efficiency** — (a) allocative. (b) productive. (c) dynamic. (d) X-inefficiency. (e) efficiency/inefficiency across structures.
- **3.4.2 Perfect competition** — (a) characteristics. (b) profit-max equilibrium SR and LR. (c) diagrammatic analysis.
- **3.4.3 Monopolistic competition** — (a) characteristics. (b) profit-max equilibrium SR and LR. (c) diagrammatic analysis.
- **3.4.4 Oligopoly** — (a) characteristics: high barriers, high concentration, interdependence, product differentiation. (b) calculating n-firm concentration ratios. (c) reasons for collusive/non-collusive behaviour. (d) overt vs tacit collusion; cartels; price leadership. (e) simple game theory — prisoner's dilemma in 2×2. (f) price competition: price wars, predatory pricing, limit pricing. (g) non-price competition.
- **3.4.5 Monopoly** — (a) characteristics. (b) profit-max equilibrium. (c) diagrammatic analysis. (d) third-degree price discrimination: conditions, diagram, costs/benefits. (e) costs/benefits of monopoly. (f) natural monopoly.
- **3.4.6 Monopsony** — (a) characteristics and conditions. (b) costs/benefits to firms, consumers, employees, suppliers.
- **3.4.7 Contestability** — (a) characteristics. (b) implications for firm behaviour. (c) barriers to entry/exit. (d) sunk costs and degree of contestability.

### 3.5 Labour market

- **3.5.1 Demand for labour** — (a) factors influencing demand. (b) labour demand as a derived demand.
- **3.5.2 Supply of labour** — (a) factors influencing supply to an occupation. (b) labour-market failure: geographical and occupational mobility/immobility.
- **3.5.3 Wage determination in competitive and non-competitive markets** — (a) diagrammatic analysis. (b) current labour-market issues. (c) government intervention: max/min wages, public-sector wage setting, policies for labour-market immobility. (d) significance of elasticities of labour demand and supply.

### 3.6 Government intervention

- **3.6.1 Government intervention** — (a) control of mergers. (b) control of monopolies: price regulation, profit regulation, quality standards, performance targets. (c) promoting competition/contestability: small-business promotion, deregulation, competitive tendering, privatisation. (d) protecting suppliers/employees: restrictions on monopsony power, nationalisation.
- **3.6.2 The impact of government intervention** — (a) impact on prices, profit, efficiency, quality, choice. (b) limits: regulatory capture, asymmetric information.

## Theme 4 — A global perspective

### 4.1 International economics

- **4.1.1 Globalisation** — (a) characteristics. (b) factors contributing to globalisation in the last 50 years. (c) impacts on countries, governments, producers, consumers, workers, environment.
- **4.1.2 Specialisation and trade** — (a) absolute and comparative advantage (numerical and diagrammatic) — assumptions and limitations. (b) advantages/disadvantages of specialisation and trade internationally.
- **4.1.3 Pattern of trade** — (a) factors influencing patterns and changes: comparative advantage, emerging economies, trading blocs and bilateral agreements, exchange-rate changes.
- **4.1.4 Terms of trade** — (a) calculation. (b) factors influencing. (c) impact of changes.
- **4.1.5 Trading blocs and the WTO** — (a) types of trading bloc: free trade areas, customs unions, common markets, monetary unions (Eurozone conditions). (b) costs/benefits of regional trade agreements. (c) WTO's role in trade liberalisation. (d) conflicts between RTAs and WTO.
- **4.1.6 Restrictions on free trade** — (a) reasons. (b) types: tariffs, quotas, subsidies to domestic producers, non-tariff barriers. (c) impact of protectionism on consumers, producers, governments, living standards, equality.
- **4.1.7 Balance of payments** — (a) components: current account; capital and financial accounts. (b) causes of current-account deficits/surpluses. (c) measures to reduce imbalance. (d) significance of global trade imbalances.
- **4.1.8 Exchange rates** — (a) systems: floating, fixed, managed. (b) revaluation vs appreciation. (c) devaluation vs depreciation. (d) factors influencing floating rates. (e) government intervention via foreign-currency transactions and interest rates. (f) competitive devaluation/depreciation and consequences. (g) impacts: current account (Marshall-Lerner condition, J-curve), growth/employment, inflation, FDI flows.
- **4.1.9 International competitiveness** — (a) measures: relative unit labour costs, relative export prices. (b) factors influencing. (c) significance — benefits of being competitive; problems of being uncompetitive.

### 4.2 Poverty and inequality

- **4.2.1 Absolute and relative poverty** — (a) distinction. (b) measures. (c) causes of changes.
- **4.2.2 Inequality** — (a) wealth vs income inequality. (b) measurements: Lorenz curve, Gini coefficient. (c) causes within and between countries. (d) impact of economic change and development. (e) significance of capitalism for inequality.

### 4.3 Emerging and developing economies

- **4.3.1 Measures of development** — (a) HDI: education, health, living standards — measurement and combination. (b) advantages/limitations of HDI. (c) other indicators.
- **4.3.2 Factors influencing growth and development** — (a) economic factors: primary product dependency, commodity-price volatility, savings gap (Harrod-Domar), foreign-currency gap, capital flight, demographic factors, debt, access to credit/banking, infrastructure, education/skills, absence of property rights. (b) non-economic factors.
- **4.3.3 Strategies influencing growth and development** — (a) market-orientated: trade liberalisation, FDI promotion, removal of subsidies, floating exchange rates, microfinance, privatisation. (b) interventionist: human-capital development, protectionism, managed exchange rates, infrastructure, joint ventures with global companies, buffer-stock schemes. (c) other: industrialisation (Lewis model), tourism, primary industries, Fairtrade, aid, debt relief. (d) role of World Bank, IMF, NGOs.

### 4.4 The financial sector

- **4.4.1 Role of financial markets** — (a) facilitate saving. (b) lend to businesses and individuals. (c) facilitate exchange of goods and services. (d) provide forward markets in currencies and commodities. (e) provide a market for equities.
- **4.4.2 Market failure in the financial sector** — asymmetric information; externalities; moral hazard; speculation and market bubbles; market rigging.
- **4.4.3 Role of central banks** — implementation of monetary policy; banker to the government; lender of last resort; banking regulation.

### 4.5 Role of the state in the macroeconomy

- **4.5.1 Public expenditure** — (a) capital vs current expenditure vs transfer payments. (b) reasons for changing size and composition globally. (c) significance of public-expenditure share of GDP for productivity & growth, living standards, crowding out, taxation level, equality.
- **4.5.2 Taxation** — (a) progressive vs proportional vs regressive. (b) economic effects of direct/indirect tax changes on incentives to work, tax revenues (Laffer curve), income distribution, real output and employment, the price level, the trade balance, FDI flows.
- **4.5.3 Public sector finances** — (a) automatic stabilisers vs discretionary fiscal policy. (b) fiscal deficit vs national debt. (c) structural vs cyclical deficits. (d) factors influencing deficit size. (e) factors influencing national debt size. (f) significance of size of deficits and national debts.
- **4.5.4 Macroeconomic policies in a global context** — (a) use of fiscal, monetary, exchange rate, supply-side policies and direct controls to reduce deficits/debt, reduce poverty/inequality, change interest rates and money supply, increase international competitiveness. (b) macro responses to external shocks. (c) controlling transnational companies — transfer-pricing regulation and limits to government control. (d) policymaker problems: inaccurate information, risks and uncertainties, inability to control external shocks.
