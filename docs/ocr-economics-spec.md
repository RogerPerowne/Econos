## Qualification at a glance

Three mandatory components, all externally examined. No coursework.

- **Component 1 — Microeconomics** (introduction to micro, role of markets, business objectives, market structures, the labour market)
- **Component 2 — Macroeconomics** (aggregate demand and supply, economic policy objectives, implementing policy, the global context, the financial sector)
- **Component 3 — Themes in economics** (synoptic; draws on the whole micro + macro content in relation to an unseen theme)

| Paper | Coverage | Format | Weight |
|---|---|---|---|
| Component 01 — Microeconomics (H460/01) | Micro content | 2h · 80 marks · data response + extended response | 33.33% |
| Component 02 — Macroeconomics (H460/02) | Macro content | 2h · 80 marks · data response + extended response | 33.33% |
| Component 03 — Themes in economics (H460/03) | Synoptic across micro + macro | 2h · 80 marks · 30 MCQs + unseen-theme short answers + extended responses | 33.33% |

### Assessment Objectives

| AO | Demand | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| AO1 | Demonstrate knowledge of terms / concepts / theories / models showing understanding of economic agents' behaviour | 25% |
| AO2 | Apply knowledge and understanding to various economic contexts | 25% |
| AO3 | Analyse issues within economics, showing understanding of their impact on economic agents | 25% |
| AO4 | Evaluate economic arguments using qualitative and quantitative evidence to support informed judgements | 25% |

A minimum of 20% of the overall A level marks target quantitative skills (Level 2 mathematical skills minimum), assessed across the three components.

## Component 1 — Microeconomics (H460/01)

### 1. Introduction to Microeconomics

- **1.1 The economic problem** — (a) economic goods and free goods. (b) the economic problem: scarcity, choice, needs, and wants. (c) normative and positive statements. (d) the role of economic agents: government, firms, and households. (e) the factors of production: land, labour, capital, and enterprise. (f) the rewards of the factors of production: rent, wages, interest and profit. (g) evaluate the problem of scarcity and the requirement to make choices. (h) evaluate rationality as a way of understanding the behaviour of economic agents. (i) evaluate the different objectives of the economic agents in an economy.
- **1.2 The allocation of resources** — (a) incentives. (b) market, planned and mixed economic systems. (c) economic efficiency: productive and allocative efficiency. (d) evaluate the effectiveness of incentives on the behaviour of economic agents and resource allocation. (e) evaluate the allocation of resources in the different economic systems.
- **1.3 Opportunity cost** — (a) opportunity cost and trade-off. (b) explain with the aid of a diagram movements along a production possibility curve (PPC). (c) shifts of a production possibility curve (PPC). (d) the usefulness of the concept of opportunity cost.

### 2. The role of markets

- **2.1 Specialisation and trade** — (a) specialisation and the division of labour. (b) barter systems. (c) money as a medium of exchange. (d) evaluate the role of specialisation and the division of labour in addressing the problem of scarcity.
- **2.2 Demand** — (a) the relationship between price and quantity demanded. (b) individual and market demand. (c) joint, competitive and composite demand. (d) movements along the demand curve (extension/contraction). (e) shifts of the demand curve (increase/decrease).
- **2.3 Supply** — (a) the relationship between price and quantity supplied. (b) individual and market supply. (c) joint and competitive supply. (d) movements along the supply curve (extension/contraction). (e) shifts of the supply curve (increase/decrease).
- **2.4 Consumer and producer surplus** — (a) consumer surplus and producer surplus. (b) the effects of changes in price on consumer surplus. (c) the effects of changes in price on producer surplus. (d) evaluate the impact of changes in price on consumer and producer surplus.
- **2.5 The interaction of markets** — (a) ceteris paribus. (b) the interaction of demand and supply. (c) market equilibrium and disequilibrium. (d) evaluate the impact of changes in demand and/or supply in one market on a related market(s).
- **2.6 Elasticity** — (a) elasticity. (b) explain and calculate price elasticity of demand (PED). (c) income elasticity of demand (YED). (d) cross elasticity of demand (XED). (e) price elasticity of supply (PES). (f) different values of PED, YED, XED and PES with diagrams. (g) the relationship between PED and a firm's total revenue. (h) evaluate factors which determine the value of PED, YED, XED and PES. (i) evaluate the usefulness and significance of PED, YED, XED and PES.
- **2.7 The concept of the margin** — (a) margin. (b) total and marginal utility, diminishing marginal utility and the demand curve. (c) explain and calculate marginal values.
- **2.8 Market failure and externalities** — (a) market failure. (b) marginal social cost, marginal external cost, marginal private cost, marginal social benefit, marginal external benefit and marginal private benefit. (c) explain with the aid of a diagram positive and negative externalities (external benefits and external costs); consumption and production.
- **2.9 Information failure** — (a) information failure. (b) asymmetric information and moral hazard. (c) merit and demerit goods. (d) market failure caused by information failure (diagram). (e) evaluate consumption and production of merit goods. (f) evaluate consumption and production of demerit goods.
- **2.10 Public goods** — (a) public goods, private goods and quasi-public goods. (b) characteristics of a public good: non-excludability, non-diminishability/non-rivalry, non-rejectability and zero marginal cost. (c) the free rider problem. (d) evaluate the provision of public goods.
- **2.11 Government intervention** — (a) government intervention in markets: taxation, subsidies, government expenditure, price controls, buffer stock systems, public/private partnerships, legislation, regulation, tradable pollution permits, information provision, competition policy. (b) government failure. (c) evaluate the effectiveness of government intervention. (d) evaluate causes and consequences of government failure.

### 3. Business Objectives

- **3.1 Business objectives** — (a) maximisation objectives: profit, sales revenue, sales volume, growth and utility. (b) non-maximising objectives: profit satisficing, social welfare, corporate social responsibility (CSR). (c) the principal-agent problem. (d) evaluate maximisation and non-maximisation objectives. (e) evaluate factors which influence the choice of objectives.
- **3.2 Costs and economies of scale** — (a) explain and calculate fixed, variable, total, average and marginal costs. (b) short run and long run in terms of fixed and variable factors. (c) the law of diminishing returns (diagram). (d) internal and external economies of scale. (e) diseconomies of scale. (f) minimum efficient scale. (g) evaluate causes of economies and diseconomies of scale. (h) evaluate the significance of economies and diseconomies of scale.
- **3.3 Revenue and profit** — (a) explain and calculate total, average and marginal revenue. (b) explain and calculate profit/loss. (c) accounting, normal and supernormal profit.

### 4. Market structures

- **4.1 Perfect competition** — (a) the characteristics of perfect competition. (b) short run perfect competition; supernormal profit/loss (diagram). (c) long run perfect competition: normal profits (diagram). (d) individual firm in perfect competition as a price taker. (e) equilibrium price and output for a firm in perfect competition. (f) allocative efficiency in short run and long run perfect competition. (g) productive efficiency in long run perfect competition.
- **4.2 Monopoly** — (a) the characteristics of monopoly. (b) dynamic efficiency. (c) X-inefficiency. (d) monopoly; supernormal profit in both short and long run (diagram). (e) a monopolist as a price maker. (f) equilibrium price and output for a profit maximising monopolist. (g) productive and allocative efficiency with a profit maximising monopolist. (h) price discrimination by a firm with monopoly power. (i) natural monopoly. (j) evaluate advantages and disadvantages of a monopoly. (k) evaluate advantages and disadvantages of a natural monopoly.
- **4.3 Monopolistic competition** — (a) the characteristics of monopolistic competition. (b) short run monopolistic competition; supernormal profit/loss (diagram). (c) long run monopolistic competition; normal profits (diagram). (d) equilibrium price and output for a firm in monopolistic competition. (e) evaluate advantages and disadvantages of monopolistic competition.
- **4.4 Oligopoly** — (a) the characteristics of oligopoly. (b) non-price competition. (c) interdependence: kinked demand curve. (d) types of collusion. (e) product differentiation. (f) evaluate and calculate concentration ratios. (g) evaluate advantages and disadvantages of oligopoly markets.
- **4.5 Contestable markets** — (a) the characteristics of a contestable market. (b) productive and allocative efficiency in a contestable market. (c) evaluate advantages and disadvantages of a contestable market.

### 5. The labour market

- **5.1 Demand for labour** — (a) derived demand for labour. (b) factors affecting the demand for labour in an industry. (c) factors affecting wage elasticity of demand for labour. (d) productivity and unit labour costs. (e) marginal revenue product theory in relation to employment and wage determination (diagram).
- **5.2 Supply of labour** — (a) factors affecting the supply of labour to an industry. (b) factors affecting the wage elasticity of the supply of labour. (c) short run and long run supply of labour. (d) economic rent and transfer earnings (diagram).
- **5.3 The interaction of labour markets** — (a) wage differentials. (b) monopsony. (c) trade union. (d) bilateral monopoly. (e) the determination of wages in a highly competitive labour market (diagram). (f) changes in demand for, and supply of, labour (diagram). (g) evaluate the impact of changes in labour market flexibility and mobility of labour. (h) evaluate the impact of trade union activity on labour markets. (i) evaluate the impact of a monopsonist employer on a labour market. (j) evaluate the impact of a bilateral monopoly on a labour market.

## Component 2 — Macroeconomics (H460/02)

### 1. Aggregate demand and aggregate supply

- **1.1 Circular flow of income** — (a) the circular flow of income, with injections and leakages. (b) the methods of measuring national income, output and expenditure.
- **1.2 Aggregate demand** — (a) aggregate demand and its components. (b) the relationship between aggregate demand and price level (diagram). (c) shifts in the aggregate demand curve. (d) evaluate the relationship between changes in income and consumption. (e) evaluate the role of expectations.
- **1.3 Aggregate supply** — (a) aggregate supply. (b) the relationship between aggregate supply and price level in the short run and long run (diagram). (c) shifts in the aggregate supply curve in the short run and long run.
- **1.4 The interaction of aggregate demand and supply** — (a) the assumptions underlying the aggregate demand and aggregate supply models. (b) equilibrium in the macroeconomy. (c) evaluate effects of changes in aggregate demand and aggregate supply on macroeconomic indicators.
- **1.5 The multiplier and the accelerator** — (a) factors which determine the size of the national income multiplier. (b) the national income multiplier and accelerator (diagram). (c) the impact of the national income multiplier and accelerator on aggregate demand and economic cycle. (d) output gaps; aggregate demand and aggregate supply model and a production possibility curve (PPC). (e) explain and calculate average and marginal propensities to consume, save and withdraw. (f) explain and calculate size of the national income multiplier. (g) evaluate causes and consequences of an output gap.

### 2. Economic policy objectives

- **2.1 Economic growth** — (a) economic growth. (b) the policy objective of economic growth. (c) the different stages of the economic cycle. (d) real and nominal Gross Domestic Product (GDP); changes in GDP over time. (e) explain and calculate economic growth rates. (f) explain and calculate GDP per capita. (g) short run and long run economic growth (diagram). (h) evaluate causes and consequences of economic growth in the short run and long run.
- **2.2 Development** — (a) the structure of an economy in terms of primary, secondary and tertiary sectors. (b) the policy objective of sustainable development. (c) evaluate the relationship between economic growth and sustainable development. (d) evaluate the usefulness of macroeconomic measures such as GDP, the Human Development Index (HDI) and other alternative social and cultural indicators.
- **2.3 Employment** — (a) employment and unemployment. (b) the policy objective of full employment. (c) the labour force survey and claimant count measures of unemployment. (d) evaluate causes and consequences of unemployment. (e) evaluate effects of full employment.
- **2.4 Inflation** — (a) inflation, deflation, disinflation and hyperinflation. (b) the policy objective of low and stable inflation. (c) real and nominal values. (d) measuring inflation using the Consumer Prices Index and Retail Prices Index. (e) explain and calculate the rate of inflation using index numbers. (f) evaluate causes and consequences of inflation and deflation.
- **2.5 Balance of payments** — (a) balance of payments. (b) the components of the current account: trade in goods, trade in services, primary and secondary income. (c) the policy objective of a sustainable balance of payments position. (d) imbalances on the balance of payments. (e) explain and calculate balances on the different components on the balance of payments. (f) evaluate causes and consequences of imbalances on the balance of payments.
- **2.6 Trends in macroeconomic indicators** — (a) key trends in UK macroeconomic performance in the last 20 years. (b) evaluate the current performance of the UK economy compared with other developed economies, emerging and developing economies.
- **2.7 Income distribution and welfare** — (a) income and wealth; distribution and inequality. (b) the policy objective of a more even distribution of income. (c) absolute and relative poverty. (d) inequality data; Gini coefficients and relevant quantiles. (e) the distribution of income — Lorenz curve (diagram). (f) evaluate causes and consequences of poverty and inequality.
- **2.8 The Phillips Curve** — (a) natural rate of unemployment; non-accelerating inflation rate of unemployment (NAIRU). (b) Keynesian and neo-classical approaches to aggregate supply (diagram). (c) short-run and long-run Phillips Curve (diagram). (d) evaluate the usefulness of the Phillips Curve for macroeconomic policymakers.

### 3. Implementing policy

- **3.1 Fiscal policy** — (a) government budget. (b) direct, indirect, progressive, proportional and regressive taxation. (c) current and capital government expenditure. (d) budget surplus, deficit and balanced budget. (e) cyclical and structural budget position. (f) national and government debt. (g) discretionary fiscal policy and automatic stabilisers. (h) crowding out. (i) the Laffer curve. (j) explain and calculate average and marginal tax rates. (k) evaluate the effectiveness of using fiscal policy to achieve the government's macroeconomic objectives.
- **3.2 Monetary policy** — (a) changes in interest rates (diagram). (b) changes in money supply. (c) inflation rate targets. (d) quantitative easing. (e) influence of exchange rates. (f) evaluate the effectiveness of using monetary policy to achieve the government's macroeconomic objectives.
- **3.3 Supply side policy** — (a) privatisation, deregulation and subsidies (diagram). (b) competition policy. (c) investment in infrastructure, education, training, research and development. (d) reforms of the tax and benefit system. (e) improved labour market flexibility. (f) immigration control. (g) evaluate the effectiveness of using supply side policy measures to achieve the government's macroeconomic objectives.
- **3.4 Policy conflicts** — (a) evaluate conflicts and trade-offs between policy objectives.

### 4. The global context

- **4.1 International trade** — (a) international trade. (b) patterns of international trade over time. (c) evaluate advantages and disadvantages of international trade to developed, emerging and developing countries.
- **4.2 Exchange rates** — (a) explain and calculate exchange rates. (b) determination of exchange rates in fixed and floating exchange rate systems (diagram). (c) evaluate causes and consequences of exchange rate changes. (d) evaluate advantages and disadvantages of different exchange rate systems.
- **4.3 Globalisation** — (a) globalisation. (b) international competitiveness. (c) absolute and comparative advantage. (d) terms of trade. (e) Marshall-Lerner condition and J-curve. (f) explain and calculate terms of trade. (g) evaluate comparative advantage as an explanation of international trade patterns. (h) evaluate causes and consequences of globalisation on developed, emerging and developing countries. (i) evaluate the impact of the performance of emerging economies on other economies.
- **4.4 Trade policies and negotiations** — (a) protectionism. (b) economic integration through free trade areas, customs unions, monetary union, economic union. (c) the impact of tariffs and quotas on trade creation and trade diversion (diagram). (d) evaluate advantages and disadvantages of protectionism and free trade. (e) evaluate the role of the World Trade Organisation (WTO) in promoting free trade.

### 5. The financial sector

- **5.1 Money and interest rates** — (a) functions and characteristics of money. (b) the creation and supply of money. (c) narrow and broad money in terms of liquidity. (d) the relationship between the money supply and the price level; Fisher equation of exchange. (e) the determination of interest rates (diagram).
- **5.2 The financial sector** — (a) the role of the financial sector. (b) the role of savings and investment in promoting economic development. (c) the Harrod-Domar model. (d) microfinance. (e) evaluate the role of the financial sector in promoting economic development.
- **5.3 Financial regulation** — (a) purpose and methods of financial regulation. (b) the role and functions of a central bank. (c) evaluate the importance of the regulation of financial institutions. (d) evaluate the effectiveness of different policy measures available to a central bank in targeting macroeconomic indicators. (e) evaluate the role of the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the World Bank in regulating the global financial system.

## Component 3 — Themes in economics (H460/03)

Component 3 is fully synoptic. It prescribes no additional content of its own — students draw on the whole of the microeconomic (Component 1) and macroeconomic (Component 2) subject content and apply it to a specific unseen theme released in the exam. Assessment combines 30 multiple choice questions (mainly AO1 / AO2 with some AO3 / AO4) with short-answer and extended-response questions on the unseen theme totalling 50 marks.

## Appendix — Quantitative skills (≥ 20% of A level marks)

Students must acquire competence in the quantitative skills relevant to A level economics. Assessment of these skills includes at least Level 2 mathematical skills as a minimum of 20% of overall A level marks, and may be assessed across all four assessment objectives.

| # | Skill |
|---|---|
| QS1 | Calculate, use and understand ratios and fractions |
| QS2 | Calculate, use and understand percentages and percentage changes |
| QS3 | Understand and use the terms mean, median and relevant quantiles |
| QS4 | Construct and interpret a range of standard graphical forms |
| QS5 | Calculate and interpret index numbers |
| QS6 | Calculate cost, revenue and profit (marginal, average, totals) |
| QS7 | Make calculations to convert from money to real terms |
| QS8 | Make calculations of elasticity and interpret the result |
| QS9 | Interpret, apply and analyse information in written, graphical and numerical forms |
